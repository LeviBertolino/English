1. ✅ **was/were + verb-ing** (e.g., *was reviewing*, *were deploying*)

3. ✅ *"…when my colleague **replied** to it first."* — the interruption uses simple past, not past continuous.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Post-mortem notes — Tuesday standup:**
>
> **Tech Lead:** Walk me through Friday's incident. What exactly happened?
>
> **Levi:** The deployment started at 3pm. While the pods were rolling out, a database migration ran automatically — that part we didn't plan. The migration dropped a column that the old pods were still reading. So while the new pods were coming up, the old pods **started** crashing.
>
> **Tech Lead:** How long did it last?
>
> **Levi:** The full outage **lasted** about 12 minutes. I was watching the dashboard when the error rate spiked — I immediately rolled it back.
>
> **Tech Lead:** Good call. So the migration ran, the old pods crashed, you rolled back. Got it.

Look at what's happening here: **"were rolling out"**, **"were still reading"**, **"were coming up"** — these are all **background actions in progress** (past continuous). But **"started crashing"**, **"lasted"**, **"rolled back"** — these are **completed events** that happened one after another (simple past).

### The rule

The **past continuous** and **simple past** are often used **in the same sentence** to show the relationship between background context and key events.

**Core contrast:**

| Tense | What it describes | Key question |
|---|---|---|
| **Past continuous** (was/were + -ing) | Action **in progress** — the background, the scene, the ongoing state | "What was already happening?" |
| **Simple past** (verb + -ed / irregular) | Action **completed** — the event, the interruption, the result | "What happened / what finished?" |

**Pattern 1 — Interrupted action (when):**

> Subject + **was/were + verb-ing** (background) + **when** + Subject + **simple past** (event)

*"I was reviewing the pull request **when** Slack crashed."*
*(background: reviewing) — (event: Slack crashed)*

**Pattern 2 — Two parallel background actions (while):**

> **While** + Subject + **was/were + verb-ing**, + Subject + **was/were + verb-ing**

*"**While** the backend team **was refactoring** the API, the mobile team **was shipping** a new client version."*

**Pattern 3 — Describing a completed period vs. a specific moment:**

| Sentence | Meaning |
|---|---|
| *Yesterday I **worked** on the architecture doc.* | Completed action — describes the whole period |
| *At 3pm yesterday I **was working** on the architecture doc.* | In progress at that specific moment |
| *She **wrote** the runbook.* | It's done — finished result |
| *She **was writing** the runbook when the alert fired.* | In progress when the alert happened |

> **Key insight:** Simple past answers "what happened?" — it's an event. Past continuous answers "what was going on at that moment?" — it's a state of activity.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The senior engineer was reviewing the PR when the CI pipeline failed — she had to restart the checks from scratch."** — interrupted action, engineering
2. **"While the platform team was migrating the storage layer, the product team shipped a feature that wrote directly to the old schema — the two changes conflicted."** — parallel background actions, tech leadership
3. **"Yesterday I worked on the technical roadmap for six hours. By 5pm I had a draft."** — completed period, simple past describes the whole block of work
4. **"At 2pm I was working on the technical roadmap when my manager pinged me with an urgent escalation."** — in progress at a specific moment, then interrupted
5. **"The AI model was processing the batch job when the GPU instance ran out of memory — the job failed silently."** — system event, incident context
6. **"She wasn't sleeping when the PagerDuty alert fired — she was already debugging another issue on her laptop."** — negative past continuous + simultaneous action
7. **"I was cooking dinner when my phone buzzed. I checked it, saw a deploy failure, and sat back down at my desk."** — everyday life interrupted, then sequence of simple past events
8. **"While he was giving the keynote, someone in the audience tweeted a bug report about the demo app — it went viral in 20 minutes."** — parallel action, relatable tech conference moment

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose the correct tense (simple past or past continuous) for each gap:

*"Last Thursday, our team \_\_\_ (run) a load test on the new API gateway. While the test \_\_\_ (run), the monitoring dashboard \_\_\_ (show) normal metrics. Then, at exactly 11:43am, latency \_\_\_ (spike) to 8 seconds. I \_\_\_ (review) the logs when a junior engineer \_\_\_ (notice) the bottleneck — a single misconfigured timeout. We \_\_\_ (fix) it in under five minutes."*

✅ **Answer:**

*"Last Thursday, our team **ran** a load test on the new API gateway. While the test **was running**, the monitoring dashboard **was showing** normal metrics. Then, at exactly 11:43am, latency **spiked** to 8 seconds. I **was reviewing** the logs when a junior engineer **noticed** the bottleneck — a single misconfigured timeout. We **fixed** it in under five minutes."*

**Why:** *"ran a load test"* = completed action (we did it). *"was running / was showing"* = background state during the test. *"spiked"* = event. *"was reviewing"* = in progress when the notice happened. *"noticed / fixed"* = sequence of completed events.

**Model answer:**

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**Picture a stage play:**

- **Past continuous** = the actors already on stage, performing. The scene is set, things are in motion.
- **Simple past** = someone walks through the door. That's the event — it changes everything.

*"I **was presenting** [actors on stage] **when** a notification **popped up** [someone walks in]."*

Also useful: **"when" usually triggers simple past** (the door opening), while **"while" usually triggers past continuous** (the ongoing scene). When you see *while*, think background. When you see *when*, think event.

## 6. 🚀 Daily Production Challenge (self-directed)

The next time you write a **Slack update, a post-mortem entry, or an incident timeline**, use one sentence that combines past continuous and simple past to show context + event:

> *"[Someone] was [doing X] when [the event] happened."*
> *"While [team] was [doing X], [something else] occurred."*

Real post-mortem register you could use today:
> *"The deploy was rolling out to the second region when the health checks started failing. While the platform team was investigating the root cause, the on-call engineer manually drained traffic from the affected zone."*

This pattern appears naturally in incident reports, retrospectives, and engineering updates — and using it correctly signals fluent professional English.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O ponto central desta unidade é a **diferença entre past continuous (was/were + -ing) e simple past**. O past continuous descreve o que estava **em andamento** — o pano de fundo, a cena em curso: *"I was reviewing the PR"*, *"the team was deploying"*. O simple past descreve o que **aconteceu** — um evento completo ou uma sequência: *"the pipeline failed"*, *"she fixed it"*. Os dois aparecem juntos com frequência: o past continuous monta o cenário, e o simple past registra o evento que aconteceu dentro desse cenário.

Os dois padrões mais comuns: **(1) ação interrompida com when** — *"I was writing the spec **when** the CTO called"* (contínuo = fundo; simple past = interrupção); e **(2) ações paralelas com while** — *"**While** the backend team was refactoring, the mobile team was shipping"* (dois panos de fundo simultâneos). Cuidado: use o past continuous apenas quando a ação estava genuinamente em andamento — não para sequências de eventos completos (para isso, use o simple past encadeado). A distinção parece sutil, mas fica natural com prática: se a frase responde "o que estava acontecendo?", use past continuous; se responde "o que aconteceu?", use simple past.
