# Unit 024 — is being done / has been done (Passive 2)

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson — **Unit 023: is done / was done (Passive 1)** — showed passive voice for ongoing policies and completed past events. The post-incident Slack thread used forms like *"The root cause **was identified**"* (past simple passive) and *"All P1s **are reviewed** by the engineering leads"* (present simple passive — recurring policy). The memory tip was: *the object takes the spotlight.*

Three rapid recall questions:

1. Transform to passive: *"The security team audits all deployments."* → *"All deployments ___ ___ by the security team."*
2. Which form is correct? *"The service **is restored** yesterday"* OR *"The service **was restored** yesterday."*
3. True or false: In English technical writing, passive voice is unnatural and should be avoided.

👉 **Answers:**
1. ✅ *"All deployments **are audited** by the security team."* (present simple passive — ongoing policy)
2. ✅ *"The service **was restored** yesterday."* — *yesterday* anchors it in finished past → *was*, not *is*
3. ✅ **False** — passive is very natural and frequent in documentation, incident reports, and process descriptions

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **A Slack status thread during a production incident:**
>
> **@Levi (to #engineering-oncall):** Quick status check — what's the current state?
>
> **@Maya (DevOps):** The broken node **is being replaced** right now. ETA 10 minutes.
>
> **@Levi:** Has the backup service taken over?
>
> **@Maya:** Yes — traffic **is being rerouted** to us-east-2. The affected customers **have been notified** already.
>
> **@Levi:** Good. **Has** the incident **been logged** in the tracking system?
>
> **@Maya:** It **has been created** as INC-4892. And the runbook **is being updated** in parallel — Priya **is** on it.
>
> **@Levi:** Thanks. Keep me posted. **Have** the on-call engineers **been paged**?
>
> **@Maya:** Yes, all three **have been paged** and two **have** already **responded**.

Notice:
- *"The node **is being replaced**"* → something happening **right now** (present continuous passive)
- *"The customers **have been notified**"* → completed very recently, result matters **now** (present perfect passive)
- *"**Has** the incident **been logged**?"* → present perfect passive question
- *"INC-4892 **has been created**"* → just done, the creation is what's relevant
- The agent (*by someone*) is usually omitted when obvious from context

---

### The rules for Unit 024

#### Rule 1 — Present continuous passive: is/are being + past participle

Use this when something **is happening right now** (or around this moment) and you want to focus on what's being done rather than who's doing it.

| Active | Passive |
|---|---|
| *"The team **is fixing** the bug."* | *"The bug **is being fixed**."* |
| *"They **are reviewing** the proposal."* | *"The proposal **is being reviewed**."* |

Structure: **is/are + being + past participle**

> ⚠️ A common error: *"The bug **is being fixed** now"* ✅ → do NOT say *"The bug **is fixed** now"* if you mean it's happening at this moment. *"The bug **is fixed**"* = a state (it's already done and fixed); *"The bug **is being fixed**"* = an ongoing action.

---

#### Rule 2 — Present perfect passive: has/have been + past participle

Use this when an action **has been completed recently** and the **result matters now** — the same logic as the active present perfect, but in passive form.

| Active | Passive |
|---|---|
| *"Someone **has deployed** the fix."* | *"The fix **has been deployed**."* |
| *"The engineers **have reviewed** the PR."* | *"The PR **has been reviewed**."* |

Structure: **has/have + been + past participle**

---

#### Rule 3 — Questions and negatives

| Form | Present continuous passive | Present perfect passive |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | *"The PR **is being reviewed**."* | *"The PR **has been reviewed**."* |
| **Negative** | *"The PR **isn't being reviewed**."* | *"The PR **hasn't been reviewed**."* |
| **Question** | *"**Is** the PR **being reviewed**?"* | *"**Has** the PR **been reviewed**?"* |

---

#### All four passive forms together

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present simple | is/are + past participle | *"PRs **are reviewed** by two engineers."* (policy) |
| Past simple | was/were + past participle | *"The bug **was deployed** on Friday."* (completed past) |
| **Present continuous** | is/are + being + past participle | *"The service **is being migrated**."* (right now) |
| **Present perfect** | has/have + been + past participle | *"The incident **has been closed**."* (recently done) |

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The new microservice is being deployed to production — expect a brief latency spike in the next five minutes."** *(present continuous passive — happening now)*
2. **"All critical vulnerabilities have been patched following the security audit last week."** *(present perfect passive — recently completed, result matters now)*
3. **"The Staff engineer's architecture proposal is being evaluated by the principal engineers this afternoon."** *(present continuous passive — in progress)*
4. **"Three candidates have been shortlisted for the senior engineering role — we'll share feedback by Thursday."** *(present perfect passive — action done, result relevant now)*
5. **"The legacy payment service is being refactored to use the new event-driven pattern introduced in Q1."** *(present continuous passive — ongoing work)*
6. **"The incident has been escalated to the VP of Engineering — we should expect questions in the all-hands."** *(present perfect passive — completed recently, consequence follows)*
7. **"My coffee is being made — I'll be in the standup in two minutes."** *(present continuous passive — everyday context, light-hearted)*
8. **"The package has been delivered — it's at the front desk."** *(present perfect passive — everyday context, result matters now)*

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose the correct passive form (present continuous passive OR present perfect passive) to complete each sentence:

1. *"The database migration is still running — the tables ___ currently ___ (migrate)."*
2. *"You can merge the PR now — it ___ already ___ (approve) by the tech lead."*
3. *"Don't restart the server yet — it ___ ___ (update) by the DevOps team."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. *"The tables **are** currently **being migrated**."* (present continuous passive — happening right now)
2. *"It **has** already **been approved** by the tech lead."* (present perfect passive — recently done, result matters)
3. *"It **is being updated** by the DevOps team."* (present continuous passive — in progress)

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of the two new passive forms as **"in progress" vs "done and delivered"**:

> **is being done** = 🚧 under construction right now
> **has been done** = ✅ ticket closed, result is live

If you can picture a Jira card **still In Progress** → use *is being done*.
If the card **has moved to Done** and you're talking about the result → use *has been done*.

And the verb chain is always predictable:
- *is/are* + **being** + past participle → ongoing now
- *has/have* + **been** + past participle → recently finished

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Esta lição completa o quadro das quatro formas passivas fundamentais do inglês, adicionando duas novas à aula anterior. O **present continuous passive** (*is/are being + particípio passado*) descreve algo que está acontecendo **agora**, no momento em que se fala: *"The service **is being deployed**"* — o deploy está em andamento. Não confunda com o present simple passive (*"The service **is deployed**"*), que descreve um estado já existente, não uma ação em curso. O **present perfect passive** (*has/have been + particípio passado*) descreve uma ação **recentemente concluída** cujo resultado importa agora: *"The vulnerability **has been patched**"* — foi corrigido, e o que importa é que o sistema está seguro agora. A lógica é exatamente a mesma do present perfect ativo que você já conhece — apenas com o foco no objeto.

Um ponto prático importante: em inglês técnico e profissional, essas formas aparecem o tempo todo em atualizações de status, relatórios de incidente e mensagens de equipe. Frases como *"The fix has been deployed"*, *"The PR is being reviewed"* ou *"Three candidates have been shortlisted"* soam naturais e precisas para um líder de engenharia. Em português brasileiro, tendemos a usar construções ativas (*"A gente já fez o deploy"*, *"Estamos revisando o PR"*) onde o inglês profissional prefere a passiva — e isso é uma diferença cultural e de registro, não apenas gramatical. Incorporar essas formas na sua escrita vai elevar o nível de qualquer comunicação técnica em inglês.
