1. Fix the mistake: *"He mightn't come to the sync."*
2. True or false: *"May it rain later?"* is the natural way to ask about possibility.

👉 **Answers:**

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Levi, investigating a production incident:**
>
> **Teammate:** Any idea why the payment service crashed at 2am?
>
> **Levi:** I'm not sure yet. The on-call engineer **may have restarted** it without checking logs first. Or the retry queue **might have overflowed** — we saw that pattern last month.
>
> **Teammate:** Is anyone looking at it right now?
>
> **Levi:** Yeah, someone **might be debugging** it already, but I haven't heard back. Honestly, we **might as well** roll back to the last stable release while we investigate — no reason to leave prod broken.

Notice:
- **may/might + have + past participle** = a guess about **something in the past** ("maybe it happened, I'm not sure").
- **may/might + be + -ing** = a guess about something happening **right now** (ongoing).
- **might as well** (do something) = "there's no reason not to" — a decision made because no better option exists.

### The rule

| Form | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| may/might **+ have + V3** | possibility in the **past** | The build **may have failed** overnight. |
| may/might **+ be + -ing** | possibility happening **now** | She **might be reviewing** the PR as we speak. |
| **might as well** + base verb | no reason not to do it | We **might as well** deploy now — nothing's blocking us. |
| Negative past | **may/might not have + V3** | He **might not have seen** the Slack message yet. |

> ⚠️ Common trap: don't drop *have*: ❌ *"She may missed the deploy window"* → ✅ *"She may **have** missed the deploy window."*
> ⚠️ **Might have** vs **must have**: *"might have"* = a guess (uncertain); *"must have"* = a strong conclusion from evidence. *"The logs are empty — the service must have crashed"* (near-certain) vs *"It might have crashed"* (just a possibility).
> ⚠️ **Might as well** isn't about probability at all — it's a mild, practical decision, not a guess.

## 3. ✏️ 8 example sentences

1. The pipeline **may have failed** because of a flaky test, not the code itself.
2. Someone **might be deploying** to staging right now — check before you push.
3. We **might as well** merge this PR; the reviewer already approved it informally.
4. The client **may not have received** our email — let's follow up.
5. The Staff engineer **might have flagged** this risk in yesterday's design review.
6. She **might be presenting** the roadmap to leadership as we speak.
7. I **might have left** my badge at home — can you let me in?
8. We **might as well** order pizza; nobody wants to cook tonight.

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

**Recognition/gap-fill:**
Fill the gap: *"The outage ___ ___ ___ (may / cause) by the config change we pushed at midnight."*

✅ **Answer:** *"The outage **may have been caused** by the config change we pushed at midnight."*

**Production task:**
Write 2 sentences guessing about something that happened on a project recently, using *may/might have + past participle*.

**Model answer:**
- *"The memory leak might have started with last week's dependency upgrade."*
- *"The customer may have hit the rate limit — that would explain the errors."*

---

## 5. 💡 Memory tip

**"Have" sends the guess into the past.** May/might alone = guessing about now/future (Unit 30). Bolt on **have + V3**, and you're guessing about something that already happened — like a detective reviewing evidence after the fact.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Hoje avançamos de **may/might** (possibilidade no presente/futuro, Unit 30) para duas extensões: **may/might + have + particípio passado**, usado para **especular sobre algo que já aconteceu** ("pode ser que tenha acontecido"), e **may/might + be + -ing**, para especular sobre algo **acontecendo agora**. Também vimos a expressão **might as well**, que não é sobre probabilidade — é uma decisão prática de "não custa nada fazer isso".

Cuidado para não esquecer o **have** na forma passada (❌ *"she may missed"*) e para não confundir **might have** (só um palpite) com **must have** (conclusão quase certa a partir de evidências). Essas duas soam parecidas em português ("deve ter sido"), mas em inglês carregam graus de certeza bem diferentes.
