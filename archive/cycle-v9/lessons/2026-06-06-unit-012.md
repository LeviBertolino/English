## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack message from Levi to his team, after a hectic week:**
>
> *"Hey everyone — quick end-of-sprint wrap-up. We shipped three features this week. I reviewed 14 pull requests, the backend team fixed two critical bugs, and Ana deployed the new ML pipeline on Friday. The infra engineer went through the entire runbook and updated it. We also got approval from product to start the Q3 planning. Good work — you made the deadline and didn't break production."*

Notice: *shipped, reviewed, fixed, deployed, went, updated, got, made* — all past. These are the **past simple** forms of action verbs: describing things that *happened* and are *finished*. That's today's unit.

### The rule: Past Simple

Use the **past simple** for actions that started and finished in the past, usually with a time anchor (*yesterday, last week, in 2023, two hours ago*).

#### Regular verbs → add **-ed**

| Base form | Past simple |
|---|---|
| work | worked |
| finish | finished |
| deploy | deployed |
| start | started |
| review | reviewed |
| need | needed |

**Spelling rules for -ed:**
- Most verbs: **+ ed** → *work → worked*
- Verb ending in **-e**: **+ d** → *decide → decided*, *use → used*
- Short verb (consonant–vowel–consonant): **double consonant + ed** → *stop → stopped*, *plan → planned*
- Verb ending in **consonant + y**: **change y → ied** → *try → tried*, *carry → carried*

#### Irregular verbs → unique forms (must be memorised)

| Base | Past | Base | Past |
|---|---|---|---|
| go | **went** | make | **made** |
| get | **got** | give | **gave** |
| come | **came** | know | **knew** |
| see | **saw** | think | **thought** |
| have | **had** | do | **did** |
| take | **took** | write | **wrote** |
| send | **sent** | find | **found** |
| run | **ran** | build | **built** |
| bring | **brought** | say | **said** |
| tell | **told** | meet | **met** |

> ⚠️ The past simple in this unit is **positive only** — negatives and questions use *didn't/did you?* which come in Unit 013.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"We goed to the all-hands yesterday."** → ✅ **"We went to the all-hands yesterday."**
*Why: "go" is irregular — its past form is "went", never "goed". Applying -ed to irregular verbs is one of the most common L2 errors.*

❌ **"The deploy taked three hours."** → ✅ **"The deployment took three hours."**
*Why: "take" is irregular → "took". Also note the noun form "deployment" is more natural than "the deploy" in formal writing.*

❌ **"She sended the report to management."** → ✅ **"She sent the report to management."**
*Why: "send" is irregular → "sent" (not "sended"). The pattern send/sent/sent is in the same family as spend/spent/spent and lend/lent/lent — learning them as a group helps.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**Two types of verbs. Two rules.**

> 🟢 **Regular** → just add **-ed** (but watch the spelling traps: stop→stopped, try→tried).
> 🔴 **Irregular** → no rule, must learn. But they cluster in families:
> - **-ight family**: bring→brought, think→thought, buy→bought
> - **-ent family**: send→sent, spend→spent, lend→lent
> - **vowel change**: run→ran, find→found, come→came, write→wrote

When you meet a new verb, ask: *"Is it regular or irregular?"* If regular, -ed. If irregular, add it to your list and use it in a real sentence today.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### -ed endings: three sounds, not one

The -ed ending has **three different pronunciations** depending on the final sound of the base verb:

| Pronunciation | When | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **/t/** | Base verb ends in unvoiced consonant (k, p, s, f, ch, sh) | worked /wɜːkt/, stopped /stɒpt/, finished /ˈfɪnɪʃt/, pushed /pʊʃt/ |
| **/d/** | Base verb ends in voiced consonant or vowel (l, n, r, v, z, m, b) | reviewed /rɪˈvjuːd/, deployed /dɪˈplɔɪd/, solved /sɒlvd/ |
| **/ɪd/** | Base verb ends in **/t/** or **/d/** | started /ˈstɑːtɪd/, decided /dɪˈsaɪdɪd/, needed /ˈniːdɪd/ |

### Key words and IPA

| Word | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| worked | /wɜːkt/ | ends in /kt/ cluster — don't drop the /t/ ⚠️ |
| deployed | /dɪˈplɔɪd/ | ends in /d/, not "ed" ⚠️ |
| finished | /ˈfɪnɪʃt/ | ends in /ʃt/ — the "ed" sounds like /t/ only ⚠️ |
| reviewed | /rɪˈvjuːd/ | ends in /d/ — soft ending |
| started | /ˈstɑːtɪd/ | ends in /ɪd/ — extra syllable because base ends in /t/ |
| decided | /dɪˈsaɪdɪd/ | ends in /ɪd/ — base ends in /d/ → extra syllable |
| went | /wɛnt/ | one syllable; /ɛ/ like in "bed" |
| built | /bɪlt/ | ends in /lt/ cluster ⚠️ |
| thought | /θɔːt/ | /θ/ = "th" sound — no equivalent in Portuguese ⚠️ |
| wrote | /roʊt/ | rhymes with "boat" |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **-ed as a full syllable** ⚠️: Brazilians often say "work-EED", "deploy-EED", adding a full vowel. In English, *worked* = one syllable /wɜːkt/ and *deployed* ends in a soft /d/. Only *started*, *decided*, *needed* have the extra /ɪd/ syllable — and that's because the base verb ends in /t/ or /d/.
- **Final consonant clusters** ⚠️: *worked* ends in /kt/, *built* ends in /lt/, *thought* ends in /θt/. Portuguese avoids these clusters at word ends, so Brazilians tend to drop the final consonant or insert a vowel. Practice: keep the clusters crisp.
- **The /θ/ in "thought"** ⚠️: English "th" (/θ/) doesn't exist in Portuguese. Place the tip of your tongue lightly behind your upper front teeth and blow air. *Thought* = /θɔːt/. Don't replace it with /f/ or /t/.
- **"went" vs. "vent"** ⚠️: The /w/ at the start of *went* is a rounded semi-vowel. Brazilians sometimes replace it with /v/ (like Portuguese *v*). Round your lips first, then open: *went*, not *vent*.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **past simple** (passado simples) é usado para ações que aconteceram e terminaram no passado, geralmente com uma referência de tempo como *yesterday*, *last week*, *last sprint* ou *two hours ago*. Verbos **regulares** formam o passado adicionando **-ed** ao final (*work → worked*, *deploy → deployed*, *finish → finished*), mas atenção às regras de ortografia: verbos curtos dobram a consoante final (*stop → stopped*, *plan → planned*), verbos terminados em consoante + y trocam o y por **ied** (*try → tried*), e verbos terminados em -e apenas recebem **d** (*decide → decided*). Verbos **irregulares** têm formas únicas que precisam ser memorizadas: *go → went*, *get → got*, *build → built*, *write → wrote*, *think → thought*, etc. Nesta unidade, trabalhamos apenas as formas **afirmativas** — as formas negativas e as perguntas (*didn't / did you?*) são o tema da próxima aula.

Na pronúncia, o ponto mais importante é que o sufixo **-ed** tem **três sons diferentes**, não um só: **/t/** (worked, finished), **/d/** (reviewed, deployed) e **/ɪd/** (started, decided). O erro mais comum de falantes de português é pronunciar "ed" como uma sílaba completa em todos os casos — dizendo "work-EED" ou "deploy-EED". Outro cuidado: verbos irregulares como *thought* contêm o som /θ/ (o "th" do inglês), que não existe em português — coloque a ponta da língua levemente atrás dos dentes superiores e sopre o ar. Por fim, fique atento aos clusters consonantais no final das palavras (*worked = /wɜːkt/*, *built = /bɪlt/*) — o português evita essas combinações, então a tendência é cortar a última consoante.
