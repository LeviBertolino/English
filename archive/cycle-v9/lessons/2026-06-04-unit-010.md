# Unit 010 — have got

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson was **Unit 009 — I am doing and I do (2)**: choosing between present continuous (*I'm working*) and present simple (*I work*) — temporary/ongoing actions vs. habits and general facts, plus the stative-verb trap.

Three rapid recall questions:

1. From Unit 009's post-mortem dialogue: *"The team \_\_\_ (always deploys / is always deploying) on Fridays."* — Which is correct, and why?
2. ❌ *"I'm knowing the root cause of the incident."* — What's wrong?
3. *"We're moving to a blameless format"* vs. *"We move to a blameless format"* — what's the meaning difference?

👉 **Answers:**
1. **always deploys** — a fixed habitual schedule → present simple. *"Is always deploying"* implies the team is doing it right this moment and is annoying about it (yes, "always + continuous" = complaint/criticism — a different meaning entirely).
2. ❌ *know* is a stative verb; it describes a mental state, not an action in progress. → ✅ *"I know the root cause."*
3. *"We're moving"* = a change happening right now / this period (temporary). *"We move"* = it's a routine or permanent fact. The first implies a project; the second implies that's always how you do it.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Levi joins a discovery call with a vendor about a new AI platform:**
>
> **Vendor:** *"Do you have a dedicated ML infrastructure team?"*
>
> **Levi:** *"We've got a small platform team that handles infra, but we haven't got a separate MLOps function yet."*
>
> **Vendor:** *"Have you got any models already in production?"*
>
> **Levi:** *"Yes — we've got three. Two NLP classifiers and a recommendation model. The classifiers have got a latency SLA of under 200ms."*
>
> **Vendor:** *"That's great. Has your recommendation model got monitoring in place?"*
>
> **Levi:** *"It has — we've got Grafana dashboards and alert rules set up."*

*Have got* is everywhere in that conversation. Notice the question form (*Have you got…?*), the negative (*we haven't got*), and the short answer (*It has*). Now let's look at the rule.

### The rule: have got

**have got** means the same as **have** — possession, relationships, characteristics, illnesses. In British English it's very common in informal and semi-formal speech. In American English, *have* alone is more typical.

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| **Positive** | I/we/you/they **have got** → **'ve got** |
| | he/she/it **has got** → **'s got** |
| **Negative** | I/we/you/they **haven't got** |
| | he/she/it **hasn't got** |
| **Question** | **Have** I/we/you/they **got** …? |
| | **Has** he/she/it **got** …? |
| **Short answer** | Yes, I **have** / No, I **haven't** |
| | Yes, she **has** / No, she **hasn't** |

### Key patterns

- ✅ *"We've got a solid observability stack."* = *"We have a solid observability stack."*
- ✅ *"Have you got access to the staging environment?"* = *"Do you have access…?"*
- ✅ *"She hasn't got the permissions she needs."* = *"She doesn't have…"*

> ⚠️ **Important:** *have got* is used for **states** (possession, characteristics) — not for habitual actions. You would NOT say *"I've got breakfast every morning"* to mean *"I have breakfast every morning."* For habits and actions, use plain *have*.
>
> - ✅ *"I have breakfast at 7am."* (habit / action)
> - ✅ *"I've got a great coffee maker."* (possession)

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"We've got three microservices handling authentication — each one has got its own database."** — possession of technical components
2. **"Have you got access to the production logs? I need to trace this request."** — question about permissions/access
3. **"The team hasn't got enough context yet — we should share the RFC before the design review."** — negative: lacking information
4. **"She's got ten years of backend experience and a strong background in distributed systems."** — characteristics of a colleague
5. **"Has the staging environment got the latest migration applied?"** — question about system state
6. **"I've got a 1:1 with my manager in 20 minutes — I can't join the debug session."** — possession of a scheduled item
7. **"They've got a good onboarding process, but they haven't got proper documentation for the legacy services."** — mixed positive/negative
8. **"My laptop hasn't got enough RAM to run the full Docker Compose stack locally."** — technical limitation

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I have got breakfast every morning at 7."** → ✅ **"I have breakfast every morning at 7."**
*Why: have got is for possession/characteristics (states), not for repeated actions or habits. For routines, use plain have — no got.*

❌ **"Have you got it? — Yes, I've got."** → ✅ **"Have you got it? — Yes, I have."**
*Why: short answers use have/has alone — never the contraction 've got. The short answer is always "Yes, I have" / "No, I haven't", never "Yes, I've got."*

❌ **"She hasn't got any experience?" (question intonation instead of question form)** → ✅ **"Hasn't she got any experience?"** or **"Has she got any experience?"**
*Why: questions require inversion — the auxiliary (has/have) moves before the subject. Relying on intonation alone is informal and can cause misunderstandings in written English.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**"Got = grabbed."**

Think of *have got* as *"have [already grabbed/acquired]"* — it emphasises possession of something you now hold. *"I've got the keys"* = I grabbed the keys and I have them. *"We've got a deadline"* = we're locked in; it's ours now.

When the meaning is a repeated action (habit), you can't "grab" an action — so *got* disappears: *"I have lunch at 1pm"* (I do this repeatedly, I'm not holding a lunch).

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| have got | /həv ɡɒt/ | *have* is often reduced to /həv/ — not "HAYV" |
| I've got | /aɪv ɡɒt/ | contraction blends smoothly: "Ive-GOT" ⚠️ |
| haven't got | /ˈhævənt ɡɒt/ | stress on HAV — "HAV-unt-got" |
| Has she got | /həz ʃi ɡɒt/ | *has* is often reduced to /həz/ in natural speech ⚠️ |
| hasn't got | /ˈhæzənt ɡɒt/ | stress on HAZZ — "HAZZ-unt-got" |
| access | /ˈækses/ | stress on first syllable: AK-ses ⚠️ |
| experience | /ɪkˈspɪəriəns/ | 4 syllables: ik-SPEER-ee-uns ⚠️ |
| permissions | /pəˈmɪʃənz/ | stress on 2nd syllable: per-MISH-uns |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Reduced *have/has*** ⚠️: In natural connected speech, *have* becomes /həv/ (a very short unstressed syllable) and *has* becomes /həz/. Brazilians often pronounce them fully: "HAVE got", "HAS got." Practice weakening them: *"I've-got"* runs into one smooth phrase — *"Ayv-got."*
- **The /æ/ vowel** ⚠️: In *have*, *haven't*, *hasn't*, *access* — the /æ/ is a flat, open-mouth "ah" with a slight "e" quality. Portuguese doesn't have this exact sound. It's between the "a" in "pai" and the "e" in "pé." Practice: *"have… have… haven't."*
- **"experience"** ⚠️: Brazilians often say "ex-pe-RI-en-ce" (5 syllables, stressing the 3rd) or "ex-PER-i-ence" (4 syllables, stressing the 2nd). The target is *ik-SPEER-ee-uns* — 4 syllables, stress on the 2nd. The first syllable is *ik-* (very short, like "ick").
- **"access"** ⚠️: In Portuguese, "acesso" is /a-ˈse-su/. In English it's /ˈæk-ses/ — stress on the first syllable, and the first vowel is /æ/, not /a/.

### Pronunciation Practice — say each phrase out loud

1. *Have you got access to the production dashboard?* ⚠️ — /həv jʊ ɡɒt ˈækses/
2. *I've got a strong background in distributed systems.* ⚠️ — /aɪv ɡɒt ə strɒŋ ˈbækɡraʊnd/
3. *She hasn't got the experience we need for this role.* ⚠️ — /ʃi ˈhæzənt ɡɒt ðə ɪkˈspɪəriəns/
4. *We've got three services in production — none of them have got proper runbooks.* — /wiːv ɡɒt/
5. *Has the team got enough context for the design review?* ⚠️ — /həz ðə tiːm ɡɒt/

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**Have got** é uma forma muito comum em inglês britânico (e em inglês mais informal em geral) que significa exatamente a mesma coisa que **have** no sentido de posse, características ou relacionamentos. *"We've got a solid team"* = *"We have a solid team"*. As formas são: positivo (*I've got / She's got*), negativo (*I haven't got / She hasn't got*), pergunta (*Have you got…? / Has she got…?*) e resposta curta (*Yes, I have / No, she hasn't* — nunca *"Yes, I've got"*). A diferença com *have* simples: *have got* é usado apenas para **estados de posse** — nunca para **ações habituais**. Não dizemos *"I've got breakfast every morning"* — dizemos *"I have breakfast every morning"* porque café da manhã é uma ação/rotina, não algo que você possui.

Na pronúncia, os pontos críticos para falantes de português são: o *have/has* em fala natural fica reduzido (*həv*, *həz*), então *"have got"* soa como *"həv got"* e não como *"HAVE got"*. O som /æ/ em *have*, *haven't*, *access* — um "a" aberto e achatado que não existe em português — merece atenção especial. E *experience* tem 4 sílabas com acento na 2ª: *ik-SPEER-ee-uns*, não 5 sílabas como em *"experiência"*.
