## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Engineering blog post draft — Levi reviews it with a teammate:**
>
> **Levi:** *"I'm working on the section about incident response right now. I think it's coming together."*
>
> **Teammate:** *"Looks good. Does your team normally **do** post-mortems the day after?"*
>
> **Levi:** *"We always **do** them within 48 hours. But this week we're changing the process — we're **moving** to a blameless format."*
>
> **Teammate:** *"Nice. Who **runs** the post-mortem, usually?"*
>
> **Levi:** *"Typically the on-call engineer. But today the EM **is running** it because it was a high-severity incident."*

Two tenses are dancing here. *We always do* and *who runs* describe regular habits — that's **present simple**. *I'm working*, *we're moving*, *is running* describe what's happening **right now or around now** — that's **present continuous**. Today's lesson is about knowing which one to reach for.

### The rule: present continuous vs. present simple

| | Present Continuous | Present Simple |
|---|---|---|
| **Form** | am/is/are + -ing | base form (+ -s for he/she/it) |
| **Use 1** | Something happening **right now** | A habit, routine, or general fact |
| **Use 2** | Something happening **around this period** (not necessarily at this exact second) | Something that is **always / usually / never** true |
| **Time signals** | now, at the moment, today, this week, currently | always, usually, every day, sometimes, never |

### The tricky overlap: "around now" vs. "in general"

This is the key challenge for Unit 9:

- *"I'm reading a book about systems design."* → You're not reading at this second, but you're in the middle of reading it this week. **→ Present continuous.**
- *"I read a lot of technical books."* → A general habit. **→ Present simple.**

Same verb, same person — different meaning, different tense.

- *"She's leading the platform migration."* → She's in the middle of that project right now / this quarter.
- *"She leads the platform team."* → That's her permanent role.

### Verbs that do NOT use the continuous (stative verbs)

Some verbs describe **states**, not actions in progress. They almost never appear in the continuous:

| Category | Verbs | ✅ Correct | ❌ Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mental states | know, understand, believe, think (= opinion), want | *I know the architecture.* | ~~I'm knowing…~~ |
| Emotions | like, love, hate, prefer | *She loves async work.* | ~~She's loving…~~ |
| Possession | have (= own), own, belong | *The team has a runbook.* | ~~The team is having…~~ |
| Senses | see, hear, smell, taste | *I see the problem.* | ~~I'm seeing the problem.~~ |

> ⚠️ *"I'm thinking"* is valid when **thinking = actively considering right now** (an action): *"I'm thinking about the rollback strategy."* But *"I think it's a good idea"* (= opinion) uses present simple.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The team is refactoring the auth service this sprint — don't assign new tickets to them."** — continuous: temporary project in progress
2. **"Our deployment pipeline usually runs in under 10 minutes, but it's taking 25 minutes today."** — simple for habit, continuous for right now
3. **"I'm currently reading *An Elegant Puzzle* — it has a lot of useful frameworks for engineering managers."** — continuous: in the middle of it this week
4. **"The Staff engineer owns the technical roadmap for Q3."** — simple: stative verb, permanent responsibility
5. **"We're moving our observability stack from Datadog to Grafana this quarter."** — continuous: a change happening around now
6. **"The CI pipeline deploys to staging automatically after every merge."** — simple: standing behaviour, always true
7. **"She's not answering Slack right now — she's presenting at the all-hands."** — continuous: happening at this exact moment
8. **"I usually start my week by reviewing open PRs and checking the incident queue."** — simple: fixed personal habit

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I am working at Google."** → ✅ **"I work at Google."**
*Why: working somewhere is a permanent state/fact, not a temporary action in progress. Use present simple for your job/role. Exception: "I'm working at Google this semester" (temporary contract) — then continuous is correct.*

❌ **"We are knowing the answer."** → ✅ **"We know the answer."**
*Why: know is a stative verb (mental state) — it describes a state you're in, not an action you're doing. Stative verbs don't take the continuous.*

❌ **"She is leading five engineers and deploys every Friday."** → ✅ **"She leads five engineers and deploys every Friday."**
*Why: both are permanent facts/habits about her role. Mixing tenses here creates a false contrast — it implies her leadership is temporary but her deployments are permanent. Parallel structure matters.*

## 5. 🛠️ Practice

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose the correct form (present simple or present continuous):

> *"Right now, our platform team \_\_\_ (migrate / is migrating) the database to PostgreSQL. It's a big project. Normally, the team \_\_\_ (deploys / is deploying) on Fridays, but this week they \_\_\_ (wait / are waiting) until the migration \_\_\_ (finishes / is finishing)."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **is migrating** — happening around now, this quarter (temporary project)
2. **deploys** — normal, habitual schedule → present simple
3. **are waiting** — temporary change to normal behaviour, happening right now
4. **finishes** — after a time conjunction (*until*), English uses present simple even when referring to the future

### Production — write + speak

Write 2 sentences about your **current work situation**: one using present continuous (something happening this week / this quarter / right now), and one using present simple (a permanent habit or standing fact).

Then say them out loud, blending the words naturally (see section 7 ⚠️ sounds).

**Model answer:**
- *"I'm reviewing the engineering leveling framework this week — it needs a complete update before Q3 planning."* (continuous: temporary focus this week)
- *"I review pull requests every morning before joining standups."* (simple: fixed daily habit)

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The camera test.**

Ask yourself: *"Could I film this right now and show it as evidence?"*

- *"She is presenting the roadmap."* ✅ — point a camera at her desk right now and you'd see it happening.
- *"She presents the roadmap every quarter."* ❌ — a camera right now shows nothing useful; this is a pattern, not a live action.

If you can film it → **continuous**.
If it's a routine, fact, or opinion → **simple**.

Bonus for stative verbs: you can't film knowing, liking, or owning. Those are states, not actions. → Always **simple**.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I'm working | /aɪm ˈwɜːrkɪŋ/ | linking: the *m* connects to *w*: "aim-wurking" |
| she's leading | /ʃiːz ˈliːdɪŋ/ | contraction: "sheez" — not "she is" in natural speech ⚠️ |
| deploys | /dɪˈplɔɪz/ | stress on 2nd syllable: di-PLOYZ ⚠️ |
| refactoring | /riːˈfæktərɪŋ/ | stress on 2nd syllable: ree-FAK-tor-ing |
| migrating | /maɪˈɡreɪtɪŋ/ | stress on 2nd syllable: my-GRAY-ting ⚠️ |
| currently | /ˈkɜːrəntli/ | 3 syllables: KUR-rent-lee — the /ɜː/ is not in Portuguese ⚠️ |
| usually | /ˈjuːʒuəli/ | 4 syllables: YOO-zhoo-uh-lee ⚠️ |
| understand | /ˌʌndəˈstænd/ | stress on 3rd syllable: un-der-STAND ⚠️ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Contracted auxiliaries** ⚠️: In natural speech, *I am* → *I'm*, *she is* → *she's*, *we are* → *we're*. Brazilians often avoid contractions and say *"I am working"* in full — it's not wrong but sounds formal/stilted. Practice running the contraction into the next word: *"I'm-working"*, *"she's-leading"*.
- **-ing ending** ⚠️: The /ŋ/ at the end of *working*, *migrating*, *presenting* — no nasal vowel as in Portuguese. The mouth closes: tongue touches the back of the palate. The *g* is silent; the sound is just /ŋ/. Not "workin-gi."
- **"currently"** ⚠️: The /ɜː/ vowel ("ur" sound) doesn't exist in Portuguese. It's the same sound as in *work*, *word*, *learn*. Lips are slightly rounded, tongue is mid-central. Practice: *"cur… cur… currently."*
- **"usually"** ⚠️: Brazilians often say "u-ZU-al-ly" (4 even syllables) or skip a syllable entirely. The target is *YOO-zhoo-uh-lee* — the *-ual-* blends into a very fast /ʒuəl/.

## 8. 🚀 Daily Production Challenge (self-directed)

Use **both tenses in contrast** at least once today in real English:

> *"I'm [doing X] this week, but I usually [do Y]."*
> or
> *"We normally [do X], but right now we're [doing Y]."*

Suggested contexts:
- **Slack status or message**: *"I'm heads-down on the incident review today — I usually join the design sync but I'm skipping this one."*
- **LinkedIn post**: *"Most days I review architecture proposals. This week I'm the one writing them — interesting shift in perspective."*
- **1:1 with your manager**: *"I usually prioritize PR reviews first thing, but I'm focusing on the migration document this sprint."*

The contrast structure is one of the most natural ways to give context quickly — it's a leadership communication pattern, not just a grammar exercise.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A grande diferença entre **present continuous** (*I am doing*) e **present simple** (*I do*) não está apenas no que está acontecendo *agora* versus *sempre* — ela também cobre o que está acontecendo *neste período* (essa semana, esse trimestre, esse sprint) versus o que é permanente ou habitual. Exemplos: *"We're migrating to PostgreSQL this quarter"* (projeto temporário em andamento) vs. *"We deploy every Friday"* (rotina fixa). A sobreposição é a armadilha: o mesmo verbo, a mesma pessoa — o tempo verbal muda o significado completamente. Quando houver dúvida, pergunte: isso é uma ação que posso "filmar agora" (ou "neste período"), ou é um hábito/fato permanente?

Um grupo importante de verbos — chamados **verbos estativos** — quase nunca aparecem no contínuo: *know, understand, believe, want, like, love, hate, have* (= possuir), *see, hear*. Eles descrevem estados mentais, emoções ou posse — não ações em progresso — então dizemos *"I know the system"* (nunca *"I'm knowing"*), *"she loves async work"* (nunca *"she's loving"*). Exceção importante: *think* pode aparecer no contínuo quando significa "estou ativamente considerando algo agora" (*"I'm thinking about the rollback"*), mas no sentido de opinião usa o simples (*"I think it's a good approach"*). Na pronúncia, foque nas contrações na fala natural (*I'm*, *she's*, *we're*) e no som /ɜː/ de *currently* e *working* — o mesmo som "ur" que não existe em português.
