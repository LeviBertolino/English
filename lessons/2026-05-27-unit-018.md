# Unit 018 — will (2): Predictions

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday was **Unit 017 — will (1): Spontaneous Decisions, Offers, and Promises**.

Three quick recall questions:

1. In yesterday's mini-dialogue, Levi said *"I'll take a look right now"* when Pedro asked for help with a failing test. Why *will* and not *going to* here?
2. From yesterday's mistake section: *"I'll to review your PR"* — what's the error? What's the correct form?
3. Quick distinction: Levi says *"I'll handle that tonight"* (spontaneous, just decided) vs. *"I'm going to run a full post-mortem next Friday"* (already calendared). What rule decides which form to use?

👉 **Answers:**
1. Because Levi decided **at that exact moment**, triggered by Pedro's message — not before. *Will* for snap decisions born right now; *going to* for plans that already existed.
2. *Will* is a modal verb — the main verb must be in the **base form with no "to."** Correct: *"I'll review your PR."*
3. The check: *"Did I decide this before I started talking, or just now?"* — **just now → will** / **already decided → going to**.

## 1. 📘 Unit 018 — will (2): Predictions

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Engineering All-Hands — Q3 planning session:**
>
> **CTO:** What's your take on the new AI infrastructure costs for next year?
>
> **Levi:** Honestly? I think **GPU prices will drop** significantly by Q4. The new generation of cloud TPUs **will make** on-demand training much cheaper.
>
> **CTO:** What about team capacity? We're hiring two Staff engineers.
>
> **Levi:** That **will help** a lot. I'm pretty sure the new hires **won't be** productive immediately — onboarding takes a quarter — but by December they **'ll be** fully ramped. I don't think we **'ll have** any capacity issues in H2.
>
> **CTO:** And the legacy monolith migration?
>
> **Levi:** It **won't be** done by June. I expect it **'ll take** at least three more quarters. But once it's done, deployments **will be** ten times faster.

Notice: none of these are plans or snap decisions — they are **predictions**. Levi is saying what he *believes* or *expects* will happen. That's the new job of *will* in this unit.

### The rule

**will (2)** is used to make **predictions** — saying what you think, believe, or expect will happen in the future. You're not deciding anything right now (Unit 17) and you have no visible current evidence (that's *going to*). You're sharing your **opinion or expectation** about the future.

---

**Use 1 — Predictions with opinion verbs**

Very often combined with: *I think, I'm sure, I expect, I believe, I hope, I doubt, I don't think, probably*

> *"I think the migration will take longer than we planned."*
> *"I'm sure the team will find a better solution."*
> *"I don't think this approach will scale beyond 10k requests."*
> *"The refactor will probably take two sprints."*

⚠️ **Key pattern for negatives**: Say *"I don't think it will..."* — not *"I think it won't..."* Both are grammatically fine, but *"I don't think + will"* is far more natural in English.

---

**Use 2 — Standalone predictions (no opinion verb needed)**

When the prediction is fairly confident, you can skip the "I think":

> *"The new model will outperform the baseline."*
> *"Async standups will save the team two hours a week."*
> *"The latency will improve once we add caching."*

---

**Use 3 — Predictions with future time expressions**

*will* pairs naturally with: *tomorrow, soon, in two weeks, next year, in the future, eventually, by [date]*

> *"The team will be fully onboarded by the end of Q3."*
> *"AI-assisted code review will eventually replace manual triage."*
> *"The bug will probably be fixed by tomorrow morning."*

---

**Compare: will vs. going to for predictions**

| | going to | will |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence? | Yes — you can see/hear it now | No — it's your opinion/belief |
| Example | *"Look at the error rate — this deploy is going to fail."* (data is there) | *"I think we'll have fewer incidents next quarter."* (expectation, no live data) |

If you're looking at something right now that proves it, use *going to*. If it's what you believe or expect — not based on present evidence — use *will*.

---

**Forms** (same as Unit 17)

| | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| All subjects | will / 'll | won't | Will...? |

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"I think this architectural change will reduce our P99 latency by at least 30%."** — engineering prediction with opinion verb.
2. **"The new embedding model will probably outperform the current baseline on our retrieval benchmarks."** — confident technical prediction.
3. **"I don't think the monolith migration will be finished before the end of the year — it's bigger than we estimated."** — negative prediction (natural word order: "I don't think + will").
4. **"I'm sure the team will adapt quickly once they see the new CI pipeline in action."** — prediction about people.
5. **"Eventually, every company will need a Staff+ engineering track to retain senior ICs."** — broader industry prediction.
6. **"I expect the on-call load will drop significantly after we add better alerting thresholds."** — prediction with *expect*.
7. **"I'll probably finish the RFC tonight — it won't take more than two hours."** — personal time prediction (spontaneous delivery, but *won't take* is a belief/estimate).
8. **"The kids will love the new coding club at school — it's hands-on from day one."** — everyday life prediction.

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I think it won't work"** → ✅ **"I don't think it will work"**
*Why: In English, it's far more natural to negate the "think" verb, not the prediction. "I think it won't work" sounds blunt or dramatic; "I don't think it will work" is the everyday form.*

❌ **"Look at the velocity chart — I think the team will miss the deadline"** (when the chart is right in front of you showing it) → ✅ **"Look at the velocity chart — the team is going to miss the deadline"**
*Why: if you have **present visible evidence**, go to *going to*, not *will*. "Will" is for belief/opinion without current proof.*

❌ **"The system will to crash under this load"** → ✅ **"The system will crash under this load"**
*Why: same as Unit 17 — modal verbs (will, won't, can, might) are always followed by the base verb with no "to."*

## 5. 🛠️ Practice

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose *will* or *going to* for each blank. Think about whether there's present evidence or just an opinion/expectation:

> *"Look at those memory leak logs — the service \_\_\_ (will / is going to) crash within the hour. I \_\_\_ (will / am going to) add more observability next sprint — I've had that on my backlog for two weeks. And honestly, I don't think the new caching layer \_\_\_ (will / is going to) fix the root cause — it's a deeper architectural issue."*

✅ **Answer:**
- *is going to* crash — you can see the memory logs **right now**; that's present evidence.
- *am going to* add — it's been on the backlog; it's a pre-existing plan.
- *will* fix — no current evidence; it's Levi's **belief/opinion** about the future.

Then say both sentences out loud, paying attention to the ⚠️ sounds in section 7.

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**Will (1) = reflex. Will (2) = crystal ball.**

- **Reflex** (Unit 17): someone throws a problem at you and you catch it: *"I'll handle it."* Instant, no thinking.
- **Crystal ball** (Unit 18): you look into the future and say what you see there: *"I think costs will drop."* No live evidence — just your read on things.

When you reach for *will*, ask: *"Am I reacting right now, or am I predicting?"*
- Reacting → Unit 17 *will*
- Predicting (no live data) → Unit 18 *will*
- Predicting (live data in front of you) → *going to*

> Bonus: notice that the three "wills" (Unit 17 + 18) all share the same form — the difference is entirely in meaning and context.

| I think it'll | /aɪ θɪŋk ɪtəl/ | Casual: "it'll" contracts to /ɪtəl/ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"probably"** ⚠️: Brazilian learners often say *"pro-BA-bly"* (stress on 2nd syllable) or *"proba-BIL-i-ty"* for the noun. Get the noun right: *prob-a-BIL-i-ty* (stress on 3rd). The adverb *"probably"* is *PROB-uh-blee* — stress on 1st syllable.
- **"eventually"** ⚠️: This is 5 syllables. Don't collapse to 3. Practice slowly: *e — VEN — choo — uh — lee*. Also: *"eventually"* in English means *"in the end, someday"* — NOT *"possibly."* ("Eventualmente" in Portuguese can mean "sometimes/occasionally" — false friend!)
- **"I think" → /θɪŋk/** ⚠️: The *th* in "think" is the voiceless dental fricative /θ/. Portuguese has no equivalent. Put your tongue tip lightly between your upper and lower teeth and blow air. Common mistake: saying */tɪŋk/* (sounds like "tink") or */fɪŋk/* (British slang, not standard).
- **"it'll"**: In natural speech, *"I think it'll work"* is more natural than *"I think it will work."* Practice the contraction: /ɪtəl/ — quick and light.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O *will (2)* desta unidade é usado para **previsões** — dizer o que você acredita, espera ou acha que vai acontecer no futuro, sem ter evidências visíveis no momento. É diferente do *going to* para previsões (que exige uma evidência concreta na sua frente, como ver os logs de erro e concluir que o sistema vai cair) e diferente do *will (1)* (que é uma decisão tomada no instante). O *will (2)* é a sua **opinião sobre o futuro**: *"I think costs will drop"*, *"The refactor will probably take two sprints"*, *"Eventually, every team will need a Staff engineer."* Use frequentemente com verbos de opinião: *I think, I expect, I'm sure, I believe, I hope, probably*.

O ponto mais importante para evitar erro: para previsões negativas, o padrão natural em inglês é **"I don't think + will"**, não *"I think + won't"*. Dizer *"I think it won't work"* não está errado, mas soa dramático e direto demais — o jeito idiomático é *"I don't think it will work."* Cuidado também com o falso cognato *eventually* (= "no final das contas / eventualmente no sentido de um dia") — em inglês não significa "às vezes" como o *eventualmente* do português pode sugerir. E treine o som /θ/ de *"think"* — não existe no português e é o som mais característico desta unidade.
