## 1. 📘 Unit 005 — I do/work/like (present simple)

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Recruiter:** So, what do you do at Nubank?
> **Levi:** I lead a platform engineering team. We own the internal developer platform.
> **Recruiter:** Does your team work with AI?
> **Levi:** Yes, we use AI to automate routine ops tasks. My engineers write most tooling in Go.
> **Recruiter:** And what does your role involve day-to-day?
> **Levi:** I run weekly 1:1s, review architecture proposals, and set technical direction. I don't write much production code anymore — I unblock people.

Notice: *I lead*, *we own*, *we use*, *my engineers write*, *I run*, *I review*, *I don't write*, *Does your team work?* — these all describe habits, routines, and general truths. No -ing. This is **present simple**.

### The rule

Use **present simple** for things that are **generally or always true** — routines, habits, facts, and regular states.

**Form:**

| Subject | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | base verb | I *lead* a team. We *use* AI. |
| he / she / it | base verb + **-s** | She *leads* a team. It *uses* AI. |

**Negatives:** use **do/does + not + base verb**

| Subject | Negative |
|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | *I don't write production code.* |
| he / she / it | *She doesn't write production code.* |

**Spelling rules for he/she/it -s:**
- Most verbs: add -s → *writes, leads, reviews*
- Verbs ending in -ch, -sh, -x, -o, -ss: add -es → *teaches, watches, goes, fixes*
- Verbs ending in consonant + y: change y → i, add -es → *carries, studies* (but: *plays* — vowel + y, just add -s)

> ⚠️ The -s only goes on the **verb**, never the subject: *"She leads"*, not *"Shes lead"*.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. Our team **owns** the CI/CD pipeline and all shared infrastructure tooling.
2. I **run** a weekly architecture review to align the staff engineers on technical direction.
3. She **doesn't review** code directly anymore — she sets standards and mentors senior ICs.
4. The model **processes** each inference request in under 200 milliseconds on average.
5. We **deploy** to production multiple times a day using automated rollouts.
6. He **works** remotely from Porto Alegre and **attends** standups via video every morning.
7. I **don't drink** coffee in the afternoon — it ruins my sleep and my focus the next morning.
8. My daughter **studies** at the same school I went to when I was a kid.

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"She lead the platform team."** → ✅ **"She leads the platform team."**
*Why: He/she/it always needs -s on the verb in present simple. This is the most common slip.*

❌ **"He don't understand the architecture."** → ✅ **"He doesn't understand the architecture."**
*Why: Third-person singular uses "doesn't", not "don't". The verb after doesn't stays base form.*

❌ **"I am work here every day."** → ✅ **"I work here every day."**
*Why: Present simple for routines does not use am/is/are + -ing. That structure (present continuous) is for things happening right now, not general habits.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of present simple as the **"always or usually" tense**. Ask yourself: *is this true today, tomorrow, and next week?* If yes → present simple. If it's only happening right now → present continuous.

And for the -s: imagine the third-person pronoun (he/she/it) as a **magnet** that pulls an extra -s onto the verb. Only they have this power.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and phrases

| Word/Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| leads | /liːdz/ | Ends in /dz/ — two sounds, not silent ⚠️ |
| owns | /oʊnz/ | Final /z/ — voiced ⚠️ |
| writes | /raɪts/ | Ends in /ts/ — unvoiced |
| uses | /ˈjuːzɪz/ | Three syllables: YOO-ziz ⚠️ |
| watches | /ˈwɒtʃɪz/ | Three syllables: WAH-chiz |
| doesn't | /ˈdʌzənt/ | DUZ-nt — not "DOH-znt" ⚠️ |
| don't | /doʊnt/ | Final /t/ — don't drop it |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Final -s on verbs**: in Portuguese, final consonants are often softened or dropped. In English, *leads*, *owns*, *writes* all have clear final consonants. Don't drop them.
- **"uses"**: Brazilians often say /ˈjuːsɪz/ (s sound) but the correct pronunciation has a /z/: /ˈjuːzɪz/. Two Z sounds: *YOO-ziz*.
- **"doesn't"**: the vowel in "does" is /ʌ/ (like "bus"), not /oʊ/ (like "go"). Say *DUZ-nt*, not *DOHZ-nt*.
- **Verb -s endings**: after a voiceless sound (/p, t, k, f/), -s sounds like /s/ → *writes /ts/*. After a voiced sound (/b, d, g, v, m, n, l, r/), -s sounds like /z/ → *leads /dz/*, *owns /nz/*. After /s, z, ʃ, tʃ/, add /ɪz/ → *watches /ɪz/*, *uses /ɪz/*.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **presente simples** é usado para hábitos, rotinas e fatos gerais — coisas que são verdade hoje, amanhã e na semana que vem. A forma é simples: usa o verbo base (*I work, we deploy, they own*), mas com **he/she/it você adiciona -s** (*she leads, he writes, it runs*). Esse -s é o erro mais comum de brasileiros — em português o verbo não muda assim, então a tendência é esquecer.

Para **negativas**, use *don't* (I/you/we/they) ou *doesn't* (he/she/it), sempre seguido do verbo na forma base: *"She doesn't write code"*, não *"She doesn't writes"*. Outro ponto de atenção: não confunda com o **presente contínuo** (Unit 003). Se a ação é uma rotina ou fato geral → presente simples. Se está acontecendo neste exato momento → presente contínuo com -ing.
