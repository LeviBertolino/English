## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **LinkedIn DM — Levi and a former colleague:**
>
> **Carlos:** Hey Levi! Are you still at the same company?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, still here. I'm leading the platform engineering team this year — it's a temporary rotation while the previous lead is on sabbatical. We're also migrating our entire infrastructure to Kubernetes this quarter, so things are getting pretty intense.
>
> **Carlos:** Nice! Are you still living in São Paulo?
>
> **Levi:** I work there normally, but I'm spending most of this month in Berlin — we're building a new team here. AI tooling is changing so fast; the whole industry is moving towards AI-assisted engineering. It's exciting but demanding.
>
> **Carlos:** Same here. Our team is growing fast — we're hiring three Staff engineers right now.

Read that again. Notice: *"I'm leading the platform team this year"* — Levi is not doing it at the exact second he's typing. He means: *for this year, as a temporary thing*. *"We're migrating to Kubernetes this quarter"* — an ongoing project, not happening at this exact moment. *"AI tooling is changing so fast"* — a trend, something developing over time.

That's the new layer today's lesson adds.

### The rule

You already know present continuous for **right now** (Unit 3):
> *"I'm writing the RFC."* → happening at this exact moment.

Today: **present continuous also covers temporary situations and changes happening around now** — this week, this month, this quarter, these days.

#### Use 1: Temporary situations ("around now" — not necessarily this exact second)

| Present simple (permanent / usual) | Present continuous (temporary / around now) |
|---|---|
| *"I work in São Paulo."* | *"I'm working from Berlin this month."* |
| *"She manages a team of five."* | *"She's managing the on-call rota this week — our lead is sick."* |
| *"He uses Python for everything."* | *"He's learning Go this quarter — the team is migrating."* |
| *"We deploy to AWS."* | *"We're testing GCP this sprint — just evaluating."* |

> 🔑 **The test**: is it temporary? Will it stop? Is it different from the normal/usual situation? → Present continuous.

#### Use 2: Changes and trends (things that are developing or evolving)

> *"AI is transforming software engineering."*
> *"The team is getting better at writing tests."*
> *"Our response times are improving since the caching refactor."*
> *"Hiring is getting harder in this market."*

These aren't happening *right now at this second* — they're gradual, ongoing changes. Present continuous signals: **this is in motion**.

Useful verbs for trends: *get, grow, change, improve, increase, decline, move, shift, rise, fall, become, evolve.*

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Read the message below. Six blanks: choose **present simple** or **present continuous**, then write the correct form.

> *"Hi all — quick update. Normally, I \_\_\_ (manage) the backend team, but this quarter I \_\_\_ (lead) both backend and platform because we \_\_\_ (reorganise) the engineering structure. The team \_\_\_ (grow) — we \_\_\_ (hire) two more engineers right now — and our deployment frequency \_\_\_ (improve) every sprint."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **manage** — permanent/usual role → present simple.
2. **'m leading** — temporary situation this quarter → present continuous.
3. **are reorganising** — ongoing project happening around now → present continuous.
4. **is growing** — a change in progress → present continuous.
5. **are hiring** — happening right now, temporary → present continuous.
6. **is improving** — trend/change in progress → present continuous.

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**Two cameras, one film:**

> Unit 3's present continuous = **close-up shot** → action frozen at this exact second.
> Unit 4's present continuous = **wide shot, slow pan** → a temporary scene that may last days or months.

Both use *am/is/are + -ing*. The difference is the **time horizon** — seconds vs. weeks — not the grammar form. Ask yourself: *"Is this temporary / in motion / different from the usual?"* If yes, reach for the continuous.

> *"I'm leading this team this year."* — slow pan: a temporary reality.
> *"AI is changing everything."* — slow pan: a trend in motion.
> *"I'm writing this message."* — close-up: exactly right now.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A unidade de hoje expande o uso do **presente contínuo** além do "agora exato" da Unidade 003. Além de ações que estão acontecendo *neste exato momento*, o presente contínuo em inglês também é usado para **situações temporárias** (que estão acontecendo *por agora*, esta semana, este mês, este trimestre — algo diferente do normal) e para **mudanças e tendências em progresso** (coisas que estão evoluindo, crescendo, melhorando). Exemplos: *"I'm leading the platform team this year"* (temporário — não é meu papel permanente), *"We're migrating to Kubernetes this quarter"* (projeto em andamento), *"AI tooling is changing everything"* (tendência em movimento). A estrutura é sempre a mesma: **am/is/are + verbo-ing**.

O contraste fundamental é com o **presente simples**, que descreve situações permanentes ou habituais: *"I work in São Paulo"* (normal/permanente) vs. *"I'm working from Berlin this month"* (temporário). Sempre que a situação for **temporária** ou **diferente do usual**, use o contínuo. Verbos úteis para tendências: *get, grow, change, improve, increase, become, shift, evolve*. Memorize o teste: *"É temporário? Vai mudar? É diferente do normal?"* — se sim, use *am/is/are + -ing*.
