## 1. 📋 Recap: What You Covered This Week

| Unit | Topic | Core rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit 7** | Present simple questions | *Do/Does* + subject + base verb. Short answers use only the auxiliary. |
| **Unit 9** | Present continuous vs. simple (2) | Continuous = in progress now/this period. Simple = habit or fact. Stative verbs (*know, have, like*) never take -ing. |
| **Unit 10** | have got | *I've got / She hasn't got* = possession or characteristics. Not for habits/actions. Short answer: *Yes, I have* — never *"Yes, I've got."* |
| **Unit 11** | was/were | Past of *to be*. *Was* = I/he/she/it. *Were* = we/you/they. Collective nouns (*team, company*) → *was* in formal English. |
| **Unit 12** | Past simple — positive | Regular: add -ed (watch spelling). Irregular: go→went, build→built, write→wrote, send→sent. Three -ed sounds: /t/, /d/, /ɪd/. |
| **Unit 13** | Past simple — negative & questions | *Didn't + base verb*. *Did + subject + base verb?* Short answers: *Yes, I did / No, she didn't*. Base form rule — no exceptions. |
| **Unit 14** | Past continuous vs. past simple | *Was/were + -ing* = background action. Past simple = the event. Classic patterns: *when* and *while*. |

## 2. 🧩 8 Mixed Exercises

### Exercise 1 — Unit 7: Present simple questions

Transform the statement into a question:

> *"Your team uses automated testing in the CI pipeline."*

_____ your team _____ automated testing in the CI pipeline?

✅ **Answer:** **Does** your team **use** automated testing in the CI pipeline?
*(does → third person singular; use → base form, no -s. The -s "moved" to the auxiliary.)*

---

### Exercise 2 — Unit 9: Present continuous vs. present simple

Choose the correct form for each blank:

> *"Right now, our platform team _____ (refactors / is refactoring) the authentication service. Normally, we _____ (release / are releasing) on Fridays."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **is refactoring** — temporary project in progress around now, not a fixed habit
2. **release** — fixed habitual schedule → present simple with time signal *normally*

---

### Exercise 3 — Unit 10: have got

Fill in the blanks with the correct form of *have got*:

> *"_____ the new engineer _____ access to the deployment tool yet? She _____ a strong background in Kubernetes, but she _____ experience with our internal CI setup."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **Has … got** — question, third person singular
2. **'s got / has got** — positive, third person singular
3. **hasn't got** — negative, third person singular

---

### Exercise 4 — Unit 11: was/were

Choose *was* or *were* for each blank:

> *"The incident _____ caused by a misconfigured timeout. The engineers _____ exhausted after the three-hour war room. _____ the on-call rotation clear to everyone? The team _____ not informed about the change."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **was** — singular "the incident"
2. **were** — plural "the engineers"
3. **Was** — singular "the on-call rotation" → question form with inversion
4. **was** — "the team" is a collective noun → *was* in formal English

---

### Exercise 5 — Unit 12: Past simple positive

Put the verbs in the correct past simple form:

> *"Last quarter, the AI team _____ (run) a series of A/B tests, _____ (write) up the results, _____ (share) them with the product team, and _____ (decide) to roll out the new model to 10% of users."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **ran** — irregular (run → ran)
2. **wrote** — irregular (write → wrote)
3. **shared** — regular (share + d); -ed sounds like /d/ because *share* ends in a voiced sound
4. **decided** — regular (decide + d); -ed sounds like /ɪd/ because base ends in /d/

---

### Exercise 6 — Unit 13: Past simple negative

Make these sentences negative:

1. *"The team finished the migration on time."*
2. *"She found the bug during the code review."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. *"The team **didn't finish** the migration on time."* — base form, not *finished*
2. *"She **didn't find** the bug during the code review."* — base form, not *found*

---

### Exercise 7 — Unit 14: Past continuous + past simple

Choose the correct tense for each blank:

> *"While the team _____ (deploy) the hotfix, a second alert _____ (fire) on the database cluster. The on-call engineer _____ (investigate) the logs when she _____ (notice) the root cause."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **was deploying** — ongoing background action (deploy was in progress)
2. **fired** — interrupting event, completed at a specific moment
3. **was investigating** — another ongoing background action
4. **noticed** — the event that happened at that moment; completed, punctual

### Exercise 8 — Integration: Find and fix 3 errors

This paragraph has **3 errors** from this week's units. Find and correct each one:

> *"Yesterday, the CI pipeline didn't ran because the configuration was wrong. While I was reviewing the logs, the monitoring dashboard showed a spike. The team were confused about the root cause, but we didn't took long to identify it."*

✅ **Answers:**
1. **"didn't ran"** → **"didn't run"** — *(Unit 13: after* didn't*, always use the base form)*
2. **"The team were"** → **"The team was"** — *(Unit 11: collective noun → singular* was *in formal English)*
3. **"didn't took"** → **"didn't take"** — *(Unit 13: base form after* didn't*, even for irregular verbs)*

## 3. ✍️ Integrative Writing Task (self-directed)

Write a short paragraph (5–7 sentences) about **a recent project, sprint, or incident at work**, using at least **3 of the 7 structures** from this week. The more structures you weave in naturally, the better.

Suggested structures:
- *Did you…? / Does your team…?*
- *I'm currently… / We're moving to…*
- *We've got… / We haven't got…*
- *The sprint was… / The results weren't…*
- *We built / found / deployed / ran…*
- *We didn't finish / didn't deploy…*
- *I was reviewing… when… / While the team was…*

**Model paragraph for self-comparison:**

> *Last quarter, we built a new API gateway for our internal microservices. The rollout didn't go smoothly at first — we didn't anticipate the traffic spike on launch day. While the team was monitoring the error logs, one of the engineers found a misconfigured rate limit that was causing the failures. The incident wasn't critical, but the dashboard wasn't showing the right metrics. We've got much better alerting now, and the gateway currently handles over 50,000 requests per hour without issues. Does your team use an API gateway pattern? It significantly reduced our inter-service coupling.*

## 4. 🎤 Pronunciation Drill: 5 Trickiest Sounds from This Week

### ⚠️ Sound 1 — "do you" / "did you": the D+Y fusion
*(Units 7 and 13)*

In natural fluent speech:
- **"do you"** → **/dʒuː/** — sounds like "dyoo"
- **"did you"** → **/dɪdʒu/** — sounds like "didja"

This is not sloppy — it is fluent. Brazilians who separate each word sound stiff and overly formal.

**Drill — say three times, faster each time:**
> *"Did you find the root cause? Do you run integration tests?"*
> → *"Didja find the root cause? Dyoo run integration tests?"*

---

### ⚠️ Sound 2 — Unstressed "was" and "were"
*(Units 11 and 14)*

| Form | Stressed (emphasis only) | Normal unstressed |
|---|---|---|
| was | /wɒz/ | **/wəz/** (sounds like "wuz") |
| were | /wɜːr/ | **/wər/** (sounds like "wur") |

Brazilians often say "WAZ" and "WAIR" with full vowels. In natural English, both are almost always reduced to a schwa. Practice the weak forms:

**Drill:**
> *"I was reviewing the RFC when the alerts were all firing at once."*
> → *"I wəz reviewing… the alerts wər all firing…"*

---

### ⚠️ Sound 3 — The /ɜː/ vowel ("ur" words)
*(Units 9 and 11)*

This vowel doesn't exist in Portuguese. Lips slightly rounded, tongue mid-central — a neutral "ur" with no movement.

| Word | IPA | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| weren't | /wɜːnt/ | **"WURN't"** — one syllable, not "WAIR-rent" |
| currently | /ˈkɜːrəntli/ | **"KUR-runt-lee"** |
| working | /ˈwɜːrkɪŋ/ | **"WUR-king"** |

**Drill:**
> *"The team weren't working on the right priority — I'm currently fixing that."*
> → *"/wɜːnt ˈwɜːrkɪŋ/ … /ˈkɜːrəntli/"*

---

### ⚠️ Sound 4 — -ed endings: three sounds, not one
*(Unit 12)*

| Final sound of base verb | -ed sounds like | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Unvoiced (k, p, s, f, sh) | **/t/** | worked /wɜːkt/, finished /ˈfɪnɪʃt/, shipped /ʃɪpt/ |
| Voiced (l, n, r, v, z, m, b) or vowel | **/d/** | reviewed /rɪˈvjuːd/, deployed /dɪˈplɔɪd/ |
| /t/ or /d/ | **/ɪd/** (extra syllable) | started /ˈstɑːtɪd/, decided /dɪˈsaɪdɪd/ |

Brazilians most commonly say "work-EED" or "deploy-EED" — adding a full syllable where English has none.

**Drill — say three times, noticing the ending of each verb:**
> *"We worked, reviewed, and started the post-mortem."*
> → *"/wɜːkt/ … /rɪˈvjuːd/ … /ˈstɑːtɪd/"*

---

### ⚠️ Sound 5 — The /æ/ vowel: "have", "access", "can't"
*(Unit 10)*

The /æ/ is a flat, wide "a" — somewhere between the "a" in *pai* and the "e" in *pé*. It does not exist in Portuguese.

| Word | IPA | Note |
|---|---|---|
| have | /hæv/ | Not "HAYV" |
| haven't | /ˈhævənt/ | "HAV-unt" |
| access | /ˈækses/ | Stress on first syllable; first vowel is /æ/ |
| has | /hæz/ | Not "HAYZ" |

**Drill:**
> *"Have you got access to the dashboard? She hasn't got the permissions."*
> → *"/hæv… ˈækses… ˈhæzənt/"*

## 5. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**O que deve estar se consolidando após esta semana:** Você cobriu sete estruturas que aparecem constantemente em comunicação profissional de tecnologia. As **perguntas no presente simples** (*Do/Does + subject + base verb*) são a base de *code reviews*, entrevistas técnicas e *1:1s*. O contraste entre **present continuous e present simple** — o que está em progresso agora versus o que é permanente ou habitual — é essencial para dar contexto em Slack, atualizações de sprint e posts no LinkedIn. O **have got** agiliza a forma de descrever o que você tem ou não tem (permissões, experiência, serviços em produção). No passado, você agora cobre quatro camadas: **was/were** para estados passados simples, **past simple positivo** para eventos e ações concluídas, **didn't/did?** para negar e perguntar sobre o passado, e o **past continuous** para descrever o pano de fundo de uma história enquanto eventos acontecem.

**O que provavelmente ainda precisa de atenção:** Os dois erros mais frequentes desta semana são: (1) usar a forma do passado depois de *didn't* — *"didn't saw"*, *"didn't ran"*, *"didn't built"* — lembre-se: *did* já carrega o passado, então o verbo principal volta à forma base sem exceção; (2) misturar past continuous e past simple errado — o continuous é o pano de fundo em andamento, o simple é o evento pontual e concluído. Na pronúncia, os sons /ɜː/ (*weren't*, *currently*, *working*), a fusão *"did you"* → /dɪdʒu/, e os três sons do -ed (/t/, /d/, /ɪd/) são os pontos que mais diferenciam um falante fluente de um ainda em desenvolvimento.

O model paragraph no Exercise 7 desta revisão também é um bom template para começar a escrever post-mortems e retrospectives em inglês com confiança.
