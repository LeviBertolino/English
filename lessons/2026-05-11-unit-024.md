## 1. 📘 Unit 024 — I will...

**Grammar focus:** Using *will* (and *won't*) for spontaneous decisions, offers, promises, and predictions — and when to use the contracted form *'ll*.

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **Engineering Slack — Friday afternoon:**
>
> **Priya:** The staging deployment just failed. I don't have capacity to debug it today.
> **Levi:** Don't worry — **I'll take a look** at it this afternoon.
> **Priya:** Are you sure? It might be a config issue.
> **Levi:** **I'll check** the environment variables first. **I'll let you know** what I find.
> **Priya:** **Will you ping** the on-call if it needs an escalation?
> **Levi:** Yeah, **I'll ping** them if I can't fix it in 30 minutes.
> **Priya:** I think the new config changes **will cause** issues in other environments too.
> **Levi:** Agreed. **I won't merge** anything else today until this is sorted.

Notice: all the *will/won't* decisions are made **right now**, in the moment — not pre-planned.

---

### The Rule

**Will (= 'll)** is used when you decide to do something **at the moment of speaking** — the decision is made as you talk, not before.

**Four main uses:**

| Use | Example |
|---|---|
| **Instant decision** (decided right now) | *"I'll take that ticket."* |
| **Offer** (volunteering help spontaneously) | *"I'll review your PR."* |
| **Promise** (committing to do something) | *"I'll have it ready by Monday."* |
| **Prediction** (what you think will happen) | *"AI will change how teams work."* |

**Forms:**

| | Affirmative | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Full form** | I/you/he/she/we/they **will** + infinitive | **will not** + infinitive | **Will** + subject + infinitive? |
| **Contracted** | I**'ll**, you**'ll**, she**'ll**, we**'ll**, they**'ll** | **won't** (= will not) | same |

**Key contrast — will vs going to:**
- *Will* = decision made **now**, while speaking → *"The build just broke. I'll restart the container."*
- *Going to* = decision made **before** speaking, already planned → *"I'm going to refactor the auth module this sprint."* (Unit 025 covers this.)

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

**Tech & leadership (5):**

1. *I'll open a ticket for that — give me two minutes.* — instant decision; decided right now while talking.
2. *This architectural change will affect every downstream service in the system.* — prediction about a consequence.
3. *I won't approve the PR until the tests pass — that's the process.* — firm promise/commitment (negative).
4. *I'll schedule a design review for next Thursday so everyone can align before we start coding.* — spontaneous decision made in the meeting.
5. *The new LLM-powered search feature will reduce support tickets by improving self-serve accuracy.* — prediction about a business outcome.

**Everyday life (3):**

6. *"There's no milk." "I'll get some on my way home."* — instant decision triggered by new information.
7. *I promise I won't be late to the call tomorrow.* — explicit promise using *won't*.
8. *I think it will rain this afternoon — I'll take an umbrella.* — prediction + decision that follows from it.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Using present simple instead of will for instant decisions:**
> ❌ *"The build is broken? OK, I fix it."*
> ✅ *"The build is broken? OK, I'll fix it."*
>
> *Why:* A decision made right now (at the moment of speaking) uses **will** + infinitive, not the present simple. *"I fix it"* sounds like a habitual action (I always fix it), not a commitment.

**Mistake 2 — Adding "to" after will:**
> ❌ *"I will to review your PR this afternoon."*
> ✅ *"I will review your PR this afternoon."*
>
> *Why:* **Will** is a modal verb. All modals (can, should, must, might, will) are followed by the **bare infinitive** — no *to*.

**Mistake 3 — Using will for pre-planned actions (use going to instead):**
> ❌ *"I will refactor the auth module next sprint."* (if you planned this yesterday)
> ✅ *"I'm going to refactor the auth module next sprint."*
>
> *Why:* If you decided **before** the conversation — it's on your sprint board, your calendar — use *going to*. **Will** is for decisions made **as you speak**. (Full comparison in Unit 026.)

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The "breaking news" trick:**

Think of *will* as your brain's **breaking news ticker** — it fires the moment you receive new information and decide something right then and there:

> 🔴 *BREAKING: The build is failing.*
> 🧠 *Decision made NOW → I'll fix it.*

If the decision was made **yesterday, last week, or in sprint planning** — that's old news. Use *going to*.

One-line shortcut: **Did I just decide this?** → *will*. **Did I already decide this before?** → *going to*.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| will | /wɪl/ | Short /ɪ/ — "wih-l", not "weel" |
| 'll (contracted) | /l/ fused | Barely a syllable — fuses onto the subject: *I'll* = /aɪl/ |
| won't | /wəʊnt/ | One syllable: "wohnt"; the /əʊ/ diphthong as in *"go"* |
| will not | /wɪl nɒt/ | Emphatic/strong form; used for refusals or emphasis |
| I'll | /aɪl/ | Sounds like *"aisle"* |
| you'll | /juːl/ | Sounds like *"yule"* |
| we'll | /wiːl/ | Sounds like *"wheel"* |
| they'll | /ðeɪl/ | Sounds like *"dale"* |
| he'll / she'll | /hiːl/ / /ʃiːl/ | *"heel"* / *"she + l"* |
| prediction | /prɪˈdɪkʃən/ | Three syllables: pri-DIK-shun |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — The contracted *'ll* almost disappears** ⚠️
In fast English, *I will* → *I'll* = /aɪl/ — just one syllable, like "aisle." Brazilians often hesitate and say *"I... will..."* with a visible gap. Practice the fusion: *I'll, you'll, we'll, they'll* — all one tight syllable, no break.

**Sound 2 — *Won't* is /wəʊnt/, NOT two syllables** ⚠️
Brazilians sometimes expand *won't* into *"will not"* rhythm or two syllables. It is always **one syllable**: /wəʊnt/. The /əʊ/ is a diphthong (starts like /ə/, glides to /ʊ/), like *"go"* or *"no"*. Practice the chain: *go → no → won't* — same diphthong, then add /nt/.

**Sound 3 — The short /ɪ/ in *will*** ⚠️
The vowel in *will* is the short, relaxed /ɪ/ — like *"bit"* or *"sit"*, not the tight Portuguese /i/ (as in *"bico"*). Saying *"weel"* (long /iː/) makes it sound like *"wheel."* Keep it short: /wɪl/.

**Sound 4 — Linking: *will + you* → "wil-ya"** ⚠️
*"Will you...?"* in fast speech sounds like /wɪljə/ — the /j/ glide from *you* merges with the /l/ of *will*. You'll hear this constantly: *"Will you review this?"* → /wɪljə rɪˈvjuː ðɪs/. It's not sloppy — it's natural connected speech.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
Esta unidade cobre o uso de *will* (e sua forma contraída *'ll*) para o futuro em inglês. O *will* tem quatro usos principais: (1) **decisão instantânea** — você decide algo no momento em que fala, não antes: *"I'll fix it"* (decidi agora); (2) **oferta espontânea**: *"I'll review your PR"*; (3) **promessa**: *"I'll have it ready by Friday"*; (4) **previsão**: *"AI will change how we work."* A estrutura é simples: sujeito + *will* + infinitivo sem *to* — nunca *"I will to fix it."* A forma negativa é *won't* (= *will not*). A forma contraída *'ll* é muito comum na fala e em mensagens informais: *I'll, you'll, we'll, they'll.*

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais frequente para falantes de português é usar o presente simples onde o inglês pede *will* para decisões instantâneas: ❌ *"OK, I fix it"* → ✅ *"OK, I'll fix it."* Outro cuidado: *will* é para decisões tomadas **agora, enquanto você fala** — se você já planejou antes (está no sprint, no calendário), use *going to* (Unidade 025). E lembre-se: depois de *will*, sempre o **infinitivo sem *to*** — ❌ *"will to review"* → ✅ *"will review."* Na pronúncia, o maior desafio é o *won't* (uma sílaba: /wəʊnt/) e a contração *'ll* que se funde ao sujeito: *I'll* soa como *"aisle."* Pratique os pares: *I will → I'll*, *we will → we'll*, *they will → they'll*.
