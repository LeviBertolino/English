## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Levi, in a sprint planning sync:**
>
> **PM:** Are we shipping the new AI feature this sprint?
>
> **Levi:** We **might ship** it Friday, but I'm not sure — the load tests **may** still **fail**.
>
> **PM:** And the mobile team's part?
>
> **Levi:** They **may need** another week. It **might slip** to next sprint.

Notice:
- **may / might + base verb** express something **possible in the present or future**, but not certain.
- In this everyday use, **may** and **might** are largely interchangeable — *might* nudges very slightly toward "less certain," but both just mean "maybe."
- Negative: **may not / might not** — never contracted (❌ *mayn't*, ❌ *mightn't* in modern usage).
- Questions with *may/might* about possibility are awkward in English — instead, ask **"Do you think...?"** (*"Do you think it'll rain?"* rather than *"May it rain?"*).

### The rule

| Form | Example |
|---|---|
| Affirmative | The build **may fail** on the first try. |
| Affirmative (alt.) | She **might join** the platform team next quarter. |
| Negative | We **may not finish** the migration this week. |
| Negative (alt.) | They **might not agree** to the new deadline. |
| Asking about possibility | **Do you think** the client **will sign** by Friday? *(not "May the client sign...?")* |

> ⚠️ Common trap: confusing **may/might** (possibility) with **can** (ability/permission). *"I can deploy on Fridays"* = I'm allowed to / capable of it. *"I may deploy on Friday"* = it's possible I will, not certain.
> ⚠️ Don't add **-s** or **to**: ❌ *"He mays finish"*, ❌ *"She might to come"*. It's always **may/might + base verb**, like other modals.
> ⚠️ **May I...?** is a separate, common use for **permission** (*"May I ask a question?"*) — different from the possibility use taught here.

## 3. ✏️ 8 example sentences

1. The deployment **may fail** if we skip the canary stage.
2. She **might join** the platform team next quarter — nothing's confirmed yet.
3. We **may need** to roll back if error rates keep climbing.
4. The client **might sign** the contract by Friday, according to sales.
5. Our on-call engineer **may not see** the alert until morning.
6. It **might rain** later, so grab an umbrella.
7. He **may be** running late — there's bad traffic downtown.
8. I **might cook** pasta tonight, I haven't decided yet.

## 5. 💡 Memory tip

**May/might = you're narrating a coin flip out loud.** Whenever you're describing something that could go either way — a deploy, the weather, someone's plans — reach for *may/might + base verb* instead of committing to *will*.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Hoje vimos **may/might + verbo no infinitivo sem "to"**, usados para expressar **possibilidade no presente ou futuro** — algo que pode acontecer, mas não é certo. Na prática do dia a dia, *may* e *might* são quase intercambiáveis (*might* soa levemente menos certo), então não vale a pena travar tentando escolher o "correto" entre os dois.

Cuidado para não confundir com **can** (capacidade/permissão) nem tentar transformar em pergunta diretamente (❌ *"May it rain?"*) — para perguntar sobre possibilidade, use **"Do you think...?"**. A negativa é **may not / might not**, sempre sem contração (nada de *mayn't* ou *mightn't*). E lembre-se: **"May I...?"** é uma estrutura à parte, usada para pedir permissão, não para expressar possibilidade.
