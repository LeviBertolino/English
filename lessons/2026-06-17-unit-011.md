## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Excerpt from a weekly engineering newsletter Levi wrote:**
>
> *"Last quarter, our platform team shipped three major features. We migrated the auth service to OAuth 2.0, rewrote the caching layer, and moved the whole stack to Kubernetes. The team worked through several production incidents along the way — we learned a lot. I also attended KubeCon in Amsterdam. The talks were excellent, and I came back with a clear roadmap for the next six months."*

Notice: **shipped**, **migrated**, **rewrote**, **moved**, **worked**, **learned**, **attended**, **came back** — all past tense verbs. Some just added *-ed* (worked, learned, attended). Others changed their form completely (rewrote, came, moved follows the *-ed* pattern, but some didn't).

### The rule

The **simple past** is used to talk about completed actions in the past — things that started and finished at a specific time.

For affirmative sentences, the structure is simple:

> **Subject + past verb form** (no auxiliaries needed)

There are two types of past verb forms:

#### Regular verbs — add -ed (or -d)

| Base form | Simple past |
|---|---|
| work | **worked** |
| help | **helped** |
| start | **started** |
| use | **used** |
| migrate | **migrated** |
| review | **reviewed** |
| deploy | **deployed** |
| finish | **finished** |

> **Spelling note:** If the verb ends in consonant + *y*, change *y* → *i* + *ed*: **study → studied**, **try → tried**. If it ends in a single stressed vowel + consonant, double the consonant: **stop → stopped**, **plan → planned**.

#### Irregular verbs — the form changes completely (must be memorised)

These are high-frequency verbs — they're worth learning cold:

| Base form | Simple past | Base form | Simple past |
|---|---|---|---|
| go | **went** | get | **got** |
| come | **came** | make | **made** |
| write | **wrote** | run | **ran** |
| give | **gave** | take | **took** |
| find | **found** | build | **built** |
| break | **broke** | ship | **shipped** ✅ regular |
| see | **saw** | think | **thought** |
| know | **knew** | tell | **told** |
| read | **read** (/rɛd/) | send | **sent** |
| rewrite | **rewrote** | begin | **began** |

> **Key point for today:** We're only dealing with **affirmative** sentences here (*I went*, *she built*, *they deployed*). Negatives (*didn't go*) and questions (*Did you go?*) come in Unit 13 — one thing at a time.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The team shipped the v2 API last Thursday — it went to production without a single rollback."** — tech, regular + irregular
2. **"I rewrote the authentication module in Rust; the latency dropped from 40 ms to 4 ms."** — engineering, irregular *rewrote*
3. **"She found a critical memory leak during the code review and filed a P0 bug immediately."** — code review, irregular *found*
4. **"Our staff engineer built a deployment pipeline that cut release time from 2 hours to 8 minutes."** — tech leadership, irregular *built*
5. **"The incident response team ran a post-mortem and identified three root causes."** — incident management, irregular *ran*
6. **"I sent the architecture proposal to the principal engineers and got their sign-off by noon."** — engineering communication, irregular *sent* + *got*
7. **"She studied computer science in São Paulo and then moved to Berlin for her first senior role."** — everyday life / career, regular *studied* + *moved*
8. **"I finished the book on a long flight — I read the whole thing in one go."** — everyday life, *read* past = /rɛd/ (same spelling, different pronunciation!)

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Fill in the simple past of the verb in brackets:

> Last week, the backend team \_\_\_ (1. finish) the database migration. They \_\_\_ (2. migrate) 500 GB of user data with zero downtime. The lead engineer \_\_\_ (3. write) a migration script, \_\_\_ (4. test) it in staging, and then \_\_\_ (5. run) it on Sunday night. The process \_\_\_ (6. take) about three hours. I \_\_\_ (7. send) a summary to the CTO first thing Monday morning — she \_\_\_ (8. reply) immediately and \_\_\_ (9. say) it \_\_\_ (10. go) better than expected.

✅ **Answers:**
1. **finished** (regular)
2. **migrated** (regular)
3. **wrote** (irregular)
4. **tested** (regular)
5. **ran** (irregular)
6. **took** (irregular)
7. **sent** (irregular)
8. **replied** (*y → ied*, regular)
9. **said** (irregular)
10. **went** (irregular)

### Production task

*Write 2 sentences about something your team (or you) completed recently — one using a regular verb and one using an irregular verb.*

**Model answer:**
1. *"We reviewed all open pull requests before the sprint ended and merged twelve of them."* (regular: *reviewed*, *merged*)
2. *"I gave a lightning talk on prompt engineering at the last all-hands meeting — the response went better than I expected."* (irregular: *gave*, *went*)

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**Regular verbs are boring — they just add -ed. Irregular verbs are famous.** The highest-frequency English verbs (go, come, make, get, find, give, write, run, build, send) are almost all irregular — they've been used so long that they've worn their own grooves. Think of them as VIPs who don't follow the rules. Learn them by encounter, not by list — every time you read a post-mortem, a changelog, or a PR description, notice the past verb forms. The irregular ones will stick through repetition.

**Pronunciation tip for -ed endings:**
- After a voiced sound → /d/: *reviewed*, *filed*, *deployed*
- After an unvoiced sound → /t/: *shipped*, *worked*, *helped*
- After *t* or *d* → /ɪd/: *started*, *migrated*, *tested*

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **simple past** (passado simples) é usado para falar de ações completas no passado — coisas que aconteceram e terminaram. Em frases afirmativas, a estrutura é direta: **sujeito + verbo no passado**. Não há auxiliar na afirmativa — o verbo já carrega o significado de passado sozinho (*"She migrated the database"*, não *"She did migrate"*).

Os verbos se dividem em dois grupos: **regulares** (acrescentam *-ed* ou *-d*: work → worked, deploy → deployed, study → studied) e **irregulares** (a forma muda completamente e precisa ser memorizada: go → went, write → wrote, build → built, send → sent). Os verbos irregulares mais importantes são exatamente os mais usados no dia a dia e no trabalho técnico — quanto mais você lê em inglês (post-mortems, changelogs, documentação de projetos), mais rápido essas formas se fixam naturalmente. Um ponto de atenção: o verbo *read* no passado tem a **mesma grafia** do presente, mas a pronúncia muda — lê-se /rɛd/ no passado.
