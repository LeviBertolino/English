## 1. 📘 Unit 019 — for, since, ago

**Grammar focus:** Understanding the three main time expressions — *for*, *since*, and *ago* — and knowing which tense to use with each.

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **Colleague:** When did you move into the Staff Engineer role?
>
> **Levi:** I got promoted about two years **ago**. I've been in the role **for** almost two years now — **since** early 2024, actually.
>
> **Colleague:** And when did the team start the platform migration?
>
> **Levi:** We kicked it off six months **ago**. We haven't shipped to the old system **since** then — so it's been a clean cut **for** six months.
>
> **Colleague:** I joined the company three months **ago** and I still haven't understood how the legacy auth works.
>
> **Levi:** Nobody has understood it **since** 2019. You're fine.

Notice three different words — *ago*, *for*, *since* — each answering a different question:
- *ago* → **When did it happen?** (in the past)
- *for* → **How long has it lasted?**
- *since* → **From when?**

---

### The Rule

| Word | Meaning | Tense it pairs with | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ago** | X time before now | **Past simple only** | *She joined three years ago.* |
| **for** | a duration / span of time | Past simple **or** Present Perfect | *I worked there for two years. / I've worked here for two years.* |
| **since** | a starting point in the past | **Present Perfect only** | *I've been here since 2023.* |

**Key contrasts:**

**ago vs. since:**
- *ago* tells you **when** an event happened: *"The PR was opened two days ago."* → past simple.
- *since* tells you **from when** a state has continued to now: *"The PR has been open since Monday."* → present perfect.

**ago vs. for:**
- *ago* = a point in the past (*"three years ago"* = the event happened at that moment).
- *for* = a length of time (*"for three years"* = it lasted that long).
- ❌ You cannot say: *"three years ago"* and use present perfect → always past simple.
- ❌ You cannot say: *"for three years ago"* — these don't combine.

**for with past simple vs. present perfect:**
- Past simple + *for* → the situation **ended**: *"She worked at Google **for** five years."* (she no longer does)
- Present perfect + *for* → the situation **still continues**: *"She has worked at Google **for** five years."* (she still does)

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Using *ago* with Present Perfect:**
> ❌ I have started this project two weeks ago.
> ✅ I **started** this project two weeks ago.
>
> *Why:* *Ago* always points to a finished past moment → it pairs with past simple only. Present Perfect looks at a connection to now; *ago* closes that connection. They are incompatible.

**Mistake 2 — Using *since* with past simple:**
> ❌ I didn't see her since the team retrospective.
> ✅ I **haven't seen** her since the team retrospective.
>
> *Why:* *Since* stretches from a past point all the way to now — that span requires Present Perfect. Past simple (*didn't*) treats the situation as finished and cut off. If she still hasn't been seen, the Present Perfect is the only correct choice.

**Mistake 3 — Mixing *for* and *ago* in the same phrase:**
> ❌ I joined the company for two years ago.
> ✅ I joined the company **two years ago**. (when it happened)
> ✅ I've been at the company **for two years**. (how long it has lasted)
>
> *Why:* *For* and *ago* answer different questions and cannot be combined. Choose one based on what you want to say: **when** (ago + past simple) or **how long** (for + present perfect or past simple).

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of a **timeline arrow** pointing right toward NOW:

```
PAST ——————————————————————► NOW
  |              |←— for ——►|
  ago            since
 (when)         (from when)
```

- **ago** = drop a pin in the past, look back from NOW. Past simple.
- **since** = draw a line from that pin to NOW. Present Perfect.
- **for** = label the length of that line (or any finished stretch). Present Perfect OR Past Simple.

Shortcut to avoid the #1 error: **If you use *ago*, you MUST use past simple.** No exceptions. *Ago* and Present Perfect are mortal enemies.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ago | /əˈɡoʊ/ | Stress on second syllable; first syllable is reduced /ə/ |
| since | /sɪns/ | Short /ɪ/ — not "seence" |
| for (unstressed) | /fər/ | Reduced — sounds like *"fer"* in natural speech |
| three months ago | /θriː mʌnθs əˈɡoʊ/ | /θ/ at start of *three*; *ago* is weak-strong |
| since then | /sɪns ðen/ | Link smoothly — no pause between *since* and *then* |
| has been | /həz bɪn/ | Both words reduce in normal speech |
| launched | /lɔːntʃt/ | Final /t/ — don't drop it |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — Reduced *ago* = /əˈɡoʊ/** ⚠️
The first syllable of *ago* is a schwa /ə/ — completely unstressed. Brazilians often stress both syllables equally: *"A-go"* instead of *"uh-GO"*. Practice saying it like one quick puff: *uh-GO*. The stress is entirely on the second syllable.

**Sound 2 — /θ/ in *three months*** ⚠️
Appears in almost every sentence about time (*three weeks ago, three months, three years*). Remember: tongue tip lightly touching the back of upper front teeth, air flowing through. Not /t/ ("tree months") and not /f/ ("free months").

**Sound 3 — *since then* linked** ⚠️
Brazilians often put a tiny pause between words: *"since... then"*. In natural English, these link: *"sinc**e** then"* — the final /s/ of *since* flows directly into *then*. Practice saying it as one unit.

**Sound 4 — *has been* reduced** ⚠️
In sentences like *"It has been running for weeks"*, *has been* sounds like /həz bɪn/ — both highly reduced. Brazilians often say the full /hæz biːn/, which sounds over-formal. Aim for: *"It-əz-bɪn running."*

**Sound 5 — Final /t/ in *launched*, *started*, *worked*** ⚠️
Past simple verbs ending in /t/ or /d/ sounds require a clear final consonant. *"Launched"* → /lɔːntʃt/ — four sounds at the end: *n-ch-t*. Don't drop the /t/ or the word blurs into *"launchd"* or just *"launch."*

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
As três expressões de tempo *for*, *since* e *ago* respondem perguntas diferentes. *Ago* responde "quando?" — indica um momento no passado medido a partir de agora (*two years ago* = há dois anos) — e sempre pede o **passado simples**: ❌ *"I have joined the team two years ago"* → ✅ *"I joined the team two years ago."* *Since* responde "desde quando?" — indica o ponto de partida de uma situação que ainda continua — e sempre pede o **Present Perfect**: ❌ *"I didn't see her since Monday"* → ✅ *"I haven't seen her since Monday."* *For* responde "por quanto tempo?" — mede a duração — e pode ir com o **passado simples** (situação encerrada: *"She worked there for five years"*) ou com o **Present Perfect** (situação que ainda continua: *"She has worked here for five years"*).

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais frequente é misturar *ago* com o Present Perfect: ❌ *"I have started this job two months ago"* → ✅ *"I started this job two months ago."* Toda vez que você usar *ago*, use o passado simples — sem exceção. O segundo erro comum é usar *since* com o passado simples: ❌ *"I didn't speak to him since the meeting"* → ✅ *"I haven't spoken to him since the meeting."* E cuidado com a tentação de escrever *"for two years ago"* — *for* e *ago* nunca se combinam. Escolha um: *ago* para dizer quando aconteceu, *for* para dizer quanto tempo durou.
