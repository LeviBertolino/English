### 1. 📘 Unit 006 — I don't... (present simple negative)

---

### 2. 🧠 Mini-context

> **CEO:** Are we betting on every AI vendor in the market?
> **You:** No — we **don't** chase every trend. The portfolio **doesn't** fund
> anything without a business case, and I **don't** approve pilots that lack a
> clear owner.
> **CEO:** So what about the generative-AI startup from last week?
> **You:** It **doesn't** fit our thesis yet. The team **doesn't** have the data
> maturity we need, so we wait.

Notice the pattern before any rule: to make the present simple negative, we don't
touch the main verb — we put **don't** or **doesn't** in front of it: *we
**don't** chase*, *the portfolio **doesn't** fund*, *it **doesn't** fit*. The
main verb (*chase, fund, fit*) stays in its plain base form.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

The present simple negative uses the auxiliary **do / does + not + base verb**.
The subject decides *do* or *does*; the main verb never changes.

| Subject | Auxiliary | Base verb | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | **don't** (do not) | base | *We **don't fund** early bets.* |
| he / she / it | **doesn't** (does not) | base | *She **doesn't fund** early bets.* |

**The one rule that catches everyone:** the **-s of he/she/it moves onto the
auxiliary** and disappears from the main verb.
- Positive: *She **funds** it.* → the -s is on *fund*.
- Negative: *She **doesn't fund** it.* → the -s is now inside *does**n't***; the
  main verb goes back to plain *fund* (never *funds*).

**Structure:** *subject + don't/doesn't + base verb + rest.*
Full forms *do not / does not* exist but sound formal or emphatic; *don't /
doesn't* are normal and correct in professional speech and writing.

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** It is the negative of everything Unit 005
covered: habits, routines, permanent facts, and how a system works — stated in
the negative. *We **don't** run pilots without an owner* says it is **not our
standing practice**. It is the "as a rule, no / generally not" form, not a denial
about this exact moment.

**Decision rule — present simple negative vs present continuous negative (the
rival).** Same fault line as Units 003–005, in the negative. Ask: am I denying a
**habit / general truth**, or something **happening right now**?

| | Present simple negative | Present continuous negative |
|---|---|---|
| Denies | a habit, routine, **general truth** | an action **happening now / temporarily** |
| Form | *don't / doesn't* + **base verb** | *am/is/are* + **not** + verb**-ing** |
| Example | *We **don't review** vendors weekly.* | *We **aren't reviewing** vendors this week.* |
| Test words | *usually, every, in general, ever* | *right now, at the moment, today* |

Minimal pair — same words, different meaning:
- *The board **doesn't approve** budgets over two million.* → as a **rule**, that
  is not within its authority.
- *The board **isn't approving** the budget.* → **right now, in this meeting**,
  it is not going through.

**PT→EN mapping.** Portuguese negates by putting **"não" directly before the
verb**: *"Ele não aprova."* English cannot copy this — it **requires the
auxiliary do/does**: *"He **doesn't** approve."* The word-for-word translation
*"He not approve"* ❌ is the single most common interference error, because
Portuguese has no equivalent of *do/does* here. Two more traps:
- **The "não" is one word; English splits it into auxiliary + not.** *"Não
  financiamos" → "We **don't** fund"* — you must add *do* out of nothing.
- **After "não" Portuguese keeps the conjugated verb** (*ele não aprova*), so
  Brazilians instinctively keep the -s in English too: *"He doesn't approves"* ❌.
  English has already spent the -s on *doesn't*, so the main verb is bare.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *He doesn't approves early-stage bets.* → ✅ *He **doesn't approve**
  early-stage bets.* — *the -s lives on **doesn't**; the main verb returns to its
  base form. Never mark the negative twice.*
- ❌ *We not invest in unproven vendors.* → ✅ *We **don't invest** in unproven
  vendors.* — *English needs the auxiliary; you can't negate a plain verb with a
  bare "not" the way "não" works in Portuguese.*
- ❌ *The directors doesn't agree with the roadmap.* → ✅ *The directors **don't
  agree** with the roadmap.* — *plural subject → **don't**, not "doesn't"; match
  the auxiliary to the subject, same as the positive -s rule.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. We **don't chase** every AI trend in the market. — *denies a general practice
   → present simple negative; "aren't chasing" would deny only a live action.*
2. The portfolio **doesn't fund** anything without a business case. — *single
   subject → **doesn't**; the standing rule of how the model works.*
3. I **don't approve** pilots that lack a clear owner. — *"I" → **don't**; a
   permanent principle of the role, not a one-time refusal.*
4. That startup **doesn't fit** our investment thesis yet. — *single subject →
   **doesn't** + base "fit"; a general judgment, not "isn't fitting right now".*
5. The board **doesn't sign off** on spend above its mandate. — *company rule →
   present simple negative; the -s sits on **doesn't**, so "sign off" is bare.*
6. Our competitors **don't have** an AI-first operating model. — *plural subject
   → **don't**; a stable fact about the market, not this moment.*
7. She **doesn't attend** every steering committee, only the strategic ones. —
   *habit with an exception → present simple negative; single subject → doesn't.*
8. I **don't drink** coffee after lunch and my wife **doesn't work** on Fridays.
   — *everyday life; "I" → don't, single "my wife" → doesn't, both base verbs.*

### 8. 🗣️ Para levar para a aula

- Ask your tutor to **challenge each of your investment principles** — *"So you
  fund everything promising?"* — and you push back every time with a present
  simple negative: *"No, we **don't** fund…", "The board **doesn't** sign off…"*
  They listen only for two faults: a missing *do/does* and a stray -s on the main
  verb. The goal is to make *doesn't + bare verb* automatic under pressure.
- Then a **now-vs-general negative drill**: your tutor names an activity and asks
  it two ways — *"You don't do that?"* (habit) and *"You aren't doing that?"*
  (right now) — and you answer each with the matching negative, so the
  simple-vs-continuous choice stays reflexive in the negative too.

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** A negativa do *present simple* é **do/does + not +
verbo na base**: *"We **don't fund** it"*, *"She **doesn't fund** it"*. O sujeito
escolhe *don't* (I/you/we/they) ou *doesn't* (he/she/it), e o **-s da terceira
pessoa "migra" para o auxiliar** — por isso o verbo principal volta à forma base:
é *"He **doesn't approve**"*, nunca *"doesn't approves"*. Use este tempo para
negar **hábitos, rotinas e verdades gerais** (*"We don't chase every trend"*).
Contraste com a negativa do *present continuous* (*am/is/are + not + -ing*), que
nega algo **acontecendo agora**: *"We **aren't reviewing** vendors this week."*
Decida pelas palavras: *usually / every / in general* → simple; *right now / at
the moment* → continuous.

**As armadilhas.** (1) A maior é a interferência do português: *"não aprova"* vira
*"He **doesn't** approve"*, **nunca** *"He not approve"* — o inglês exige o
auxiliar *do/does*, que não existe em português. (2) Não marque a negativa duas
vezes: como o -s já está em *doesn't*, o verbo fica na base — *"doesn't approve"*,
não *"doesn't approves"*. (3) Combine o auxiliar com o sujeito: plural → *don't*
(*"The directors **don't** agree"*), singular → *doesn't*.
