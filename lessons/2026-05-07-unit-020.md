## 1. 📘 Unit 020 — I have done and I did (Present Perfect vs. Past Simple)

**Grammar focus:** Knowing when to use Present Perfect (*have done*) vs. Past Simple (*did*) — the most important and most often confused distinction for Brazilian English speakers.

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **Recruiter:** Have you worked with distributed systems?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, I've worked with them for about six years.
>
> **Recruiter:** When did you first use Kafka?
>
> **Levi:** I started using it in 2020. My team built a real-time event pipeline at my previous company.
>
> **Recruiter:** Have you ever led a production incident response?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, I've led several. The most recent one happened three months ago. We had a database failover and I coordinated the entire response.

Notice: Some answers use *have worked*, *'ve led* (Present Perfect). Others use *started*, *built*, *happened* (Past Simple). Why?

---

### The Rule

**Present Perfect** connects a past action to *now*. Use it when:
- The action still matters now (result, consequence, news)
- You're talking about experience **without saying when**: *"I've used Kubernetes before."*
- The time period is **not finished yet**: *this week, today, this sprint, this year*
- With these words: *just, already, yet, ever, never, for, since*

**Past Simple** treats an action as **finished and in the past**. Use it when:
- You say **when** it happened: *yesterday, last week, in 2020, three months ago, at 2am*
- The time period is clearly over
- You're telling a story or a sequence of events

**The deciding question:** *Are you saying WHEN it happened?*
- Yes → Past Simple
- No (or the result is still relevant now) → Present Perfect

### Key contrasts

| Present Perfect | Past Simple |
|---|---|
| *Have you seen this PR?* (no time) | *Did you see it this morning?* (time = this morning) |
| *I've reviewed your proposal.* (result: I know it now) | *I reviewed it last Tuesday.* (when = last Tuesday) |
| *She's worked here for two years.* (still here) | *She worked there for two years.* (she left — finished) |
| *We've never rolled back a migration.* (experience) | *We rolled back last March.* (specific event) |

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

**Tech & leadership (5):**

1. *I've just merged the refactor.* → Present Perfect: *just* = very recent; the change is live **now**.
2. *The team deployed the hotfix at 2am. It went smoothly.* → Past Simple: specific time (2am); event finished and over.
3. *She has shipped three features this sprint.* → Present Perfect: this sprint is **still in progress** — open time period.
4. *He resigned from the principal role last month.* → Past Simple: *last month* = closed period.
5. *We've never rolled back a migration in production.* → Present Perfect: experience up to now, no specific time.

**Everyday life (3):**

6. *I've read that book — it's excellent.* → Present Perfect: result is relevant now (I can recommend it); no time given.
7. *I read it last summer.* → Past Simple: *last summer* = finished period.
8. *The incident happened on Friday and the team fixed it by Sunday.* → Past Simple: specific days; closed sequence.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Present Perfect + specific past time:**
> ❌ I have joined this company in 2021.
> ✅ I **joined** this company in 2021.
>
> *Why:* *In 2021* is a finished, specific past moment. Present Perfect never pairs with a closed time reference. Once you say **when**, you must use past simple. No exceptions.

**Mistake 2 — Past Simple for experience with no time reference:**
> ❌ Did you ever work with microservices?
> ✅ **Have you ever** worked with microservices?
>
> *Why:* *Ever* asks about life experience up to now — no specific time. That is Present Perfect territory. *"Did you ever"* is a common interference from Portuguese (*você já trabalhou...* → "have you ever worked"), but in standard English, *have you ever* is the correct form.

**Mistake 3 — Past Simple when the time period is still open:**
> ❌ We released two updates today. *(if it's still today)*
> ✅ We**'ve released** two updates today.
>
> *Why:* When *today*, *this week*, *this sprint*, or *this year* are still ongoing, Present Perfect is the natural choice — the period isn't closed yet. Past simple treats the period as finished.

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of a **news headline vs. a police report**:

- **Present Perfect = News headline** → *"Team has launched new AI feature!"* The reader doesn't care exactly when; they care that it's new and relevant right now. No timestamp needed.
- **Past Simple = Police report** → *"The incident occurred at 11:47pm on Friday. The on-call engineer responded at 11:52pm."* Specific, timestamped, closed.

**Golden test:** *Did I say when?*
- Yes → Past Simple
- No, and it still matters now → Present Perfect

This single question covers 80% of real usage.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I've | /aɪv/ | Contraction; final /v/ must be voiced — not /f/ |
| have you | /həvjuː/ | Reduces to /həvjə/ in natural speech |
| I've worked | /aɪv wɜːrkt/ | Link *I've* directly into *worked* — no gap |
| I did | /aɪ dɪd/ | Short /ɪ/ — not /iː/ ("deed") |
| have you ever | /həvjuː ˈɛvər/ | *Ever* = stress on first syllable |
| just | /dʒʌst/ | Vowel is /ʌ/ — not /uː/ ("joost") |
| yet | /jɛt/ | Short /ɛ/ — not "yeet" |
| already | /ɔːlˈrɛdi/ | Stress on second syllable: *ol-RED-ee* |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — *I've* = /aɪv/** ⚠️
Brazilians often say the full *"I have"* instead of the contracted *I've*. In natural speech, the contraction is far more common. The final /v/ must be voiced: *"eye-v"*, never *"eye-f"*.

**Sound 2 — *have you* → /həvjə/** ⚠️
In questions like *"Have you ever...?"*, the two words run together into one fast unit: *"həvjə ˈɛvər..."*. Don't say each word separately. Practice until it sounds like a single blur.

**Sound 3 — *just* = /dʒʌst/** ⚠️
Brazilians often say /dʒuːst/ — rhyming with "joost". The correct vowel is /ʌ/ — the same short, central sound as *cup*, *run*, *bus*. Short and open: *"juhst"*.

**Sound 4 — *already* = /ɔːlˈrɛdi/** ⚠️
Stress lands on the **second** syllable: *ol-RED-ee*. Brazilians sometimes stress the first: *ALL-ready* — which sounds like *"all ready"* (a completely different phrase meaning "fully prepared").

**Sound 5 — final /kt/ in *worked*, *checked*, *liked*** ⚠️
Past simple and past participle forms ending in /kt/ need both consonants. *"Worked"* = /wɜːrkt/ — keep the final *-kt* clear. Don't reduce to *"work"* or let it blur into *"workd"*.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
A diferença entre o *Present Perfect* (*have done*) e o *Past Simple* (*did*) é uma das distinções mais importantes do inglês — e uma das mais difíceis para falantes de português, porque o pretérito perfeito em português cobre os dois casos. A pergunta decisiva é: **você está dizendo quando aconteceu?** Se sim, use o passado simples. Se não, e a ação ainda tem relevância agora (resultado, experiência, notícia recente), use o Present Perfect. *"I joined this company in 2021"* → passado simples (disse quando: *in 2021*). *"I've worked with microservices before"* → Present Perfect (não disse quando; é uma experiência que ainda importa agora). Palavras como *yesterday, last week, in 2020, three months ago, at 2am* sempre pedem o passado simples. Palavras como *just, already, yet, ever, never, for, since* normalmente pedem o Present Perfect. Períodos ainda em aberto (*today, this week, this sprint, this year*) também costumam pedir o Present Perfect.

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais comum de brasileiros é usar o Present Perfect com uma referência de tempo passada específica: ❌ *"I have sent the report on Friday"* → ✅ *"I sent the report on Friday."* Toda vez que você mencionar **quando** algo aconteceu — mesmo que implicitamente (*"at the meeting", "last week"*) — use o passado simples, sem exceção. O segundo erro frequente é usar o passado simples para perguntar sobre experiência: ❌ *"Did you ever work with Docker?"* → ✅ *"Have you ever worked with Docker?"* Perguntas sobre experiência de vida, sem referência a um momento específico, usam sempre o Present Perfect. Outro cuidado: o período ainda aberto (*today, this sprint*) pede Present Perfect — ❌ *"We released two updates today"* → ✅ *"We've released two updates today."* (se o dia ainda não acabou).
