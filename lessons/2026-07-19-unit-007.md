Notice the pattern before any rule: every question starts with **do** or
**does** — *Do we have…*, *Does the CFO know…*, *Do you want…*. The main verb
(*have, know, want, meet*) stays in its plain base form. The **-s** you would
expect on *the CFO knows* has moved onto **does**.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

The present simple question uses the auxiliary **do / does + subject + base
verb**. The subject decides *do* or *does*; the main verb never changes.

| Subject | Auxiliary | Subject | Base verb | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | **do** | you / we / they | base | ***Do** we **have** an owner?* |
| he / she / it | **does** | he / she / it | base | ***Does** she **have** an owner?* |

**The rule that catches everyone:** the **-s of he/she/it lands on the
auxiliary**, not on the main verb.
- Statement: *The CFO **knows** the numbers.* → the -s is on *know*.
- Question: ***Does** the CFO **know** the numbers?* → the -s is now inside
  *does*; the main verb goes back to plain *know* (never *knows*).

**Two shapes of question:**
- **Yes/No question:** *Do/Does* + subject + base verb? → *Does the board
  approve this?*
- **Wh- question:** question word + *do/does* + subject + base verb? → *When
  does the board meet?* / *Why do we fund this?*

**Short answers** repeat only the auxiliary, never the main verb:
*"Do you approve?" → "Yes, I **do**." / "No, I **don't**."*
*"Does it fit?" → "Yes, it **does**." / "No, it **doesn't**."*

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** It is how present simple asks about **habits,
routines, systems, and general facts** — the question form of everything Units
005–006 stated and denied. *Do we review vendors quarterly?* asks about a
**standing practice**, not about this exact moment.

**Decision rule — present simple question vs present continuous question (the
rival).** Same fault line as the whole present-tense block, now in the question.
Ask: am I asking about a **habit / general truth**, or about something
**happening right now**?

| | Present simple question | Present continuous question |
|---|---|---|
| Asks about | a habit, routine, **general truth** | an action **happening now / temporarily** |
| Form | *do / does* + subject + **base verb** | *am/is/are* + subject + verb**-ing** |
| Example | ***Does** the board **approve** big budgets?* | ***Is** the board **approving** the budget?* |
| Test words | *usually, ever, how often, in general* | *right now, at the moment, today* |

Minimal pair — same words, different question:
- ***Does** the CFO **review** the AI spend?* → is that **part of their role**,
  as a rule?
- ***Is** the CFO **reviewing** the AI spend?* → is it **going on right now**, in
  this moment?

**PT→EN mapping.** Portuguese asks a yes/no question with **intonation only** —
same words as the statement, just a rising tone: *"Você aprova?"* = *"Você
aprova."* with a question mark. English **cannot** do this; it **requires the
auxiliary do/does** and reorders the sentence: *"**Do** you approve?"* The
word-for-word *"You approve?"* ❌ sounds broken to a native ear. Two more traps:
- **Wh- questions still need do/does.** Portuguese *"Onde a equipe se reúne?"*
  has no auxiliary, so Brazilians drop it: *"Where the team meets?"* ❌ →
  *"Where **does** the team **meet**?"* ✅.
- **After the auxiliary, the verb is bare.** Portuguese keeps the conjugated
  verb in the question, so the -s sneaks back: *"Does the CFO knows?"* ❌ — the
  -s already lives on *does*, so it's *"Does the CFO **know**?"* ✅.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *You approve budgets over two million?* → ✅ ***Do** you **approve** budgets
  over two million?* — *English needs the auxiliary; rising intonation alone is
  not a question here, the way "Você aprova?" is in Portuguese.*
- ❌ *Does the board approves this spend?* → ✅ *Does the board **approve** this
  spend?* — *the -s already sits on **does**; the main verb returns to base.
  Never mark it twice.*
- ❌ *Where the committee meets to decide?* → ✅ *Where **does** the committee
  **meet** to decide?* — *wh- questions also require do/does; you can't just
  front the question word and keep a plain verb.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. **Do** we **have** a clear owner for every AI pilot? — *asks about a standing
   requirement → present simple question; "Are we having" would wrongly ask
   about this moment.*
2. **Does** the CFO **understand** the ROI model behind the budget? — *single
   subject → **does** + base "understand"; a general fact about their knowledge.*
3. **How often does** the steering committee **meet** on the roadmap? — *wh-
   question about frequency → do/does required; "how often" flags a habit.*
4. **Do** our competitors **operate** an AI-first model already? — *plural
   subject → **do**; asks about their general way of working, not this instant.*
5. **Does** this vendor **fit** our long-term architecture? — *single subject →
   **does**; a general judgment, not "is this vendor fitting right now".*
6. **Where do** we **stand** on the innovation portfolio this quarter? — *wh-
   question; "we" → **do** + base "stand"; asks the general state of play.*
7. **Do** you **think** the board **approves** spend at this level? — *outer
   question uses **do you think**; the inner clause keeps its normal statement
   order and the -s stays on "approves".*
8. **Does** your team **work** on Fridays, or **do** you **close** early? —
   *everyday life; single "your team" → does, "you" → do, both base verbs.*

### 8. 🗣️ Para levar para a aula

- Ask your tutor to **role-play a board member firing rapid yes/no questions** at
  you about the AI plan — *"Do we have an owner? Does the CFO agree? Do the
  numbers hold?"* — and you answer with clean short answers: *"Yes, we **do**."
  / "No, it **doesn't**."* They listen only for two faults: a missing *do/does*
  and a stray -s on the main verb. The goal is to make *does + bare verb*
  automatic under pressure.
- Then flip roles: **you interview your tutor about a hypothetical vendor**,
  mixing yes/no and wh- questions — *"How often does your team ship?", "Do you
  support our stack?", "Where does the data live?"* — so building the question
  from *do/does + subject + base verb* becomes reflexive, wh- words included.

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** A pergunta no *present simple* é **do/does + sujeito +
verbo na base**: *"**Do** we **have** an owner?"*, *"**Does** she **know**?"*. O
sujeito escolhe *do* (I/you/we/they) ou *does* (he/she/it), e o **-s da terceira
pessoa "migra" para o auxiliar** — por isso o verbo principal volta à forma base:
é *"Does the CFO **know**?"*, nunca *"Does the CFO knows?"*. As perguntas com
palavra interrogativa (*where, when, how often*) também exigem o auxiliar:
*"**Where does** the team **meet**?"*. Nas respostas curtas, repita só o auxiliar:
*"Yes, I **do**." / "No, it **doesn't**."* Use este tempo para perguntar sobre
**hábitos, rotinas e verdades gerais**; contraste com a pergunta do *present
continuous* (*Is the board approving…?*), que pergunta sobre algo **acontecendo
agora**.

**As armadilhas.** (1) A maior é a interferência do português: em português
pergunta-se só pela entonação (*"Você aprova?"*), mas o inglês **exige** o
auxiliar — *"**Do** you approve?"*, nunca *"You approve?"*. (2) Não marque a
pergunta duas vezes: como o -s já está em *does*, o verbo fica na base —
*"Does he approve?"*, não *"Does he approves?"*. (3) Não esqueça o auxiliar nas
perguntas com *where/when/how* — *"Where **does** the committee meet?"*, não
*"Where the committee meets?"*.
