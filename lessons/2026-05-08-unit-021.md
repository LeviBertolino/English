1. In *"The team deployed the hotfix at 2am. It went smoothly."* — why is this Past Simple, not Present Perfect?
2. Complete with the correct tense: *"We _______ (not deploy) the new version yet — it's still in review."*
3. True or False: *"I have sent the proposal on Monday"* is a correct sentence.

**👉 Answers:**
1. **Past Simple** because of *at 2am* — a specific, closed timestamp. Golden test: *did I say when?* Yes → Past Simple. No exceptions.
2. **haven't deployed** — *yet* signals a pending result still relevant now → Present Perfect.
3. **False.** *On Monday* is a specific past time → Past Simple is required: *"I sent the proposal on Monday."* Present Perfect never pairs with a closed time reference.

## 1. 📘 Unit 021 — is done / was done

**Grammar focus:** Passive voice in the present simple and past simple — shifting the focus from who does something to what happens or what was done.

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **Incident post-mortem (internal wiki):**
>
> The outage was caused by a misconfigured load balancer. All affected services were identified within ten minutes. A rollback was triggered automatically by the monitoring system. The faulty configuration was removed and replaced. All traffic is now routed through the backup cluster. New alerting rules are added after every incident to prevent recurrence.
>
> This report was written by the on-call engineer.

Notice: *was caused*, *were identified*, *was triggered*, *was removed*, *is routed*, *are added*, *was written* — the subject (services, traffic, the report) is **receiving** the action, not doing it. Nobody says *"the team caused…"* or *"John identified…"* — what matters is the result, not who did it.

---

### The Rule

**Active** = the doer is the grammatical subject — focus is on who acts.
**Passive** = the result is the grammatical subject — focus is on what happens.

| Voice | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Active (present) | Subject + **verb** | *"The team reviews all PRs."* |
| **Passive (present)** | Subject + **is/are + past participle** | *"All PRs are reviewed."* |
| Active (past) | Subject + **verb (past)** | *"The team merged the branch."* |
| **Passive (past)** | Subject + **was/were + past participle** | *"The branch was merged."* |

**When to use passive (instead of active):**
1. The doer is **unknown** → *"The server was hacked."* (we don't know who)
2. The doer is **obvious or unimportant** → *"The build was broken."* (by someone on the team — obvious)
3. **Focus on the result**, not the person → *"The migration was completed successfully."*
4. **Technical / formal writing** — documentation, post-mortems, changelogs, and engineering specs almost always use passive.

**Adding *by + agent*:**
You can add *"by [doer]"* when it matters: *"The architecture was reviewed by the principal engineer."*
Omit it when obvious or irrelevant: *"The tests were run"* — no need to say *"by the CI pipeline."*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

**Tech & leadership (5):**

1. *Pull requests are reviewed by at least two engineers before they are merged.* — present passive; standard process, focus on the requirement.
2. *The production database was backed up automatically at 3am.* — past passive; specific time, focus on the result.
3. *A new microservices architecture was adopted by the platform team last quarter.* — past passive with agent and time.
4. *All API responses are cached for performance.* — present passive; general fact about how the system works.
5. *The deployment was blocked because a critical test was failing.* — past passive; incident narrative, focus on what happened.

**Everyday life (3):**

6. *English is taught in schools in over 100 countries.* — present passive; general fact.
7. *This bridge was built in 1889.* — past passive; historical fact, doer is unimportant.
8. *My order was delivered this morning — I didn't even hear the doorbell.* — past passive; result of a recent completed action.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Using base form instead of past participle:**
> ❌ The PR was merge by the senior engineer.
> ✅ The PR was **merged** by the senior engineer.
>
> *Why:* Passive requires **was/were + past participle** (the -ed/-en form). *Merge* is the base form — it can't follow *was* alone. Past participle of *merge* → *merged*.

**Mistake 2 — Mixing passive structure with active subject:**
> ❌ The engineer was deployed the app to production.
> ✅ The **app was deployed** to production (by the engineer).
>
> *Why:* In a passive sentence, the subject receives the action. The engineer is the doer, not the receiver — so the subject of the passive must be the thing done: *the app*. If the engineer is the subject, use active: *"The engineer deployed the app."*

**Mistake 3 — Wrong form of *be* (ignoring subject agreement):**
> ❌ All PRs is reviewed before merging.
> ✅ All PRs **are** reviewed before merging.
>
> *Why:* The verb *be* must agree with the subject. *PRs* is plural → *are*. A single PR → *is reviewed*. Past: one PR → *was reviewed*; two PRs → *were reviewed*.

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The post-mortem mindset:**

In engineering, post-mortems don't say *"John broke the database."* They say *"The database was taken down by a misconfigured query."* This is passive voice — and it's the default language of technical documentation, release notes, and incident reports.

**Formula to remember:**
> Active = **who** did it → *"The CI pipeline ran the tests."*
> Passive = **what** happened → *"The tests were run."*

When you don't care about (or don't know) who — make it passive. When the **doer is important** — use active.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| was (unstressed) | /wəz/ | Almost always unstressed in sentences — reduced vowel |
| was (stressed) | /wɒz/ | Full vowel; only stressed for contrast: *"It WAS fixed."* |
| were (unstressed) | /wər/ | Reduces to /wər/ — rhymes with *"her"* |
| reviewed | /rɪˈvjuːd/ | Stress on second syllable; final /d/ |
| merged | /mɜːrdʒd/ | Consonant cluster /dʒd/ at end |
| deployed | /dɪˈplɔɪd/ | Stress on second syllable; diphthong /ɔɪ/ |
| identified | /aɪˈdɛntɪfaɪd/ | Four syllables: i-DEN-ti-fied |
| tested | /ˈtɛstɪd/ | Two syllables; -ed = /ɪd/ after /t/ |
| rejected | /rɪˈdʒɛktɪd/ | Three syllables; -ed = /ɪd/ after /t/ |
| approved | /əˈpruːvd/ | Final /vd/ cluster |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — Unstressed *was* = /wəz/** ⚠️
In *"The PR was merged"*, *was* reduces to /wəz/ — a very short, almost swallowed vowel. Brazilians often give it full stress (*"woz"*). Practice saying it fast and light: *"The PR was merged"* → /ðə piː ɑːr wəz mɜːrdʒd/.

**Sound 2 — Three -ed endings for past participles** ⚠️
The suffix *-ed* is pronounced three ways, crucial for passive sentences:
- **/d/** after voiced sounds: *reviewed* /rɪˈvjuːd/, *merged* /mɜːrdʒd/, *deployed* /dɪˈplɔɪd/
- **/t/** after voiceless sounds: *checked* /tʃɛkt/, *pushed* /pʊʃt/, *blocked* /blɒkt/
- **/ɪd/** after /t/ or /d/: *tested* /ˈtɛstɪd/, *rejected* /rɪˈdʒɛktɪd/, *updated* /ˈʌpdeɪtɪd/

Brazilians often add an extra vowel: ❌ *"reviewed-ee"* → ✅ *"reviewed"* /rɪˈvjuːd/. The final consonant is not followed by any vowel.

**Sound 3 — *were* = /wər/** ⚠️
Unstressed *were* in *"PRs were reviewed"* reduces to /wər/. It doesn't sound like *"wear"* /wɛr/ or *"war"* /wɔːr/ in fast speech.

**Sound 4 — The /dʒd/ cluster in *merged*** ⚠️
*Merged* ends in three sounds: /r/ + /dʒ/ + /d/. Don't add a vowel after: ❌ *"merged-ee"* → ✅ *"merged"* /mɜːrdʒd/. Short and ending cleanly on /d/.

**Sound 5 — *deployed* = /dɪˈplɔɪd/** ⚠️
Stress on the second syllable: *di-PLOYD*. The diphthong /ɔɪ/ is similar to *"oi"* in Portuguese (*"foi"*, *"poi"*) — Brazilians usually get this right. Watch the stress placement.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
A voz passiva no inglês diz que algo **acontece a** alguém ou alguma coisa, em vez de dizer **quem** faz a ação. Para o presente, a estrutura é **is/are + particípio passado**: *"The code is reviewed"* (O código é revisado). Para o passado, é **was/were + particípio passado**: *"The bug was fixed"* (O bug foi corrigido). Usamos a voz passiva quando o agente (quem faz a ação) é desconhecido, óbvio ou irrelevante — é a linguagem padrão de documentação técnica, post-mortems, changelogs e especificações de engenharia. Se precisar mencionar quem fez a ação, use *"by"*: *"The architecture was approved by the CTO."*

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais comum é usar o verbo no infinitivo em vez do particípio: ❌ *"The PR was merge"* → ✅ *"The PR was merged"*. A estrutura é sempre *was/were + particípio passado*, não o verbo base. Outro erro frequente é confundir quem é o sujeito: ❌ *"The engineer was deployed the app"* → ✅ *"The app was deployed by the engineer"* (o sujeito da voz passiva é o objeto da voz ativa). Atenção também à concordância: sujeito singular → *was/is*, sujeito plural → *were/are*. Finalmente, a pronúncia dos particípios em *-ed* tem três formas: */d/* em *reviewed*, */t/* em *checked*, e */ɪd/* em *tested* e *rejected* — vale praticar separadamente.
