## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **One-on-one — engineering manager check-in:**
>
> **Manager:** You've been the tech lead on the platform team for a while now, right? How long have you been leading the squad?
>
> **Levi:** I've been in this role for about eighteen months — since we reorganised after the Series B.
>
> **Manager:** And how long has the observability stack been in production?
>
> **Levi:** We've had it running since March. It's been stable, but we haven't finished migrating all the legacy services yet.
>
> **Manager:** How long have you known about the migration gap?
>
> **Levi:** Since Q1. It's on the backlog — we've been prioritising the reliability work.

Notice the pattern: every question asks *from when* or *how long*, and every answer uses **for** or **since**. The situation started in the past and is **still true right now**.

### The rule

Use the **present perfect** (or **present perfect continuous**) with **for** and **since** to talk about something that **started in the past and continues up to now**.

| Word | What it takes | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **for** | a period/duration | for 18 months · for three sprints · for a few weeks · for ages |
| **since** | a point in time | since March · since 2022 · since the last reorg · since I joined |

**Two valid structures:**

1. **have + past participle** — stresses the *state* or *fact*
   - *"I have worked here for two years."* (I still work here)
   - *"She has been the engineering director since 2023."*

2. **have been + verb-ing** — stresses the *ongoing activity/duration*
   - *"I have been working on this RFC for three weeks."* (still working)
   - *"We've been running the beta since January."*

Both are correct. The continuous form is very natural when you want to emphasise how long something has been in progress.

**Asking the question:**

| | Question | Short answer |
|---|---|---|
| State | *How long **have** you **been** a Staff engineer?* | *For two years.* |
| Activity | *How long **has** the team **been** working on this?* | *Since the last sprint.* |

> ⚠️ **Key contrast — start vs. current:** compare *"I **started** this role two years ago"* (simple past — the starting moment) with *"I **have been** in this role **for** two years"* (present perfect — the continuous span up to now). Both can be true at the same time; they frame the same fact differently.

> ⚠️ **For Portuguese speakers:** *"Há quanto tempo você…?"* maps to *"How long have you…?"*, not *"How long are you…?"*. English requires the present perfect here — never the present simple or continuous. *"How long are you working here?"* ❌ → *"How long **have you been** working here?"* ✅

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"How long have you been the Staff engineer on this team? — For about a year, since the last promotion cycle."** *(tech leadership — role duration)*
2. **"We've been running the new inference pipeline in production for three weeks. Latency is down 40%."** *(AI/engineering — ongoing deployment)*
3. **"How long has the security incident been open? — Since Friday. The on-call has been investigating for 72 hours."** *(engineering — incident management)*
4. **"I've had this architectural concern for two sprints — it keeps coming up in code review. We need to address it."** *(engineering — technical debt awareness)*
5. **"She has been leading the AI platform initiative since Q1. The team has grown from 3 to 11 engineers."** *(tech leadership — organisational fact)*
6. **"How long have you known about this dependency issue? — Since the last sprint review."** *(engineering — timeline accountability)*
7. **"I've lived in São Paulo for most of my life, but I've been working remotely since 2020."** *(everyday life — personal history)*
8. **"He has had that guitar for fifteen years. He's been playing since he was twelve."** *(everyday life — possessions and hobbies)*

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Fill in the blanks with **for**, **since**, **have/has**, or the correct form of the verb in brackets:

*"How long \_\_\_ the team \_\_\_ (work) on the new data platform? — They \_\_\_ been working on it \_\_\_ six months — \_\_\_ the original timeline was approved in Q4 last year. The lead engineer \_\_\_ (not / finish) the schema migration \_\_\_ then, but progress has been solid."*

✅ **Answer:**

*"How long **has** the team **been working** on the new data platform? — They **have** been working on it **for** six months — **since** the original timeline was approved in Q4 last year. The lead engineer **hasn't finished** the schema migration **since** then, but progress has been solid."*

### Production task

*Write 2 sentences about your current role or a current project: one using "I've been… for…" and one using "We've had / We've been… since…".*

**Model answer:**
1. *"I've been working on the AI-assisted code review tool for the past two sprints — it's starting to show real value in the review cycle."*
2. *"We've been running the feature flag infrastructure since the platform migration in Q3 — it's now a core part of how we ship safely."*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **for** and **since** as answering two different questions:

> **for** → *"How much time?"* → a **quantity** → *for 3 months, for ages, for the whole quarter*
> **since** → *"From which moment?"* → a **timestamp** → *since Monday, since I joined, since 2022*

A quick self-check: can you replace the word with a duration (*three months*) or a date/event (*January*)? If duration → **for**. If date/event → **since**.

And remember: **never use present simple or continuous for duration up to now.** If your sentence has "for" or "since" and the situation is still ongoing, you almost certainly need the present perfect: *"I work here for two years"* ❌ → *"I **have worked** here for two years"* ✅

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **present perfect com for e since** é usado para descrever situações que **começaram no passado e ainda continuam até agora**. A estrutura é **have/has + particípio passado** (para estados e fatos) ou **have/has been + verbo-ing** (para enfatizar a duração de uma atividade em andamento). A palavra **for** é seguida de um **período de tempo** (*for three months, for two years, for ages*), enquanto **since** é seguida de um **ponto específico no tempo** (*since Monday, since 2022, since I joined the team*). A pergunta "How long have you (been)...?" equivale em português a "Há quanto tempo você...?".

**Atenção para falantes de português:** o erro mais comum é usar o presente simples com "for" ou "since" no lugar do present perfect. Em inglês, *"I work here for two years"* está **errado** — o correto é *"I have worked here for two years"* ou *"I've been working here for two years"*. Outro ponto importante: não confunda **for** e **since**. Se consegue substituir a palavra por uma duração (*três meses*), use **for**. Se consegue substituir por um momento ou data (*desde janeiro*), use **since**. Praticando essa distinção, você vai internalizá-la rapidamente — especialmente no contexto profissional, onde frases como *"I've been in this role for…"* ou *"We've been running this system since…"* aparecem o tempo todo.
