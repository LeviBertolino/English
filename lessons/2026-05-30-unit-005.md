## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **A Slack thread in a tech team:**
>
> **Ana (Staff Engineer):** Hey Levi — quick question for the new hire doc. Where does the backend team work from?
>
> **Levi:** Most of us **work** remotely. We **meet** in person once a quarter. Marcus **lives** in São Paulo, so he **joins** the São Paulo hub a few days a week. Priya and I **work** from home full-time.
>
> **Ana:** And what does the deployment process look like?
>
> **Levi:** The pipeline **runs** automated tests first. Then a senior engineer **reviews** the diff and **approves** it. After that, the system **deploys** automatically. We **don't merge** anything without a review.
>
> **Ana:** Great. Does the team use feature flags?
>
> **Levi:** Yes — we **use** feature flags for everything. It **makes** rollbacks much safer.

You already know the present continuous (*I am doing*). Now look at the verbs above: *work, meet, lives, joins, runs, reviews, deploys* — none of them are happening **right now** in that moment. They describe **how things generally are** — habits, routines, permanent truths.

That's the **present simple**.

### The rule: present simple for general truths and habits

Use the **present simple** (not continuous) when you describe:

- **Habits and routines** — things that happen regularly: *"I review PRs every morning."*
- **General facts and permanent situations** — things that are always or usually true: *"The pipeline runs automated tests."*
- **Schedules and timetables** (fixed, institutional) — different from personal arrangements: *"The standup starts at 10."*

### Formation

| Subject | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | **work** | **don't work** | **Do** you work? |
| he / she / it | **works** | **doesn't work** | **Does** she work? |

**Adding -s / -es for he/she/it:**

| Base form | He/She/It form | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| work | work**s** | most verbs: add -s |
| review | review**s** | most verbs: add -s |
| deploy | deploy**s** | most verbs: add -s |
| teach | teach**es** | -ch, -sh, -s, -x, -z: add -es |
| go | go**es** | -o: usually add -es |
| have | ha**s** | irregular — memorize |

**Negative and question forms:**
- Always use **don't** (I/you/we/they) or **doesn't** (he/she/it) + base form (no -s on the main verb when negating).
- ❌ *"She doesn't works here."* → ✅ *"She doesn't work here."*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"Our CI pipeline runs the full test suite on every pull request."** — tech, general fact
2. **"The team uses feature flags to decouple deployments from releases."** — tech, habit/practice
3. **"I review at least three PRs a day — it's part of my routine as Staff engineer."** — tech, habit
4. **"She doesn't attend sprint planning — she leads the architecture forum instead."** — tech, habit (negative)
5. **"Does the system automatically roll back failed deployments?"** — tech, general question
6. **"Our AI model updates its weights every time new data comes in."** — tech, general fact
7. **"I drink coffee before I open my laptop in the morning."** — everyday, habit
8. **"He doesn't eat lunch at his desk — he takes a proper break every day."** — everyday, habit (negative)

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"She doesn't works on Fridays."** → ✅ **"She doesn't work on Fridays."**
*Why: with "doesn't," the main verb goes back to its base form — no -s. The -s is already carried by "doesn't" (does + n't).*

❌ **"The pipeline is running tests on every PR."** (as a permanent fact) → ✅ **"The pipeline runs tests on every PR."**
*Why: this is a permanent setup, not something temporarily in progress right now. General facts about systems → present simple. Save the continuous for when you want to highlight something in progress at this moment.*

❌ **"Do she review the code?"** → ✅ **"Does she review the code?"**
*Why: he/she/it needs "does" in questions — not "do." And the main verb stays in base form: no -s after "does."*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**Present simple = how life is in general.**

Think of it as a photograph of your normal life — not what's happening *right now* in this exact moment, but what's true **in general**, day after day.

- *"I drink coffee in the morning."* (That's your life, your habit.)
- *"I'm drinking coffee."* (You're holding a mug right now.)

A quick mental test: *"Is this a general fact or something happening right now / temporarily?"*
- General / habit / always true → **present simple**: *I review PRs. The system deploys at midnight.*
- Right now / temporary / arranged → **present continuous**: *I'm reviewing this PR. The system is deploying.*

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| works | /wɜːrks/ | one syllable — the -s is /s/ after unvoiced /k/ ⚠️ |
| reviews | /rɪˈvjuːz/ | the -s is /z/ after voiced /v/ — *"re-VYOOZ"* ⚠️ |
| deploys | /dɪˈplɔɪz/ | -s is /z/ after a vowel sound ⚠️ |
| doesn't | /ˈdʌzənt/ | 2 syllables: DUZ-nt — NOT "doz-ent" ⚠️ |
| don't | /doʊnt/ | diphthong /oʊ/ — one syllable |
| runs | /rʌnz/ | short /ʌ/ — "run" rhymes with "fun" ⚠️ |
| uses | /ˈjuːzɪz/ | 3 syllables: YOO-ziz — not "YOO-ses" ⚠️ |
| teaches | /ˈtiːtʃɪz/ | 2 syllables for the -es ending ⚠️ |
| aggregates | /ˈæɡrɪɡeɪts/ | 4 syllables: AG-ri-gate-s |
| routine | /ruːˈtiːn/ | stress on 2nd syllable: roo-TEEN |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Third-person -s** ⚠️: The -s on he/she/it is **not always /s/**. It follows a voicing rule:
  - After voiceless sounds (p, k, t, f): → /s/ — *stops, works, writes*
  - After voiced sounds (b, d, g, v, l, m, n, r, all vowels): → /z/ — *reviews /rɪˈvjuːz/, runs /rʌnz/, deploys /dɪˈplɔɪz/*
  - After /s/, /z/, /ʃ/, /tʃ/: → /ɪz/ (extra syllable) — *uses /ˈjuːzɪz/, teaches /ˈtiːtʃɪz/*
  Brazilians often pronounce the -s too strongly or as /s/ every time. Listen to native speakers and match the voicing.
- **"doesn't"** ⚠️: Brazilians often say *"doz-ent"* (influenced by Portuguese spelling). The vowel is a short /ʌ/: **DUZ-nt**. Two syllables, fast.
- **"routine"** ⚠️: In Portuguese, *rotina* is stressed on the second syllable too — this one carries over! *roo-TEEN*. Good news.
- **"don't"** ⚠️: The /oʊ/ diphthong doesn't exist in Brazilian Portuguese. Avoid reducing it to a plain /o/ (like Portuguese *"dont"*). Open it up: starts with /o/, glides to /ʊ/ — *"DOH-nt"*.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **presente simples (1)** descreve hábitos, rotinas e fatos gerais — coisas que são verdadeiras em geral, não necessariamente acontecendo agora mesmo. Em inglês, o presente simples é a forma base do verbo: *I work, you use, we deploy, they review*. Para **he/she/it**, adicionamos **-s** (ou **-es** em verbos terminados em -ch, -sh, -s, -x, -z) — isso é obrigatório e um dos erros mais frequentes de falantes de português, pois em português o verbo não muda da mesma forma. Exemplo: *"The pipeline **runs** tests automatically"* — não "run".

Para **negativas** e **perguntas**, usamos os auxiliares **don't** (I/you/we/they) ou **doesn't** (he/she/it), e o verbo principal volta para a forma base — **sem o -s**: *"She **doesn't work** on Fridays"* (não "doesn't works"); *"**Does** the system deploy automatically?"* (não "Does it deploys"). O contraste principal com a aula de ontem: se é um fato geral ou hábito → presente simples; se é algo temporário, em andamento, ou compromisso futuro combinado → presente contínuo. *"I work remotely"* (fato permanente) vs. *"I'm working from São Paulo this week"* (situação temporária).
