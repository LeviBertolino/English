## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Recruiter:** Do you work with distributed systems?
> **Levi:** Yes, I do — mostly event-driven architectures.
> **Recruiter:** Does your team own the infrastructure, or does another team handle that?
> **Levi:** The SRE team owns infra. We don't manage servers directly.
> **Recruiter:** Do your engineers collaborate with the data science team?
> **Levi:** We do, yeah. Does the role involve cross-functional work?
> **Recruiter:** It does. The position reports into the AI platform org.

Notice *Do you work...?*, *Does your team own...?*, *Do your engineers collaborate...?*, *Does the role involve...?* — all present simple questions using *do* or *does* as the helper.

### The rule

To form a **present simple question**, bring the auxiliary in front of the subject:

| Subject | Question form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | **Do** + subject + base verb? | *Do you own the repo?* |
| he / she / it | **Does** + subject + base verb? | *Does she lead the team?* |

**Key points:**
- The main verb stays in **base form** — never *"Does she leads?"*
- Short answers: *Yes, I do. / No, I don't. / Yes, she does. / No, she doesn't.*
- Wh-questions follow the same pattern: *What do you use for logging? / Where does the pipeline run?*
- *Do/does* carries the grammar marker so the main verb never needs -s in questions.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **Do you use feature flags** for gradual rollouts, or do you deploy straight to all users?
2. **Does the CI pipeline run** integration tests before every merge, or only on the main branch?
3. **Does your team follow** a trunk-based development workflow?
4. **Do your engineers pair-program** on complex features, or do they prefer async code review?
5. **Does the data model support** multi-tenancy, or is it single-tenant by design?
6. **Do you speak** to stakeholders directly, or does your PM handle those conversations?
7. **Does she commute** to the office every day, or does she work fully remote?
8. **Do you drink** coffee before the morning standup?

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"Does she leads the backend team?"** → ✅ **"Does she lead the backend team?"**
*Why: After "does", the verb must be the base form. "Does" already signals third-person singular — the verb doesn't need to repeat it.*

❌ **"Do the system supports autoscaling?"** → ✅ **"Does the system support autoscaling?"**
*Why: "The system" = it → use "does", not "do". And the verb stays in base form.*

❌ **"She works at Anthropic?"** *(as a written professional question)* → ✅ **"Does she work at Anthropic?"**
*Why: Rising intonation on a statement works in casual spoken English. In professional writing — emails, Slack to a manager, interview questions — always form the question properly with do/does.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **do/does as a spotlight** 🔦: it jumps in front of the sentence to signal "This is a question!" The main verb stays in the shade — base form only, no -s allowed.

If the subject is he/she/it, the spotlight is **does**. Anything else, the spotlight is **do**. The spotlight takes the grammar marker so the verb never has to.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and phrases

| Word/Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Do you | /də jə/ (casual) / /duː juː/ (clear) | Reduces to "d'ya" in fast speech ⚠️ |
| Does she | /dʌz ʃiː/ | /ʌ/ like "bus" — not /doʊz/ ⚠️ |
| Does it support | /ˈdʌz ɪt səˈpɔːrt/ | Stress falls on "support", not "does" |
| Do you use | /də jə juːz/ | Three words often blend to two syllables in casual speech |
| Yes, I do | /jɛs aɪ duː/ | "Do" gets full stress in short answers |
| No, she doesn't | /noʊ ʃiː ˈdʌzənt/ | Reinforcing yesterday's sound |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"Do you"** in casual speech: native speakers reduce this to /də jə/ — almost *"d'ya"*. Brazilians often over-articulate to /duː juː/. Both are understood, but the reduced form sounds far more natural. ⚠️
- **"Does"** as auxiliary: the vowel is /ʌ/ (as in *bus*), not /oʊ/. *"DOHZ your team use it?"* → *"DUZ your team use it?"* This is the same vowel as yesterday's *"doesn't"* — /ˈdʌzənt/. ⚠️
- **Yes/no questions → rising intonation**: *"Do you work with Kubernetes?"* — pitch rises at the end. Brazilians usually do this instinctively.
- **Wh-questions → falling intonation**: *"What do you use for logging?"* — pitch falls at the end. This is the opposite of Portuguese wh-questions for some Brazilian dialects, so it can feel backwards. ⚠️

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **presente simples em forma de pergunta** usa *do* ou *does* como auxiliar antes do sujeito: *Do you use Terraform? / Does the system support autoscaling?* A regra de ouro é a mesma do negativo (Unit 006): depois de *does*, o verbo fica na forma base — nunca *"Does she leads?"*. Isso confunde porque em português a pergunta é formada pela entonação ou pela inversão da ordem, sem precisar de um auxiliar. Em inglês, o *do/does* é obrigatório em perguntas formais e na escrita.

Fique atento à pronúncia: *"do you"* em fala natural vira /də jə/ — quase *"d'ya"* — e *"does"* tem o som /ʌ/, não /oʊ/ (o mesmo som de ontem em *"doesn't"*). A entonação das perguntas sim/não sobe no final; as perguntas com *wh-* (what, where, who) descem. Se o *doesn't* ainda estava escorregando ontem, observe que a lógica é idêntica aqui: *does* já carrega a marca de terceira pessoa, então o verbo fica intocado.
