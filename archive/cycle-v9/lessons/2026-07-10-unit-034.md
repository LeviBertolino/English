# Unit 034 — Should (1)

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday was **Unit 033 — Must and Have to (Obligation)**, where Levi told a new hire: *"You **have to** acknowledge every PagerDuty alert within 5 minutes — that's the team's SLA,"* and *"You **mustn't** silence an alert without writing a note in the channel."*

Three rapid recall questions:

1. Which word describes an obligation coming from an outside rule or policy — **must** or **have to**?
2. Fix the mistake: *"You don't have to push to `main` directly."* (meaning it's forbidden, not optional)
3. What's the past-tense form of **must** for obligation?

👉 **Answers:**
1. ✅ **have to** — an external rule/policy (like an SLA). **must** is the obligation the speaker feels personally.
2. ✅ *"You **mustn't** push to `main` directly."* — forbidden needs **mustn't**, not **don't have to** (which means optional).
3. ✅ **had to** — *"I had to redeploy twice yesterday."* (there's no past form of *must*).

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Levi, reviewing a teammate's pull request:**
>
> **Levi:** This works, but I think you **should** extract that validation logic into its own function — it'll make the diff easier to review next time.
> **Teammate:** Fair. Should I also add a test for the edge case, or is that overkill?
> **Levi:** You **should** add one, yeah — it's a cheap way to prevent regressions. You **shouldn't** merge without at least one test on a change like this.
> **Teammate:** Got it, I'll push an update in a few minutes.

Notice:
- **should** is used to give an **opinion, recommendation, or advice** — it's not a rule and not a hard obligation. It's what the speaker thinks is the *right* or *sensible* thing to do.
- Unlike **must/have to** (Unit 033), **should** doesn't say something is required — it says it's *advisable*.
- **shouldn't** = the negative — advice against doing something.

### The rule

| Form | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **should** + base verb | opinion / advice / recommendation | You **should** back up the database before running the migration. |
| **shouldn't** + base verb | advice against something | You **shouldn't** ignore linter warnings. |
| **I think ... should** | softened opinion | I think we **should** postpone the launch. |
| **Should I/we ...?** | asking for advice | **Should** I loop in the security team on this? |

> ⚠️ **should** is much weaker than **must/have to**. *"You should write tests"* = good idea; *"You have to write tests"* = required by policy. Portuguese *"deveria"* maps well to **should**; *"tem que"* maps to **have to**. Mixing them up makes advice sound like a hard rule, or a rule sound like a casual suggestion.
> ⚠️ **ought to** means almost the same as **should**, but is less common in everyday speech and sounds a bit more formal: *"We ought to document this decision."*
> ⚠️ **should** is also used for expectation (*"The build should finish in 5 minutes"*), a different sense we'll meet again later — today's focus is advice/opinion.

## 3. ✏️ 8 example sentences

1. You **should** add more context to that commit message — it's hard to follow right now.
2. I think we **should** roll this out to 10% of traffic before a full release.
3. The team **shouldn't** skip the retro just because the sprint went well.
4. You **should** ask the Staff engineer to review the architecture doc before you build it.
5. She **should** get more sleep before her big presentation tomorrow.
6. You **shouldn't** leave your laptop unlocked in a coffee shop.
7. We **should** call a plumber — that leak isn't going to fix itself.
8. You **should** try the new restaurant downtown; the reviews are great.

## 5. 💡 Memory tip

**SHOULD = a friendly nudge, not an order.** Picture a colleague tapping your shoulder (should ≈ "shoulder") and quietly suggesting a better path — they're not blocking your merge, just offering advice you're free to weigh.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Hoje vimos **should**, usado para dar **opinião, recomendação ou conselho** — bem mais leve do que **must/have to** (Unit 033), que expressam obrigação. **Should** é o que o falante considera a atitude sensata ou correta, mas não uma regra obrigatória; **shouldn't** é o conselho contrário, dizendo que algo não é uma boa ideia.

O cuidado principal é não confundir a força de **should** com a de **must/have to**: *"deveria"* (should) é uma sugestão que a pessoa pode ou não seguir, enquanto *"tem que"* (have to) é uma exigência real. **Ought to** tem o mesmo sentido de **should**, mas soa mais formal e é menos comum na fala do dia a dia.
