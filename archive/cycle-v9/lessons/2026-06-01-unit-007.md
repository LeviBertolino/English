# Unit 007 — Do you...? (present simple questions)

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson: **Unit 006 — I don't... (present simple negative)** — building negatives with *don't* and *doesn't*, and the rule that the main verb always drops its -s after the auxiliary.

Three rapid recall questions:

1. From yesterday's examples: *"The alerting system \_\_\_ alert on every error — only on anomalies above the threshold."* — *don't* or *doesn't*?
2. ❌ *"She doesn't works on the backend team."* — What's the fix and why?
3. Yesterday's memory tip: *"Doesn't steals the -s."* What does that mean in practice for the sentence *"The pipeline runs → The pipeline \_\_\_"*?

👉 **Answers:**
1. **doesn't** — "the alerting system" = it → third person singular → *doesn't*.
2. ✅ *"She doesn't work on the backend team."* — *doesn't* already carries the third-person -s signal, so the main verb returns to base form. Double -s (*doesn't works*) is never correct.
3. *"The pipeline **doesn't run**."* — The -s jumped from *run**s*** to *doe**s**n't*. One signal, one location.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack message thread — onboarding a new Staff engineer:**
>
> **Carlos (new hire):** Hey Levi — quick questions while I get set up. **Do** you normally **run** a weekly architecture sync?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, I do — every Tuesday at 10. **Do** the engineers **attend** in person or async?
>
> **Carlos:** Mix of both. **Does** your team **use** ADRs for architectural decisions?
>
> **Levi:** We do. **Does** the platform team **review** them, or just the backend guild?
>
> **Carlos:** Both, usually. **Do** you **document** the decisions in Confluence or somewhere else?
>
> **Levi:** We keep them in the repo — closer to the code. **Does** that work for you?

Notice the question pattern: **Do** (I/you/we/they) or **Does** (he/she/it) at the start, then the subject, then the **base form** of the verb. Exactly like negatives — the auxiliary carries the signal, the main verb stays bare.

### The rule: present simple questions

To form a yes/no question in the present simple, move the auxiliary **do** or **does** to the front:

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| You use ADRs. | **Do** you use ADRs? |
| She reviews PRs. | **Does** she review PRs? |
| They run daily standups. | **Do** they run daily standups? |
| The system deploys at midnight. | **Does** the system deploy at midnight? |

### Wh- questions (information questions)

Add a question word before *do* / *does*:

| Question word | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| What | What **do** you + verb? | *What do you use for observability?* |
| Where | Where **does** she + verb? | *Where does she work?* |
| How often | How often **do** they + verb? | *How often do they release?* |
| Why | Why **does** it + verb? | *Why does it fail on every deploy?* |
| Who | Who **do** you + verb? | *Who do you report to?* |

### Short answers

| Question | Short answer (yes) | Short answer (no) |
|---|---|---|
| Do you use Linear? | Yes, **I do**. | No, **I don't**. |
| Does she own the frontend? | Yes, **she does**. | No, **she doesn't**. |
| Do they use microservices? | Yes, **they do**. | No, **they don't**. |

Never repeat the main verb in a short answer — *"Yes, I do use."* ❌ Just the auxiliary: *"Yes, I do."* ✅

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"Do you review infrastructure changes before they hit production?"** — tech, yes/no question (you)
2. **"Does your observability platform integrate with PagerDuty?"** — tech, yes/no question (it)
3. **"How often do they push to production — daily or weekly?"** — tech, wh-question (they)
4. **"Does the CI pipeline run integration tests on every commit?"** — tech, yes/no question (it)
5. **"What do you look for first when triaging an incident?"** — tech, wh-question (you)
6. **"Do Staff engineers at your company write code, or mostly lead design reviews?"** — tech, yes/no question (they)
7. **"How do you usually prepare for a one-on-one?"** — everyday/work, wh-question (you)
8. **"Does he commute to the office, or does he work fully remote?"** — everyday, yes/no question (he)

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"Does she reviews the PRs?"** → ✅ **"Does she review the PRs?"**
*Why: does already carries the third-person signal. The main verb must be base form — no -s. Same rule as negatives.*

❌ **"Do the system deploys automatically?"** → ✅ **"Does the system deploy automatically?"**
*Why: "the system" = it → third person singular → use does, not do. And lose the -s on deploy.*

❌ **"Yes, I do use it."** → ✅ **"Yes, I do."**
*Why: short answers use only the auxiliary. Repeating the main verb sounds unnatural and redundant in English. "Yes, I do" is complete.*

## 5. 🛠️ Practice

### Recognition — gap-fill

Complete the blanks with *do*, *does*, *don't*, or *doesn't*:

> *"\_\_\_ your team run a post-mortem after every incident? — Yes, we \_\_\_. \_\_\_ the on-call rotation change every week? — No, it \_\_\_ — it rotates monthly. And who \_\_\_ the incident report go to?"*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **Do** — "your team" → you → *do*
2. **do** — short answer matching the question auxiliary
3. **Does** — "the on-call rotation" = it → *does*
4. **doesn't** — short negative answer → *doesn't*
5. **does** — wh-question with "the incident report" (it) → *does*

1. One yes/no question with **Do** or **Does**.

Say both out loud, focusing on the ⚠️ sounds in section 7.

**Model answer:**

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**"Do/Does = detective at the door."**

In a statement, the auxiliary hides inside the verb: *she work**s***.
To ask a question, the detective (do/does) steps to the front of the sentence, takes the -s with it, and interrogates the subject:

> *She work**s**.*
> → *DOES she work?* ← detective at the door, -s in hand.

If you need *does*, the main verb loses its -s (the detective took it). If you use *do*, there was no -s to begin with.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| do you | /duː juː/ | in fast speech: /dʒuː/ — "dyoo" ⚠️ |
| does | /dʌz/ | short /ʌ/ — NOT /doʊz/ ⚠️ |
| doesn't | /ˈdʌzənt/ | same as yesterday: **DUZ**-nt ⚠️ |
| do you use | /duː juː juːz/ | linking: "doo-yoo-yooz" — 3 syllables ⚠️ |
| does it deploy | /dʌz ɪt dɪˈplɔɪ/ | schwa on unstressed *it*: /ɪt/ |
| review | /rɪˈvjuː/ | stress on 2nd syllable: ri-VYOO |
| integrate | /ˈɪntɪɡreɪt/ | stress on 1st syllable: IN-ti-grayt |
| prioritize | /praɪˈɒrɪtaɪz/ | 4 syllables: pry-OR-i-tize ⚠️ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"does" vowel** ⚠️: /ʌ/ again — the same sound as in *doesn't* from yesterday. *Does* is /dʌz/, not /doʊz/ (which would sound like the plural of *doe*, the female deer). Practice: open your mouth, tongue low and center, say "duz" — not "dohz."
- **"do you" in connected speech** ⚠️: When *do* and *you* are spoken together naturally, the /j/ sound absorbs into the /d/ and becomes a soft /dʒ/ sound — like the start of *just*. So *"do you work?"* sounds like *"dyoo work?"* Brazilians often over-articulate each word separately, which sounds unnatural. Blend them.
- **"review"** ⚠️: The diphthong /juː/ at the end — not Portuguese "reviu." Both syllables need clear stress: ri-**VYOO**. Extend the /uː/ a little.
- **"prioritize"** ⚠️: Four syllables, stress on second: *pry-OR-i-tize*. Brazilians often stress the first syllable (*PRI-ori-tize*) following Portuguese rhythm. Shift the stress: the OR is the loud one.

3. *What do you prioritize in a code review?* ⚠️ — /wɒt duː juː praɪˈɒrɪtaɪz/
4. *Does she review infrastructure changes?* ⚠️ — /dʌz ʃiː rɪˈvjuː/

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Para fazer perguntas no **presente simples**, usamos o auxiliar **do** (I/you/we/they) ou **does** (he/she/it) no início da frase, seguido do sujeito e do verbo na **forma base** — sem o -s. Exemplo: *"She review**s**"* → *"**Does** she review?"* (não *"Does she reviews?"*). O mesmo princípio da aula anterior: o auxiliar carrega a marcação de terceira pessoa, então o verbo principal fica limpo. Para perguntas com pronome interrogativo (wh-questions), basta adicionar a palavra antes do auxiliar: *"What do you use?"*, *"How does it work?"*, *"Where do they deploy?"*. Nas respostas curtas, nunca repita o verbo principal — *"Yes, I do"* é completo; *"Yes, I do use"* soa estranho.

Na pronúncia, dois pontos críticos: **"does"** é /dʌz/ — mesma vogal /ʌ/ de *doesn't* de ontem, não /doʊz/; e a sequência **"do you"** na fala fluente se transforma em /dʒuː/, quase como o início de *"just"* — então *"do you work?"* soa como *"dyoo work?"*. Articular cada palavra separadamente soa robótico. Para perguntas com pronomes interrogativos, note o acento em **"prioritize"** (pry-**OR**-i-tize, não **PRI**-oriti) e em **"review"** (ri-**VYOO**, não **RE**-viu). Essas perguntas são ferramentas diárias de liderança técnica — *design reviews*, *1:1s*, *incident debriefs* — então vale dominar a forma com confiança.
