## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Engineering blog — Levi's post on Staff engineering:**
>
> *"A lot of people ask me what a Staff engineer actually does all day. Here's the honest answer: I spend about 40% of my time in cross-team conversations — aligning stakeholders, unblocking decisions, translating business context into technical direction. I also write a lot: RFCs, architecture docs, strategy memos. I don't write much production code anymore, but I review it constantly. My team works across three time zones, so I start early and I rely heavily on async communication. Engineers at this level don't just solve technical problems — they shape how problems are framed."*

Read that again. Notice: *"I spend"*, *"I write"*, *"I don't write"*, *"I review"*, *"I start"*, *"I rely"*, *"they shape"*. None of these are happening at this exact second. They describe **what is generally true, what Levi does regularly, what his job looks like in general**.

That's the present simple — today's unit.

### The rule

The **present simple** describes:

1. **Things that are generally or permanently true** — facts, states, habits, repeated actions.
2. **How often something happens** (always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never).

It is NOT about what's happening right now (that's present continuous from Units 3–4).

#### Form

| Subject | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | **work** | **don't** work | **Do** you work…? |
| he / she / it | **works** | **doesn't** work | **Does** she work…? |

> ⚠️ Third-person singular (he/she/it) always adds **-s** or **-es**: *work → works*, *go → goes*, *watch → watches*, *study → studies* (y → ies).

#### The invisible frequency

Even when there's no "always/usually/often" in the sentence, the present simple implies regularity:

> *"I review pull requests."* → This is something I do regularly — it's part of my job.
> *"She leads the platform team."* → This is her role, not just what she's doing right now.

The question to ask: **"Is this a general truth or repeated pattern?"** → Present simple.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"A Staff engineer aligns stakeholders before major architectural decisions."** — general truth, tech leadership
2. **"Our team ships code twice a week — we use trunk-based development."** — regular habit, software engineering
3. **"She doesn't attend sprint ceremonies; she focuses on cross-team strategy instead."** — what's generally true, engineering manager
4. **"I review every RFC that touches the data layer — it's part of my accountability."** — regular practice, Staff engineering
5. **"Do you use feature flags for every release? Our team does, even for small changes."** — habitual practice, DevOps
6. **"The AI model doesn't hallucinate much on structured data, but it struggles with ambiguous prompts."** — general truth, AI/ML
7. **"My daughter starts school at 8am, so I never schedule meetings before 9."** — personal habit, real-life context
8. **"He reads engineering papers on Sunday mornings — it's how he stays current."** — regular habit, everyday life

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Read the Slack message below. Three blanks: fill with the correct present simple form (positive, negative, or question).

> *"Hey — quick context for the new folks. Our team \_\_\_ (work) in two-week sprints. We \_\_\_ (not / use) Jira — we prefer Linear. \_\_\_ everyone \_\_\_ (do) async stand-ups? Yes, we post updates by 10am every day."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **works** — third-person singular (our team = it) → add -s.
2. **don't use** — subject "we" → don't + base verb.
3. **Does** / **do** — question with "everyone" (treated as singular) → Does everyone do…?

### Production task

*Write 2 sentences about your regular work practices or team habits using the present simple.*
1. One sentence about something **you** do regularly (positive or negative).
2. One sentence about something **your team or a colleague** does regularly (use he/she/it → remember the -s).

**Model answer:**
1. *"I don't block calendar time for deep work — I probably should, but I rely on early mornings instead."*
2. *"Our engineering director reviews the quarterly OKRs every Friday and adjusts priorities before the following sprint."*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**The "job description" test:**

> If you could put the sentence into a job description or a Wikipedia-style fact, it belongs in the **present simple**.

- *"A Staff engineer aligns stakeholders."* ✅ (could be in a job description)
- *"She is aligning stakeholders right now."* → present continuous (happening at this moment)
- *"AI models process natural language."* ✅ (general fact)
- *"The model is processing your request."* → continuous (happening right now)

And don't forget the **-s** trap: every time the subject is *he, she, it* (or any singular noun like *"the team", "our system", "the model"*), you must add **-s** to the verb. This is the single most common error Brazilian learners make in the present simple.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **presente simples** (present simple) em inglês é usado para descrever **coisas que são verdadeiras em geral**, **hábitos**, **rotinas** e **ações que se repetem regularmente** — não o que está acontecendo agora (esse é o presente contínuo das Unidades 003 e 004). A estrutura é simples: **sujeito + verbo na forma base**, exceto na terceira pessoa do singular (he, she, it, ou qualquer substantivo singular como "the team", "the system"), que recebe **-s** ou **-es** no verbo: *I work / she works, I go / he goes, I watch / it watches*. A negativa usa **don't** (para I/you/we/they) ou **doesn't** (para he/she/it), e o verbo principal volta à forma base: *"She doesn't write code anymore"*. Perguntas usam **do** ou **does** no início: *"Does your team use Linear?"*.

O erro mais comum para falantes de português é **esquecer o -s na terceira pessoa do singular** — em português não existe essa variação no presente, então parece desnecessário. Mas em inglês é obrigatório: *"The model works well"*, *"He reviews PRs every day"*. O teste prático: se a frase descreve algo que faz parte de uma rotina, um papel ou uma verdade geral (como uma descrição de cargo ou um fato sobre como o sistema funciona), use o presente simples — não o contínuo.
