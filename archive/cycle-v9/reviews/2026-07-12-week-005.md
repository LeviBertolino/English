## Recap

- **Unit 030 — May and Might (1):** *may/might + base verb* for something possible but not certain in the present/future ("We might ship it Friday, but I'm not sure").
- **Unit 031 — May and Might (2):** *may/might + have + past participle* to guess about the past, *may/might + be + -ing* to guess about right now, and *might as well* for "no reason not to."
- **Unit 032 — Must and Can't (Certainty):** *must* = near-certain something is true; *can't* = near-certain something is **not** true — the negative twin of *must*, never *mustn't*.
- **Unit 033 — Must and Have to (Obligation):** *have to* = obligation from an outside rule/authority; *must* = obligation the speaker feels personally; *don't have to* = optional; *mustn't* = forbidden.
- **Unit 034 — Should (1):** *should/shouldn't* = opinion, advice, recommendation — much weaker than must/have to.
- **Unit 035 — Should (2):** *should have / shouldn't have + past participle* = judging a past decision with hindsight ("we should have added a canary deployment").

## 8 Mixed Exercises

**1.** (Unit 030) Fill the gap: *"The API integration ___ ___ (may / slip) to next sprint if the vendor's docs stay incomplete."*

**2.** (Unit 031) Fill the gap: *"We're not sure why the service restarted — someone ___ ___ ___ (might / restart) it manually without telling the on-call channel."*

**3.** (Unit 032) Fill the gap: *"The dashboard shows zero traffic, but support is getting complaints — it ___ ___ (can't / be) a real outage, it must be a monitoring bug."*

**4.** (Unit 033) Fix the mistake: *"You mustn't attend the postmortem if you were on PTO during the incident."* (meaning: it's optional, not forbidden)

**5.** (Unit 034) Fill the gap: *"This touches billing logic — you ___ ___ (should / request) a second reviewer before merging."*

**6.** (Unit 035) Fill the gap: *"Looking back at the outage, we ___ ___ ___ (should / add) a canary stage before the full rollout."*

**7.** Choose the correct modal: *"The client (must / might) sign the contract by Friday — sales genuinely isn't sure yet."*

**8.** True or false: *"must have"* and *"might have"* express the same level of certainty about the past.

### 👉 Answers

1. *"The API integration **may slip** to next sprint if the vendor's docs stay incomplete."*
2. *"Someone **might have restarted** it manually without telling the on-call channel."*
3. *"It **can't be** a real outage, it must be a monitoring bug."*
4. *"You **don't have to** attend the postmortem if you were on PTO during the incident."* — *mustn't* would mean it's forbidden; the intended meaning is that it's optional.
5. *"You **should request** a second reviewer before merging."*
6. *"We **should have added** a canary stage before the full rollout."*
7. **might** — sales isn't sure, so this is possibility (may/might), not near-certainty (must).
8. **False** — *must have* is a strong, evidence-based conclusion; *might have* is just one possibility among others.

## Integrative Writing Task (self-directed)

Write a short paragraph (5–7 sentences) about a recent project at work, using at least 3 of the 7 structures from this week (may/might, may/might have, might as well, must/can't, must/have to, should, should have/shouldn't have).

**Model paragraph:**

> Last month we shipped a major refactor of our checkout service. In hindsight, we **should have added** a canary deployment before the full rollout — without it, we didn't catch a subtle currency-rounding bug until Friday afternoon. When the alerts fired, the on-call engineer **must have restarted** the pods without checking the root cause first, because the error rate kept climbing right after. We're still not sure what triggered it: it **may have been** the new rounding library, or it **might be** a race condition in the cart-merge logic — nobody's confirmed it yet. Either way, we **have to** write a proper postmortem, and we **mustn't** skip the load test next time. Honestly, we **might as well** add contract tests for that service while we're at it.

## 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Essa semana o fio condutor foram os **modais de possibilidade, certeza, obrigação e conselho**. Deve estar sólido: **may/might** para possibilidade no presente/futuro (Unit 30), a extensão para o passado com **may/might + have** (Unit 31), e a diferença entre **should** (conselho leve, Unit 34) e **have to** (obrigação externa, Unit 33) — três blocos que Levi já usou naturalmente em contextos de trabalho ao longo da semana.

O que provavelmente ainda merece atenção é a palavra **must**, que carrega **dois sentidos completamente diferentes**: dedução/certeza (Unit 32 — "it must be a permissions issue") vs. obrigação pessoal (Unit 33 — "I must send that postmortem today"). Como a forma é idêntica, só o contexto separa os dois usos, e essa é a armadilha mais fácil de cair. Vale também revisar a diferença entre **should** (conselho sobre agora/futuro) e **should have** (arrependimento sobre o passado, Unit 35) — são parecidos na superfície mas apontam para momentos diferentes. Para o fim de semana, vale dar uma olhada rápida nos arquivos da **Unit 032** e da **Unit 033**, já que são os dois mais fáceis de confundir entre si.
