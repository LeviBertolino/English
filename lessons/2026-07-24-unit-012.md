### 1. 📘 Unit 012 — I didn't… / Did you…? (past simple: negatives and questions)

---

### 2. 🧠 Mini-context

> **CEO:** **Did** the board **approve** the AI budget yesterday?
> **You:** No, they **didn't**. They **didn't reject** it either — they **asked**
> for a business case first.
> **CEO:** **Did** you **speak** to the CFO afterwards?
> **You:** I **did**. She **didn't push back** on the numbers; she just **wanted**
> more detail on the ROI.

Notice the shape before any rule. Yesterday (Unit 011) the past lived **inside the
verb**: *approved, spoke, asked*. Here, the moment you ask or deny, the past jumps
onto a little helper — **did / didn't** — and the main verb snaps back to its
**base form**: *did approve*, *didn't reject*, *did speak*. The verb never carries
the past twice.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

To make a **negative** or a **question** in the past simple, you use the auxiliary
**did** (the past of *do*). **Did** already carries the past tense, so the main
verb goes back to its **base form** — no *-ed*, no irregular change.

| | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | subject + **past verb** | *We **approved** the budget.* |
| **Negative** | subject + **didn't** + **base verb** | *We **didn't approve** the budget.* |
| **Question** | **Did** + subject + **base verb**? | ***Did** we **approve** the budget?* |
| **Wh- question** | Wh- + **did** + subject + **base verb**? | *Why **did** we **approve** it?* |
| **Short answer** | Yes, … **did** / No, … **didn't** | *Yes, we **did**. / No, we **didn't**.* |

**Key facts:**
- **didn't = did not.** Same form for **every** subject — *I didn't, she didn't,
  they didn't*. No *-s*, no *doesn't* in the past.
- **The verb returns to base.** After *did / didn't*, use the plain verb: *did you
  **go**?* (not *went*), *we didn't **spend*** (not *spent*).
- **Only one carrier of the past.** The tense sits on **did**, so the main verb
  stays neutral. *Did* + *went* would mark the past twice — wrong.
- **Short answers use only did/didn't** — you don't repeat the main verb: *"Did
  the board sign off?" → "No, they didn't."*

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** English has no way to ask or deny a past action
by changing the main verb alone. It borrows a helper — **did** — to carry the
tense, which frees the main verb to stay in its simplest form. This is the same
logic as *do/does* in the present (Unit 005), just moved into the past. One helper,
one past marker, base verb underneath.

**Decision rule #1 — did (past) vs do/does (present).** The helper tells you the
time. *Do/does* = a present habit or general truth; *did* = a finished past action.
Same base verb underneath, different helper:
- *Do you **present** to the board every quarter?* → a **routine** (present). ✅
- *Did you **present** to the board **yesterday**?* → a **finished** past event. ✅
- *She **doesn't approve** early-stage bets.* → her standing rule (present). ✅
- *She **didn't approve** the bet **last week**.* → one past decision. ✅

**Decision rule #2 — did + base, never did + past.** This is the error that marks a
learner instantly. The past is already on **did**, so the main verb must be plain:
- ❌ *Did the board **approved** it?* → ✅ *Did the board **approve** it?*
- ❌ *We didn't **went** to market.* → ✅ *We didn't **go** to market.*
- The irregulars are the trap: because *go→went* feels "so past", people keep it
  after *did*. Resist it — after *did/didn't* even the wildest irregular goes home
  to base: *go, speak, spend, take, make*.

**PT→EN mapping.** Portuguese has **no auxiliary like *did***. It negates by putting
*não* straight on the verb (*"não aprovamos"*) and asks by **intonation or word
order** alone (*"Vocês aprovaram o orçamento?"* — same words as the statement, just
a rising tone). That's the root of two interference errors:
- **Denying without *didn't*:** *"We not approved the budget"* / *"We no approved"*
  → English **requires** *didn't* + base: *"We **didn't approve** the budget."*
- **Asking with only intonation:** *"The board approved the budget?"* — in
  Portuguese a rising tone is a full question; in English that's at best informal
  and often unclear. A real question needs *did*: *"**Did** the board **approve**
  the budget?"*

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *Did you **spoke** to the CFO?* → ✅ *Did you **speak** to the CFO?* — *did
  already carries the past; the main verb drops back to base "speak", never
  "spoke".*
- ❌ *We **didn't approved** the roadmap.* → ✅ *We **didn't approve** the
  roadmap.* — *after "didn't" the verb is base "approve"; the past lives on
  "didn't", not on the main verb.*
- ❌ *The board **rejected** the plan? / You **went** to the meeting?* (real
  question, only intonation) → ✅ ***Did** the board **reject** the plan? / **Did**
  you **go** to the meeting?* — *English forms past questions with "did" + base,
  not by tone alone.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. **Did** the board **approve** the AI budget, or **did** they **push** it to next
   quarter? — *both halves use "did + base" (approve, push); "did" carries the past
   for the whole question.*
2. We **didn't get** the CFO's sign-off, so the pilot is on hold. — *negative =
   "didn't" + base "get"; the past sits on "didn't", not on "got".*
3. **Why did** the transformation **slow down** last quarter? — *wh- question:
   Why + did + subject + base "slow down"; "slowed" would double the past.*
4. The board **didn't reject** the proposal — they **asked** for more detail. —
   *"didn't reject" (base after didn't) contrasts with a plain positive past
   "asked"; only the negative half takes the helper.*
5. **How much did** the team **spend** on the migration? — *irregular "spend" goes
   to base after "did"; never "did spend spent" and never "did spent".*
6. **Did** you **speak** to the vendor before you **signed** the contract? — *the
   question uses base "speak" after did; the second, positive verb keeps its past
   form "signed".*
7. I **didn't** hear back from the board — **did** they **make** a decision? — *two
   patterns: negative "didn't hear" and question "did … make", both base verbs.*
8. We **didn't go** on holiday last year, and the kids **didn't mind**. — *everyday
   life; "didn't go" (base after didn't, not "went") and "didn't mind".*

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** Para **negar** ou **perguntar** no passado simples, o
inglês usa o auxiliar **did** (o passado de *do*). Como o **did** já carrega o
passado, o verbo principal **volta à forma base**: *"We **didn't approve**"* (não
*"didn't approved"*), *"**Did** you **go**?"* (não *"did you went"*). Vale para
**todos os sujeitos** — sempre *didn't*, nunca *doesn't*, no passado. Respostas
curtas usam só *did/didn't*: *"Did the board sign off?" → "No, they didn't."* Use
*do/does* para presente/hábito e *did* para uma ação **passada e terminada**
(ontem, semana passada, no trimestre passado) — o auxiliar é que marca o tempo.

**As armadilhas.** (1) A maior: **did + verbo base**, jamais *did + passado* —
*"Did you speak?"*, nunca *"Did you spoke?"*; os irregulares (go, spend, speak) são
os que mais enganam porque "parecem passado". (2) O português nega colando *não* no
verbo e pergunta só pela entonação — em inglês isso **não basta**: é preciso
*didn't* para negar (*"We **didn't approve**"*, não *"we not approved"*) e *did*
para perguntar (*"**Did** the board **approve**?"*, não só *"The board approved?"*
com tom de pergunta). (3) Não confunda o tempo do auxiliar: *usually* pede *do/does*
(presente), *yesterday* pede *did* (passado) — o verbo principal fica igual, só o
ajudante muda.
