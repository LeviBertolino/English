## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Levi's retrospective notes from last quarter:**
>
> *"The incident response last Friday was a mess. The on-call engineer wasn't available for the first 20 minutes, and the dashboards were completely unreliable — half the alerts were false positives. We were in a bridge call for two hours. Were all the right stakeholders notified? Not initially — communications were delayed.*
>
> *The good news: the team was incredibly focused once we got everyone in the room. The root cause was a misconfigured timeout, and the fix was straightforward. Was it preventable? Absolutely."*

Notice how the whole paragraph is in the past. The backbone of it is *was* and *were* — the simple past of the verb *to be*. Let's look at the rule.

### The rule: was / were

*Was* and *were* are the **past simple** forms of *to be*.

| Subject | Positive | Negative | Contracted negative |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | **was** | was not | **wasn't** |
| he / she / it | **was** | was not | **wasn't** |
| we / you / they | **were** | were not | **weren't** |

### Questions and short answers

| | Question | Short answer (yes) | Short answer (no) |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / he / she / it | **Was** I/he/she/it …? | Yes, I/he/she/it **was**. | No, I/he/she/it **wasn't**. |
| we / you / they | **Were** we/you/they …? | Yes, we/you/they **were**. | No, we/you/they **weren't**. |

### Key patterns

- ✅ *"The deployment was successful."* (he/she/it → was)
- ✅ *"The engineers were exhausted after the incident."* (they → were)
- ✅ *"Was the PR reviewed before merge?"* (question)
- ✅ *"The alerts weren't reliable."* (negative contraction)

> ⚠️ **Important:** *was/were* describes a **completed past state** — something that was true at a specific time in the past. It's not used for habitual past actions (that's *used to* or *would*) or for describing a sequence of past events (that's past simple with action verbs: *deployed*, *fixed*, *reviewed*).

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The production incident was caused by a misconfigured load balancer — the root cause wasn't a code bug."** — past state, negative
2. **"Were all the engineers on the on-call rotation aware of the new escalation policy?"** — question about past state
3. **"The design review was thorough, but the implementation timeline wasn't realistic."** — contrast: positive and negative
4. **"The AI model's accuracy was 91% on the test set, but the results weren't consistent across all regions."** — technical past state
5. **"I was in a stakeholder meeting when the alert fired — I wasn't available to join the incident bridge immediately."** — personal past state
6. **"Were the sprint goals aligned with the product roadmap last quarter?"** — question about team/process
7. **"The junior engineers were fantastic during the outage — they weren't intimidated by the pressure at all."** — praise + negative
8. **"It was a tough quarter, but the team was resilient."** — general past assessment

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"The team were very productive last week."** → ✅ **"The team was very productive last week."**
*Why: in American English (and formal British English), collective nouns like "team", "company", "management" take singular verb forms — was, not were. (In informal British English, "the team were" is acceptable, but in professional writing, default to was for collectives.)*

❌ **"Was the engineers notified?"** → ✅ **"Were the engineers notified?"**
*Why: "engineers" is plural — use were, not was, with plural subjects. A common error for speakers whose native language doesn't change the verb for number.*

❌ **"The server wasn't worked properly last night."** → ✅ **"The server wasn't working properly last night."** or **"The server didn't work properly last night."**
*Why: wasn't + past participle is used for passive (e.g., "it wasn't fixed"). For describing a past state in progress, use wasn't + -ing (past continuous). For a simple past fact, use didn't + base verb. Don't mix wasn't with a bare verb in the wrong pattern.*

## 5. 🛠️ Practice

### Recognition — gap-fill

Fill in the blank with *was*, *were*, *wasn't*, or *weren't*:

> *"The deployment \_\_\_ scheduled for midnight, but the infra team \_\_\_ ready — the configuration changes \_\_\_ complete yet. \_\_\_ the release manager informed? She \_\_\_ — no one had updated her."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **was** — singular subject "the deployment"
2. **wasn't** — singular "the infra team" (collective, singular)
3. **weren't** — plural "the configuration changes"
4. **Was** — singular "the release manager" → question form
5. **wasn't** — negative, she was not informed

### Production — write + speak

**Model answer:**
- *"The migration was more complex than estimated, but the team was well-prepared."*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**"Was = one person or thing. Were = more than one (or you)."**

If you can replace the subject with *he*, *she*, or *it* → use **was**.
If you can replace it with *they* or *we* → use **were**.
Special case: *you* always uses **were**, even for one person: *"Were you in the call?"*

Quick test: *"The server ___ down."* → *It* was down → **was**. ✅
*"The microservices ___ responding slowly."* → *They* were → **were**. ✅

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| was | /wɒz/ (stressed) / /wəz/ (unstressed) | Almost always unstressed in speech: "wuz" ⚠️ |
| were | /wɜː/ (stressed) / /wə/ (unstressed) | Reduced to "wuh" in connected speech ⚠️ |
| wasn't | /ˈwɒzənt/ | "WOZ-unt" — stress on first syllable |
| weren't | /wɜːnt/ | One syllable only: "WURN't" ⚠️ |
| incident | /ˈɪnsɪdənt/ | "IN-si-dunt" — 3 syllables, stress on first |
| configuration | /kənˌfɪɡjʊˈreɪʃən/ | "kun-fig-yuh-RAY-shun" — stress on 4th syllable ⚠️ |
| deployment | /dɪˈplɔɪmənt/ | "di-PLOY-munt" — stress on 2nd syllable |
| misconfigured | /ˌmɪskənˈfɪɡjəd/ | "mis-kun-FIG-yud" — stress on 3rd syllable |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Reduced *was* and *were*** ⚠️: In natural English, *was* sounds like "wuz" (/wəz/) and *were* shrinks to a quick "wuh" (/wə/). Brazilians often say "WAHZ" and "WAIR" with full vowels — this sounds stiff. Practice making them weak: *"The team wuz great"*, *"They wuh ready."*
- **weren't** ⚠️: This is one syllable in natural speech: "WURN't." Brazilians often add a vowel and produce "WAIR-rent" (2 syllables) or "WAIR-ent." Compress it: *WURN't*. The /ɜː/ sound (as in "bird", "turn") doesn't exist in Portuguese — round your lips slightly and put your tongue in the middle of your mouth.
- **Final consonant clusters** ⚠️: *wasn't* ends in "-nt", *weren't* ends in "-rnt." Portuguese words rarely end in consonant clusters, so Brazilians often drop the final /t/: *"woz-un"*, *"wurn."* Keep the /t/ — it marks the negative clearly.
- **configuration** ⚠️: In Portuguese, "configuração" is stressed differently. The English word has stress on the 4th syllable: *kun-fig-yuh-RAY-shun*. The "-tion" at the end is /ʃən/ — like "shun", not "saun".

### Pronunciation Practice — say each phrase out loud

2. *Were the engineers aware of the new escalation policy?* ⚠️ — /wə ðə ˌendʒɪˈnɪəz əˈweə/
3. *The configuration wasn't correct — the timeouts were set to zero.* ⚠️ — /kənˌfɪɡjʊˈreɪʃən ˈwɒzənt/
4. *The stakeholders weren't aligned on the scope.* ⚠️ — /ðə ˈsteɪkˌhoʊldəz wɜːnt əˈlaɪnd/
5. *Was the PR reviewed before the deployment?* — /wɒz ðə piːˈɑː rɪˈvjuːd bɪˈfɔː ðə dɪˈplɔɪmənt/

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**Was** e **were** são as formas do passado simples do verbo *to be*. A regra de uso é simples: *was* para sujeitos singulares (*I, he, she, it*) e *were* para sujeitos plurais (*we, you, they*). Atenção especial: *you* sempre usa *were*, mesmo no singular (*"Were you in the call?"*). Para nomes coletivos como *team*, *company* e *management*, o inglês americano (e o inglês formal) usa *was*: *"The team was aligned"* — não *"were"*. O negativo contrai para *wasn't* (singular) e *weren't* (plural), e as perguntas invertem o sujeito: *"Was the deployment successful? / Were the engineers notified?"*

Na pronúncia, o ponto mais crítico é que *was* e *were* são quase sempre **átonos** (sem ênfase) na fala natural — *was* soa como "wuz" e *were* como "wuh". Falantes de português tendem a pronunciar esses verbos com vogal cheia, o que soa artificial. Outro ponto: *weren't* é **uma sílaba** — *"WURN't"* — não duas. O som /ɜː/ (como em "bird") não existe em português: tente colocar a língua no meio da boca e arredondar levemente os lábios. Por fim, *configuration* tem acento na 4ª sílaba: *kun-fig-yuh-RAY-shun*.
