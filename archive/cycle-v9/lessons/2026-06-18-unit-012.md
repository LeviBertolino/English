## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — incident post-mortem channel:**
>
> **Paulo:** Did the monitoring alert fire before the outage started?
>
> **Levi:** No, it didn't. The alert threshold was too high — it didn't catch the spike in time.
>
> **Paulo:** Did you page the on-call team manually?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, I did. But they didn't see the message for about 15 minutes.
>
> **Paulo:** Did the rollback fix the issue?
>
> **Levi:** It did, but we didn't document the steps properly. We need to do that today.

Notice: **Did…?** for questions, **didn't** for negatives. Neither form changes the main verb — the main verb stays in the **base form** (the infinitive without *to*).

### The rule

The **simple past negative and questions** use the auxiliary **did/didn't**:

| Structure | Formula | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Negative | Subject + **didn't** + base verb | *She didn't review the PR.* |
| Yes/No question | **Did** + subject + base verb + ? | *Did you merge the branch?* |
| Short answer (yes) | Yes, + subject + **did** | *Yes, she did.* |
| Short answer (no) | No, + subject + **didn't** | *No, she didn't.* |

> **Key rule:** When you use *did* or *didn't*, the **main verb returns to its base form**. This trips up many learners.
>
> ✅ *"Did you **go** to the meeting?"*
> ❌ *"Did you **went** to the meeting?"*
>
> ✅ *"She **didn't write** the test."*
> ❌ *"She **didn't wrote** the test."*

*Did* does the "past" job. The main verb is released — it gets to be base form again.

**Wh- questions** follow the same pattern, with the question word first:

| Question word | + did + subject + base verb |
|---|---|
| *Why* did the build fail? | *What* did she find in the logs? |
| *When* did you deploy? | *How long* did the migration take? |

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The CI pipeline didn't finish within the allotted timeout — it failed at the integration test step."** — engineering, negative
2. **"Did the staff engineer approve the architecture decision record before you opened the RFC?"** — engineering leadership, yes/no question
3. **"We didn't ship the feature last sprint because the security review flagged two open issues."** — product/engineering, negative
4. **"Why did the memory usage spike after the latest deploy? Nobody saw it in staging."** — incident analysis, Wh- question
5. **"Did you read the post-mortem report? It explains exactly what didn't work during the rollback."** — engineering communication, mixed question + negative
6. **"I didn't attend the all-hands meeting — I was debugging a production issue the whole time."** — work, negative
7. **"She didn't study for the certification exam, but she passed anyway — she had years of practical experience."** — everyday life, negative
8. **"Did you enjoy the conference? I heard the keynote didn't disappoint this year."** — everyday life / work, question + negative

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — error correction

Four sentences below contain errors. Find and fix them:

1. *"Did the deployment went smoothly?"*
2. *"I didn't saw the pull request before it was merged."*
3. *"Did she found the root cause of the bug?"*
4. *"They didn't ran the regression tests before the release."*

✅ **Answers:**
1. ❌ *went* → ✅ *"Did the deployment **go** smoothly?"* (base form after *did*)
2. ❌ *saw* → ✅ *"I didn't **see** the pull request…"* (base form after *didn't*)
3. ❌ *found* → ✅ *"Did she **find** the root cause…?"* (base form after *did*)
4. ❌ *ran* → ✅ *"They didn't **run** the regression tests…"* (base form after *didn't*)

### Production task

*Write 2 sentences about a decision or event in a recent project: one negative (something that didn't happen) and one question (asking about something that did or didn't happen).*

**Model answer:**
1. *"The team didn't include rollback instructions in the deploy checklist, so the incident took longer to resolve."*
2. *"Did the product manager sign off on the scope change before the sprint started, or did it come in mid-sprint?"*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**Think of *did/didn't* as a shield that takes the "past" burden.** Once *did* steps in, the main verb is off duty — it goes back to base form. A useful self-check: if your sentence has *did* or *didn't*, look at the main verb. If it ends in *-ed* or is in its irregular past form, something is wrong. Strip it back.

> ❌ *"Did you **reviewed** it?"* → ✅ *"Did you **review** it?"*
> ❌ *"She **didn't went**."* → ✅ *"She **didn't go**."*

One pattern, no exceptions.

---

## 6. 🚀 Daily Production Challenge (self-directed)

The next time you write a **retrospective, incident report, or Slack post-mortem message**, use *didn't* at least once to describe what went wrong or what was missing, and frame at least one learning as a *Did we…?* question you can now answer.

Example: *"We didn't have a runbook for this failure mode. Did the on-call engineer know where to look? No — that's the gap we're closing."*

This is the exact register of engineering communication — honest, past-oriented, and actionable.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

No **simple past negativo e interrogativo**, usamos o auxiliar **did** (afirmativo/interrogativo) ou **didn't** (negativo). A estrutura é: *sujeito + didn't + verbo na forma base* para negativas, e *Did + sujeito + verbo na forma base + ?* para perguntas. O ponto mais importante — e onde a maioria dos aprendizes erra — é que o **verbo principal volta para a forma base** quando *did/didn't* está presente. Ou seja: *"She didn't **write**"* (não *"didn't wrote"*) e *"Did you **go**?"* (não *"Did you went?"*). O *did/didn't* carrega todo o peso do tempo passado; o verbo principal fica "descarregado".

As respostas curtas seguem o padrão: *"Yes, I did."* / *"No, I didn't."* — sem repetir o verbo principal. Perguntas com *Wh-* (why, what, when, how long) seguem a mesma lógica: *"Why did the build **fail**?"* / *"What did she **find**?"*. Preste atenção especial aos verbos irregulares: a tentação de usar *went*, *wrote* ou *found* depois de *did/didn't* é grande — resista. Se *did/didn't* está na frase, o verbo principal é sempre o infinitivo sem *to*.
