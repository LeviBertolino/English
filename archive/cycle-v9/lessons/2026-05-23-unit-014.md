## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Engineering All-Hands — retrospective session:**
>
> **Levi:** Walk me through the timeline of the incident.
> **Ana:** Sure. *When the monitoring alert fired*, *the on-call engineer was already investigating* a separate issue — so response time was slower.
> **Levi:** And the hotfix?
> **Ana:** *When we deployed the hotfix*, the problem disappeared immediately. *While we were deploying*, the SRE team was watching the dashboards live.
> **Levi:** Did anything surprise you?
> **Ana:** Yes — *when I joined the incident channel*, everyone was already calm. The team had reacted fast.

Notice three different uses of *when* and *while*:

- *"When the alert fired, the engineer was already investigating"* → the engineer was **in the middle** of something when the alert happened. Past continuous behind, past simple event.
- *"When we deployed the hotfix, the problem disappeared"* → we deployed, **and then** it disappeared. Sequence: one thing finished, the next began. **Both past simple**.
- *"While we were deploying, the SRE was watching"* → **two actions happening at the same time**. Both ongoing. Both past continuous.

This is the key new layer on top of yesterday: **"when" does two different jobs**, and knowing which one is happening changes which tense you use.

### The rule

**"While" — always simultaneous, always ongoing**

*While* signals that two actions were happening **at the same time**, both in progress. Both verbs → past continuous.

> *While I was reviewing the PR, she was updating the test suite.*
> *While the model was training, we were cleaning the next dataset.*

**"When" — two jobs, two patterns**

*When* can mean two different things:

**Job 1 → Interruption** (one action in progress, another interrupts it)
The ongoing action = **past continuous**. The interruption = **past simple**.

> *I was writing the RFC **when** the VP asked for a live update.* ← I was in the middle of writing; the ask interrupted it.
> *We were halfway through the sprint **when** a P0 landed.* ← in progress; then a sudden event.

**Job 2 → Sequence** (one action finishes, the next begins)
Both actions are complete, one after the other → **both past simple**.

> ***When** the CI passed, we merged the PR.* ← CI passed first, then we merged. Two completed steps.
> ***When** she finished the design doc, the team started reviewing it.* ← finished → started. Sequence.

### The test question

Ask yourself: **Was the first action already in progress when the second happened, or did the first action complete before the second began?**

- Already in progress → past continuous + past simple (interruption)
- Completed first → past simple + past simple (sequence)

### Bonus: "when" with states

*When* can also introduce a **state** (not an action) — states use simple forms even in the past:

> *When I joined the team, the codebase was a mess.* ← "was" = state, not action in progress
> *When she became Staff engineer, she knew the system inside out.* ← state at that moment

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **When the load balancer detected the spike, it automatically rerouted traffic** — two completed steps in sequence, both past simple.
2. **While the backend team was migrating the database, the frontend team was finishing the new UI components** — two simultaneous ongoing work streams.
3. **I was writing my performance self-assessment when my manager pinged me about the roadmap meeting** — background task interrupted by a sudden message.
4. **When the team adopted the new CI pipeline, deployment time dropped by 40%** — adopted first, then drop happened. Sequence.
5. **She was presenting the technical proposal when someone in the audience asked a question that changed the whole direction** — ongoing presentation interrupted.
6. **When I read the post-mortem, I understood exactly what had gone wrong** — I read it; understanding followed. Sequence.
7. **While my phone was charging, I went through the backlog of review comments** — two things happening during the same window.
8. **I was making coffee when I suddenly realised the solution to the memory leak** — classic: something clicks during a mundane background action.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"When the deploy finished, we were celebrating"** → ✅ **"When the deploy finished, we celebrated"**
*Why: "finished" signals the first step is complete — celebrating came next as a sequence. Past continuous ("we were celebrating") would imply the celebration was already in progress when the deploy finished.*

❌ **"While the tests ran, I checked the logs"** → ✅ **"While the tests were running, I checked the logs"**
*Why: "While" frames an ongoing background — the action inside "while" should be past continuous. "The tests ran" sounds like a completed event, not an ongoing backdrop.*

❌ **"I was joining the call when the presenter was already ten minutes in"** → ✅ **"When I joined the call, the presenter was already ten minutes in"**
*Why: "Joining" here is a single entry event — it happened and was done. The state (presenter being in progress) was the background. Use past simple for the entry event, past continuous for the ongoing state you walked into.*

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose the correct verb form:

> *"___ (While / When) the data pipeline ___ (processed / was processing) the batch, someone ___ (noticed / was noticing) a spike in memory usage and ___ (stopped / was stopping) the job. ___ (While / When) we investigated the root cause, it ___ (turned out / was turning out) to be a misconfigured buffer size."*

✅ **Answer:**
*While the data pipeline **was processing** the batch, someone **noticed** a spike in memory usage and **stopped** the job. When we investigated the root cause, it **turned out** to be a misconfigured buffer size.*

- *While ... was processing* → ongoing background action.
- *noticed / stopped* → sudden completed events (interruptions).
- *When we investigated ... it turned out* → both completed in sequence; "turned out" is a result that followed the investigation.

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of *while* and *when* as **time lenses**:

- 🔭 **While** = a telescope — you're looking at **two things happening at the same distance in time**, both visible at once. Both past continuous.
- 📍**When** = a pin on a timeline — it marks a **single point**. What comes after that pin is either a new completed step (past simple) or something that was already in motion (past continuous).

> *While I was in the meeting* (🔭 both ongoing) *when the alert fired* (📍 the pin) *→ I was mid-sentence, and the alert pinned a new moment.*

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| while | /waɪl/ | One clean syllable — no trailing vowel ⚠️ |
| when | /wɛn/ | Short /ɛ/ vowel — same as "pen", "ten" |
| sequence | /ˈsiːkwəns/ | *SEE-kwunce* — stress on first syllable |
| simultaneous | /ˌsɪməlˈteɪniəs/ | *sim-ul-TAY-nee-us* — five syllables ⚠️ |
| interrupted | /ˌɪntəˈrʌptɪd/ | *in-tuh-RUP-tid* |
| was already | /wəz ɔːlˈrɛdi/ | *wuz awl-RED-ee* — "was" weak, "already" stressed |
| turned out | /tɜːnd aʊt/ | Two-syllable phrasal verb — *TURND owt* |
| ongoing | /ˈɒnɡəʊɪŋ/ | *ON-go-ing* — three syllables |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"while" /waɪl/**: The /l/ at the end is the same "dark L" that trips Brazilians up in words like "full" or "call." The word ends on that dark /l/ — don't add an /i/ sound after it. Practice: close your mouth after the /l/. ⚠️
- **"when" vs. "then"**: The /w/ at the start of *when* is a lip-rounding sound that Portuguese speakers sometimes drop or weaken. Make sure your lips round before the /ɛ/: *"w-hen"*, not *"en"* or *"yen"*. ⚠️
- **"simultaneous"**: This word trips up even native speakers. Stress is on syllable 3: *sim-ul-**TAY**-nee-us*. Brazilians often stress syllable 2 (*sim-**ul**-tâneo* in Portuguese). Shift the stress right. ⚠️
- **Weak "was"**: In connected speech, *"was already"* sounds like *"wuz awl-RED-ee"* — "was" almost disappears into the surrounding words.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A lição de hoje aprofunda o contraste entre past continuous e past simple com foco em duas palavras-chave: *while* e *when*. O *while* é mais simples — ele sempre indica simultaneidade: duas ações acontecendo ao mesmo tempo, ambas em andamento, ambas no past continuous (*"While I was testing, she was deploying"*). Já o *when* tem dois empregos distintos. No primeiro, ele marca uma interrupção: a ação em andamento (past continuous) é interrompida por um evento pontual (past simple) — *"I was writing when the alert fired."* No segundo emprego, ele marca sequência: a primeira ação terminou, e então a segunda começou — ambas no past simple — *"When the tests passed, we merged the PR."*

O teste prático é simples: a primeira ação já estava acontecendo quando a segunda ocorreu? → past continuous + past simple (interrupção). A primeira ação terminou antes da segunda começar? → past simple + past simple (sequência). Na pronúncia, atenção ao *while* /waɪl/ — uma sílaba só, sem vogal final — e ao *when* /wɛn/ — não deixe o /w/ inicial desaparecer. Ambas são palavras curtas que Brasileiros frequentemente distorcem ao falar rápido.
