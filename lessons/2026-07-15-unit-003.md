### 2. 🧠 Mini-context

> **CEO:** How's the transformation going this quarter?
> **You:** We **are rolling out** the AI copilot to three business units right now,
> and adoption **is climbing** faster than we expected.
> **CEO:** And the data team?
> **You:** They **are rebuilding** the pipeline this month — I**'m watching** the
> migration closely.

Notice the shape before any rule: each verb is **two parts** — a form of *to be*
(*are, is, am*) **plus** a verb ending in **-ing**. It describes things **in
progress right now** or **around now**, not finished, not permanent.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

Present continuous = **am / is / are** + verb**-ing**.

| Subject | to be | verb-ing |
|---|---|---|
| I | **am** | working |
| He / She / It / The team | **is** | rolling out |
| We / You / They / The directors | **are** | climbing |

**Spelling of -ing:**

| Base verb | -ing | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| work | working | just add -ing |
| make | mak**ing** | drop silent -e |
| run | run**ning** | double final consonant (short vowel) |
| lie | ly**ing** | -ie → -ying |

**Negative:** subject + *am/is/are* + **not** + -ing → *"We **are not** hiring
this quarter."*
**Contractions:** *I**'m** reviewing, she**'s** presenting, they**'re** scaling.*

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** English needs a way to say *this is happening
now, and it isn't finished*. The present continuous is that "live camera" tense:
it frames an action as **in progress** — this second, today, this quarter — and
**temporary**. That's meaning the simple present cannot carry.

**Decision rule — continuous vs. simple present (the rival that matters most).**
This is *the* confusion for Portuguese speakers.

| | Present continuous | Present simple |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | happening **now / temporarily** | **habit, routine, permanent fact** |
| Example | *We **are hiring** three engineers this month.* | *We **hire** through referrals.* |
| Test | "right now / these days" | "always / usually / in general" |

Minimal pair — same words, different truth:
- *I **am leading** the AI task force.* → a temporary assignment, in progress now.
- *I **lead** the innovation team.* → my permanent role, a general fact.

Choose by **duration and permanence**, not by tense-for-tense translation.

**PT→EN mapping.** Portuguese has a near-identical form: *estar* + gerúndio —
*"estou revisando" = "I am reviewing."* The mapping mostly **works**, which is
good news. Two places it **betrays** Levi:
- Brazilian Portuguese often uses the **simple present** for something happening
  now (*"Eu trabalho nisso agora"* = *"I'm working on that now"*). Translated
  literally you get *"I work on that now"* — wrong; English wants **am working**
  for the live action.
- **Stative verbs** don't take -ing in English even when PT feels continuous:
  *know, want, need, agree, believe, understand.* You say *"I **understand**
  the risk,"* never *"I am understanding the risk"* — even though PT might phrase
  it as ongoing.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *We **discuss** the strategy right now.* → ✅ *We **are discussing** the
  strategy right now.* — *"right now" = in progress → continuous, not simple.*
- ❌ *I **am knowing** the numbers.* → ✅ *I **know** the numbers.* — *stative
  verb; knowledge isn't an action in progress, so no -ing.*
- ❌ *The team is **runing** the pilot.* → ✅ *The team is **running** the
  pilot.* — *short vowel + single consonant → double it before -ing.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. We **are rolling out** the AI copilot across three business units this quarter.
   — *a live, temporary initiative in progress → continuous, not "we roll out".*
2. Adoption **is climbing** faster than the board forecast. — *a trend happening
   now; the simple "climbs" would sound like a permanent habit.*
3. I**'m watching** the data migration closely this month. — *"this month" frames
   a temporary focus → am + -ing.*
4. The directors **are not aligning** on the investment yet. — *negative
   continuous: alignment is a process still underway, still unfinished.*
5. Right now the market **is shifting** toward AI-first players. — *"right now" +
   a change in motion → the live-camera tense.*
6. We **are hiring** two AI leads this quarter, but normally we **promote**
   internally. — *continuous for the temporary push vs. simple present for the
   standing policy — the contrast in one line.*
7. She**'s presenting** the 2027 vision to the CEO this afternoon. — *a scheduled
   action around now; simple "presents" would imply a routine.*
8. The kids **are studying** for exams this week. — *everyday life; a temporary
   situation ("this week"), not a permanent trait.*

### 8. 🗣️ Para levar para a aula

- Ask your tutor to play the CEO asking *"How's the transformation going?"* and
  give a 60-second live status update where **every ongoing action** uses the
  present continuous — *"We are rolling out…, adoption is climbing…, the team is
  rebuilding…"*. Goal: make the two-part *to be* + -ing automatic under pressure.
- Then a **now-vs-always sorting round**: your tutor names a topic (*hiring,
  investing, reporting*) and you say one continuous sentence for what's happening
  **this quarter** and one simple-present sentence for the **standing policy** —
  *"We are hiring two leads now"* vs *"We hire through referrals"* — so you stop
  defaulting to the simple present for live action.

### 9. ✅ Gabarito

**Warm-up (Unit 002)**
1. **"Is the pilot live?"** — *flip the statement's* is *to the front of the
   subject to make the question.*
2. ❌ *"Why the board is still cautious?"* → ✅ **"Why is the board still
   cautious?"** — *after the question word, the verb still comes before the
   subject.*
3. **Are** — *"confident" runs on* to be, *so the question flips* are; do *is only
   for action verbs (*Do you agree?*).*

**Recognition drill**
1. **is rebuilding** — *"this sprint" = an action in progress → continuous, not
   the habitual "rebuilds".*
2. ❌ *"I am wanting more budget."* → ✅ **"I want more budget for the AI
   program."** — *"want" is a stative verb; it doesn't take -ing.*
3. ❌ *"planing"* → ✅ **"planning"** — *"plan" ends in short vowel + single
   consonant, so double the -n before -ing.*
4. **are focusing** — *"this quarter" marks a temporary shift → continuous;
   "focus" (simple) would wrongly describe it as the permanent habit, which the
   sentence explicitly contrasts with "normally we invest".*

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** O *present continuous* é **am/is/are + verbo-ing** e
serve para ação **em andamento agora** ou **temporária** (*"We **are rolling
out** the copilot this quarter"*). É a "câmera ao vivo": algo acontecendo, não
terminado, não permanente. Contraste com o *present simple*, que é para
**hábito, rotina ou fato permanente** (*"We **hire** through referrals"*).
Decida pela **duração**: agora/estes dias → continuous; sempre/em geral → simple.

**As armadilhas.** (1) O português usa muito o presente simples para o que está
acontecendo agora (*"eu trabalho nisso agora"*) — em inglês isso vira **am
working**, não *"I work now"*. (2) Verbos de estado (*know, want, need, agree,
understand*) **não** aceitam -ing: é *"I **understand** the risk"*, jamais *"I am
understanding"*. (3) Cuidado com a ortografia do -ing: *plan → **planning**, run
→ **running**, make → **making***.
