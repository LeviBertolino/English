### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

The present simple uses the **base verb**. The only change is the **-s** on the
third person singular (*he / she / it*, and any single-thing subject).

| Subject | Verb | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | base | *I **lead** the AI portfolio.* |
| he / she / it | base **+ s** | *She **leads** the AI portfolio.* |

**Spelling of the -s:**
- most verbs: **+ s** → *runs, owns, sets, approves*
- verbs ending in *-s, -sh, -ch, -x, -o*: **+ es** → *watches, pushes, goes, does*
- consonant **+ y** → *y* becomes **ies** → *study → studies, apply → applies*
  (but *play → plays*, because there's a vowel before the *y*)

**Irregular but everyday:** *have → **has***, *do → **does***, *go → **goes***.

**Structure:** *subject + verb(-s for he/she/it) + rest.*

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** The present simple is the tense of **things
that are generally true** — habits, routines, repeated actions, permanent
facts, and how a system works. It is not tied to this exact moment; it's the
"in general / as a rule" tense. *We **run** quarterly reviews* means it's our
standing practice, not that we're running one right now.

**Decision rule — present simple vs present continuous (the rival that
matters most).** This is the same fault line as Units 003–004, now from the
other side. Present continuous = happening **now / temporarily**; present
simple = **habit, routine, permanent fact, general truth**.

| | Present simple | Present continuous |
|---|---|---|
| Talks about | a habit, routine, **general truth** | something happening **now / temporarily** |
| Form | base verb (**+s** for he/she/it) | *am/is/are* + verb**-ing** |
| Example | *We **review** the portfolio every quarter.* | *We **are reviewing** the portfolio right now.* |
| Test words | *usually, every, always, in general* | *right now, at the moment, this week* |

Minimal pair — same words, different meaning:
- *The board **approves** budgets over two million.* → that's the **standing
  rule**, how it always works.
- *The board **is approving** the budget.* → it's happening **in this meeting**,
  a one-time action in progress.

**PT→EN mapping.** The present simple lines up well with the Portuguese
*presente do indicativo*: *"Eu lidero a estratégia" → "I lead the strategy."*
Two places the direct translation betrays Levi:
- **The -s is invisible in Portuguese.** Portuguese also changes the verb
  ending by person (*ele lidera, eles lideram*), but Brazilian speakers routinely
  **drop the English -s** because in speech it's small and easy to forget:
  *"He lead the team"* ❌. English **requires** it: *"He **leads** the team."*
- **"Now" in Portuguese often uses the simple present**, but English switches
  to continuous. *"O que você faz?" = "What are you doing?"* (right now) — not
  *"What do you do?"* (which asks about your **job/role in general**). Same
  Portuguese verb form, two different English tenses depending on now vs. general.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *The CFO report to the board every month.* → ✅ *The CFO **reports** to the
  board every month.* — *single subject → the verb needs -s; "every month"
  confirms it's a routine, so present simple is right, but the -s is
  non-negotiable.*
- ❌ *She don't approve early-stage bets.* → ✅ *She **doesn't** approve
  early-stage bets.* — *the -s "moves onto" the auxiliary in the negative:
  he/she/it → **doesn't**, not "don't".*
- ❌ *Right now I lead the review.* (meaning: at this moment) → ✅ *Right now I**'m
  leading** the review.* — *"right now" = in progress → continuous; the simple
  present here would wrongly mean "it's my general role".*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. We **run** a portfolio review every quarter. — *a standing routine ("every
   quarter") → present simple, not "are running".*
2. Each squad **owns** one strategic bet. — *single subject "each squad" → verb
   takes -s; describes how the model permanently works.*
3. The board **approves** any investment above two million. — *the standing
   rule of the company → present simple; "is approving" would mean it's
   happening in this meeting.*
4. I **set** the strategy and **approve** the big investments. — *"I" → base
   verb, no -s; a general description of the role, not a live action.*
5. Our AI-first positioning **shapes** every hiring decision. — *general truth
   about how the company operates → present simple; single subject → -s.*
6. She **doesn't** sign off on anything without a business case. — *negative
   with he/she/it → **doesn't** + base verb; the -s sits on the auxiliary.*
7. The CFO **reviews** the numbers before every board meeting. — *repeated,
   habitual action ("before every…") → present simple; single subject → -s.*
8. My kids **go** to school at seven and my wife **works** from home. — *everyday
   life; plural "kids" → base "go", single "my wife" → "works".*

### 8. 🗣️ Para levar para a aula

- Ask your tutor to have you **describe how your operating model works** in
  60 seconds — *"We run… each squad owns… the board approves…"* — while they
  listen only for the **third-person -s**. Every dropped -s, they stop you. The
  goal is to make *owns / reports / approves* automatic, not a thing you check.
- Then a **now-vs-general switch drill**: your tutor points at an activity and
  asks it two ways — *"What do you do?"* (role) and *"What are you doing?"*
  (this moment) — and you answer each with the right tense back-to-back, so the
  simple-vs-continuous choice becomes reflex.

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** O *present simple* usa o **verbo na base**, e a única
mudança é o **-s** na terceira pessoa do singular (*he / she / it* e qualquer
sujeito único): *"I **lead**"* mas *"She **leads**"*. É o tempo das coisas
**geralmente verdadeiras** — hábitos, rotinas, fatos permanentes e como um
sistema funciona: *"We **run** a review every quarter."* Contraste com o
*present continuous* (*am/is/are* + **-ing**), que é para algo **acontecendo
agora ou temporário**: *"We **are running** the review right now."* Decida pelas
palavras-chave: *usually / every / in general* → simple; *right now / at the
moment* → continuous.

**As armadilhas.** (1) A maior é **esquecer o -s** na terceira pessoa: é *"He
**reports**"*, nunca *"He report"* — em português a flexão existe (*ele
reporta*) mas em inglês o -s é fácil de engolir. (2) Na negativa o -s "pula"
para o auxiliar: *"She **doesn't** approve"*, não *"She don't"*. (3) "Agora" em
português costuma usar o presente simples, mas o inglês troca para o continuous:
*"O que você faz?"* pode ser *"What **are you doing**?"* (neste momento) ou
*"What **do you do**?"* (sua função em geral) — mesma forma em português, dois
tempos diferentes em inglês.
