## 3. Integrative Writing Task (self-directed)

Write a **short paragraph of 5–7 sentences** about a recent project or initiative at work. You must use **at least 3 of the 7 structures from this week**:

- *will* for a spontaneous decision or offer
- *will* for a prediction
- present continuous for something in progress (now or temporarily)
- present continuous for a future arrangement
- present simple for a habit, routine, or process
- present simple negative (*don't/doesn't*)
- present simple question form (*do/does...?*)

Frame it as a status update you might write for your manager or post as a LinkedIn reflection.

### Model paragraph (for self-comparison after you've written yours)

> *We're currently migrating our authentication service to a new identity provider — it's a temporary project, but it touches every part of the stack. I'm leading the architecture review this quarter, which wasn't my original plan. When I saw the complexity last Monday, I said, "I'll own this end-to-end." Our team generally doesn't make changes at this layer without a full RFC and sign-off from two senior engineers. Does this slow us down sometimes? Yes — but I don't think speed is the constraint here; correctness is. I'm presenting the final migration plan to the CTO next Thursday, and I think the new approach will reduce our auth latency by at least 20%.*

Notice the structures used:
- *We're currently migrating* → present continuous (temporary)
- *I'm leading* → present continuous (temporary assignment)
- *"I'll own this"* → will (1), spontaneous decision
- *doesn't make changes* → present simple negative
- *Does this slow us down?* → present simple question
- *I don't think … will reduce* → will (2) negative prediction (natural form)
- *I'm presenting … next Thursday* → present continuous (future arrangement)

## 4. Pronunciation Drill: The 5 Trickiest Sounds of the Week

These came up repeatedly across all 7 lessons. Drill each one:

---

### Sound 1 — /θ/ — "th" in *think, threshold, they're*

**Why it's hard**: Portuguese has no dental fricative. Brazilians typically substitute /t/ (*"tink"*) or /d/ (*"dey're"*), which changes the word entirely.

**How to do it**: Touch your tongue tip lightly to the edge of your upper teeth (or just between them). Release a soft stream of air.
- Voiceless /θ/: *think, threshold, three, both* — air only, no voice.
- Voiced /ð/: *they're, the, there, that* — same position, but hum.

**Drill phrases** (from Units 018, 003, 006):
> *I don't **think** it will work.*
> *It **doesn't** alert below the **threshold**.*
> ***They're** running the tests.*

---

### Sound 2 — /wəʊnt/ (*won't*) vs. /wɒnt/ (*want*)

**Why it's hard**: Two very common words, near-identical spelling, completely different vowels — and confusion changes the meaning entirely.

| Word | IPA | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| won't | /wəʊnt/ | rhymes with "don't" |
| want | /wɒnt/ | rhymes with "font" |

**Drill pair** (from Unit 017):
> *I **won't** approve this without tests.* ← "dohnt"
> *I **want** to approve this.* ← "wont" (rhymes with font)

Say the pair three times, exaggerating the vowel difference each time.

---

### Sound 3 — /ɪŋ/ — the -ing ending (no vowel after /ŋ/)

**Why it's hard**: Brazilians often add a ghost vowel: *"working-ee," "running-ee."* The /ŋ/ is a nasal stop — silence follows it.

**Fix**: End the word completely on the /ŋ/. Your tongue rises to the soft palate and stops — no vowel escapes after it.

**Drill words** (from Unit 003):
> *work**ing**, run**ning**, review**ing**, debug**ging**, investigat**ing***

Say each one and cut off cleanly after the /ŋ/. No trailing vowel.

---

### Sound 4 — /ʌ/ — the vowel in *doesn't, does, won't* (for Brazilians)

**Why it's hard**: This short central vowel doesn't exist in Portuguese. Brazilians substitute /o/ from words like *"dose"* — making *"doesn't"* sound like *"doz-ent."*

**How to do it**: Open your mouth as if saying /a/, but keep the tongue low and central. It's the vowel in *cup, fun, run, done*. Quick and unrounded.

**Drill** (from Units 006 + 007):
> ***Does** the system **deploy** automatically?*
> *She **doesn't** work on the backend team.*
> *The pipeline **doesn't run** on weekends.*

Focus on the first syllable of *does* and *doesn't*: **DUZ**, not *"dohz."*

---

### Sound 5 — "do you" → /dʒuː/ in connected speech

**Why it's hard**: Brazilians often articulate each word separately (*"doo yoo"*), which sounds robotic. In natural English speech, the /d/ and /j/ merge into /dʒ/ — like the start of *"just."*

**What it sounds like**: *"Do you work?"* → *"Dyoo work?"* — three syllables, not four.

**Drill phrases** (from Unit 007):
> ***Do you** run a weekly architecture review?* → *"Dyoo run..."*
> ***Do you** prioritize technical debt?* → *"Dyoo prioritize..."*
> *What **do you** look for in a code review?* → *"What dyoo look..."*

Practice until the /dʒ/ comes naturally without thinking.

## 5. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**O que deve estar sólido após esta semana:**

Você cobriu sete unidades densas que formam o núcleo da gramática cotidiana em inglês. Até agora devem estar automáticos:
- **Will (1)**: decisão espontânea, oferta, promessa — *"I'll take a look"*, *"I'll handle that"*.
- **Will (2)**: previsão sem evidência presente — *"I think costs will drop"* — e o padrão natural para negativas: *"I don't think it will work"* (não *"I think it won't"*).
- **Presente contínuo**: tanto para ações em andamento agora (*"I'm debugging"*) quanto para situações temporárias (*"I'm leading the migration this quarter"*) e compromissos futuros já marcados (*"I'm presenting on Tuesday"*).
- **Presente simples**: fatos gerais, hábitos, comportamento de sistemas (*"The pipeline runs every night"*); negativas com *don't/doesn't* e o verbo principal na forma base (*"she doesn't work"*, nunca *"doesn't works"*); perguntas com *do/does* no início (*"Does the system deploy?"*) e respostas curtas sem repetir o verbo principal (*"Yes, it does."*).

**O que provavelmente ainda precisa de atenção:**

O maior campo de erros desta semana é a **fronteira entre presente simples e contínuo**: saber quando usar *"I work"* (fato permanente) vs. *"I'm working"* (temporário/agora). Se esse ponto ainda parecer instável, volte às tabelas das Unidades 004 e 005 — o teste prático *"seria para sempre?"* é a âncora mais confiável. Na pronúncia, o som /θ/ de *think* e *threshold* é o mais difícil para brasileiros e apareceu em quatro das sete aulas — invista cinco minutos nesse som esta semana. O par *won't / want* também é armadilha frequente em conversas de trabalho.

**Arquivos para revisar este fim de semana se algo ficou nebuloso:**
- Fronteira simples/contínuo: **Unit 004** (tabela de permanente vs. temporário) e **Unit 005** (foto da rotina normal).
- Negativas e perguntas: **Unit 006** (memória tip: *"doesn't steals the -s"*) e **Unit 007** (detective at the door).
