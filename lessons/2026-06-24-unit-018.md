## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — incident post-mortem:**
>
> **@platform-lead:** When did the alert first fire?
>
> **@on-call:** The alert fired **two hours ago**. We identified the root cause **forty minutes ago** — a misconfigured autoscaler. The service has been recovering **for the last thirty minutes**.
>
> **@platform-lead:** How long has the issue been open **since** the alert?
>
> **@on-call:** Almost two hours. We changed the autoscaler config **ten minutes ago**. Since then, error rates have been dropping steadily.

Notice three words doing different jobs: **ago** (two hours ago, ten minutes ago) — **for** (for the last thirty minutes) — **since** (since the alert, since then). Each one anchors time differently.

---

### The rule

| Word | Pairs with | What it expresses | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **for** | present perfect **or** past simple | a duration (how long) | *"I've worked here **for** 2 years."* / *"I worked there **for** 5 years."* |
| **since** | present perfect | a starting point (from when until now) | *"I've worked here **since** 2022."* |
| **ago** | **past simple only** | distance from now looking backward | *"I joined **2 years ago**."* |

**The critical contrast — all three can describe the same fact:**

> 🗓️ You joined the company in January 2024. It is now June 2026 — 2.5 years later.

| Structure | Sentence | Tense |
|---|---|---|
| **for** | *"I have worked here **for** two and a half years."* | present perfect |
| **since** | *"I have worked here **since** January 2024."* | present perfect |
| **ago** | *"I joined **two and a half years ago**."* | **past simple** |

All three sentences are correct. They frame the same fact from different angles:
- **for/since** → the situation is **still ongoing** (you're still there) → present perfect
- **ago** → you're looking back at **when something happened** → past simple

> ⚠️ **Never mix ago with present perfect:** *"I have joined two years ago"* ❌ → *"I joined two years ago"* ✅ (past simple only)

> ⚠️ **for with past simple** = the situation ended: *"She worked at Google for three years (before joining our team)."* The past simple signals she's no longer there.

---

### For vs Since — quick check

| Test | Word to use |
|---|---|
| Can you replace it with a **quantity of time** (*three months, six sprints, ages*)? | → **for** |
| Can you replace it with a **date or event** (*January, the last reorg, 2022*)? | → **since** |

---

### 🇧🇷 Portuguese speaker alert — "há"

In Portuguese, **"há"** covers both meanings that English splits into two:

| Portuguese | English equivalent | Note |
|---|---|---|
| *"Trabalho aqui há dois anos."* | *"I've worked here for two years."* | still working here → **present perfect + for** |
| *"Comecei aqui há dois anos."* | *"I started here two years ago."* | looking back at the event → **past simple + ago** |

The word **"há"** in Portuguese is the source of the most common error: *"I work here since two years"* ❌ or *"I've started here two years ago"* ❌. English forces you to choose.

Fill in **for**, **since**, or **ago**. You may also need to choose the correct verb form (past simple or present perfect):

*"Our current CI/CD pipeline ___ (replace) the old Jenkins setup two years ___. We ___ (use) it ___ two years now — ___ the platform team ___ (finish) the migration in Q2. It ___ (save) us hours of manual work ___ then."*

*"Our current CI/CD pipeline **replaced** the old Jenkins setup two years **ago**. We **have used** it **for** two years now — **since** the platform team **finished** the migration in Q2. It **has saved** us hours of manual work **since** then."*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of a **timeline with an arrow pointing right (→ now)**:

```
Past ─────────────────────────────────────────→ NOW
       [Event] ← "ago" looks back from NOW
       [Event] ──── "since" runs forward to NOW ───→
       [←────── "for" measures the span ─────────]→
```

**ago** always points **backward** from now → past simple.  
**since/for** always stretch **forward to now** → present perfect.

Quick verbal trick: if you can naturally say *"It happened __ ago"*, use past simple. If you can naturally say *"It's been happening for/since __"*, use present perfect.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

As palavras **for**, **since** e **ago** são usadas para situar algo no tempo, mas funcionam de maneiras distintas em inglês. **For** acompanha uma **duração** (*for three years, for six months*) e pode aparecer tanto com o present perfect (situação ainda em andamento) quanto com o simple past (situação encerrada). **Since** acompanha um **ponto de início** (*since 2022, since the last sprint*) e é usada **sempre** com o present perfect — nunca com o simple past. **Ago** indica **distância do presente olhando para trás** (*two years ago, ten minutes ago*) e é usada **sempre** com o simple past — jamais com o present perfect.

O erro mais comum para falantes de português é causado pela palavra **"há"**, que em português cobre os dois casos: *"comecei há dois anos"* (passado, algo que aconteceu → *I started two years ago* — simple past + ago) e *"trabalho aqui há dois anos"* (situação contínua → *I've worked here for two years* — present perfect + for). Em inglês, você é obrigado a escolher: se a situação ainda é verdade agora, use o present perfect com *for* ou *since*. Se você está olhando para trás para quando algo aconteceu, use o simple past com *ago*. Erro clássico a evitar: *"I've joined two years ago"* ❌ → *"I joined two years ago"* ✅ ou *"I've been here for two years"* ✅.
