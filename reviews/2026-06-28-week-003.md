# 🔁 Review Week 3 — Units 016 to 021

> **Week in focus:** The Present Perfect and its relationship to time — six units, one big idea: how to connect the past to *right now* in professional English.

---

## 1. Recap: What You Covered This Week

| Unit | Title | Core idea |
|---|---|---|
| 016 | I have done (Present Perfect — Introduction) | **have/has + past participle**: past action whose result matters *now*. The "news headline" tense. |
| 017 | How long have you (been)...? (Part 1) | **for** (duration) and **since** (starting point) with present perfect for situations still ongoing. |
| 018 | for, since and ago | **ago** always goes with past simple (looking back at the event); **for/since** go with present perfect (span up to now). |
| 019 | I have done and I did (1) | The key split: **open time** (*this week, ever, already, yet*) → present perfect; **closed time** (*yesterday, last month, in 2022, ago*) → past simple. |
| 020 | I have done and I did (2) | PP opens the topic → PS fills in the detail. **been to** (came back) vs **gone to** (still there). *"It's the first/second time…"* → always present perfect. |
| 021 | How long have you (been)...? (Part 2) | **Continuous** for ongoing activities (*I've been working*); **simple** for state verbs (*know, have, believe, own*). Never *"I've been knowing"*. |

## 3. Answers

### Exercise 1
(a) **has scheduled** — the Thursday post-mortem is a result relevant now  
(b) **have already sent** — already + present perfect  
(c) **haven't included** — not yet = absence is the current situation  
(d) **Have** you **reviewed** — present perfect question, no specific time given

---

### Exercise 2
(a) **ago** — looking back at the moment the alert fired → past simple implied  
(b) **ago** — looking back at when the root cause was found → past simple  
(c) **for** — duration: "the last thirty minutes" is a span, not a starting point  
(d) **since** — starting point: "since we changed…" is a specific event  
(e) **ago** — past simple: "ten minutes ago" (you changed it; looking back)

---

### Exercise 3
1. **has shipped** — *this quarter* is still open (not over yet) → present perfect
2. **shipped** — *last quarter* is a closed period → past simple
3. **designed** — *ever* = any point in your career up to now → present perfect: *"Have you ever designed…?"*
4. **designed** — *when she was* anchors it to a finished past context → past simple
5. **haven't reviewed** — *yet* = present perfect negative (result still missing now)
6. **reviewed** — *last Friday* is a closed timestamp → past simple

---

### Exercise 4
1. **I've been to** — you went, experienced it, and came back. *"Been to"* = round trip.
2. **She's gone to** — she left and hasn't returned; *"back on Thursday"* confirms this.
3. **been to** — asking about an experience you've had and returned from.

---

### Exercise 5
1. **have known** — *know* is a state verb; never continuous. → *"I've known about this…"*
2. **has been working** — *work* is an activity, duration ongoing → continuous
3. **have you had** — *have* (possess) is a state verb → simple: *"How long have you had…?"*
4. **have been running** — *run* (operate a system) is an activity → continuous
5. **have understood** — *understand* is a state verb → simple

---

### Exercise 6
1. *"Yes, I have. The first one **went** smoothly; the second one **caused** a two-hour outage in 2023."* — once you give the specific events/dates, switch to past simple.
2. *"Yes! I **met** her **at** the all-hands last Tuesday. She **seemed** very sharp."* — *last Tuesday* closes the time → past simple.
3. *"Yes — I **worked** with it pretty heavily at my previous company. We **migrated** three internal APIs over about eight months."* — past context, specific company → past simple.

---

### Exercise 7
1. *"I've been working here for three years."* (present perfect + *for* — still working there)
2. *"I started this role six months ago."* (past simple + *ago* — looking back at the start event)
3. *"How long have you been learning Rust?"* (present perfect continuous — ongoing activity, duration)
4. *"It's the first time I've presented to the board."* (*first time* → always present perfect)
5. *"I've known about this problem since the last sprint."* (state verb *know* → present perfect simple + *since*)

---

### Exercise 8
1. ❌ *"I have seen him yesterday"* → ✅ *"I **saw** him yesterday."* (*yesterday* forces past simple)
2. ❌ *"She has gone to three AWS re:Invent conferences"* → ✅ *"She has **been to** three AWS re:Invent conferences."* (*gone to* = still there; she came back → *been to*)
3. ❌ *"We've been knowing"* → ✅ *"We've **known** about this security gap for months."* (*know* is a state verb — no continuous)
4. ❌ *"How long are you working"* → ✅ *"How long **have you been working** in tech leadership?"* (duration up to now → present perfect, not present simple)
5. ❌ *"I've joined two years ago"* → ✅ *"I **joined** this team two years **ago**."* (*ago* forces past simple — never present perfect)

## 5. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**O que deve sentir sólido depois desta semana:**

As seis unidades formam um único bloco conceitual em torno do **present perfect** e suas relações com o tempo. O núcleo da semana é simples: o present perfect (*I have done, I've been working*) liga o passado ao **presente** — o resultado importa agora, ou a situação ainda está em andamento. O past simple (*I did, I worked*) arquiva o evento em um momento específico e encerrado do passado. A diferença entre **for** (duração), **since** (ponto de início) e **ago** (olhando para trás desde agora) é o andaime que sustenta toda a semana. E o uso de **continuous** (*I've been working*) versus **simple** (*I've known*) depende de um único teste: o verbo é uma ação ou um estado? Verbos de estado (*know, have, understand, believe, own*) nunca vão no continuous.

**O que provavelmente ainda precisa de trabalho:**

O ponto que mais gera erros para falantes de PT-BR é a combinação **ago + past simple**. O instinto é traduzir *"há dois anos"* como present perfect — mas quando você está olhando para o momento em que algo aconteceu (*"comecei aqui há dois anos"*), o inglês exige past simple + *ago*: *"I started here two years ago."* O segundo ponto mais difícil é o **handoff PP→PS**: abrir o assunto no present perfect e depois dar os detalhes no past simple é um padrão natural do inglês falado que não existe de forma explícita em português. O terceiro: *"It's the first time I've done this"* — em PT-BR você usa o presente; em inglês, o present perfect é obrigatório.
