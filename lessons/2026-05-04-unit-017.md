## 1. 📘 Unit 017 — Have you ever...?

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Ana:** "Have you ever worked with a monorepo setup?"
> **Levi:** "Yes, I have. We used one at my last company — Nx with a shared component library."
> **Ana:** "Really? I've never tried it. Was it worth the migration effort?"
> **Levi:** "Honestly, I've never regretted it. Once the tooling clicked, velocity went up."

Notice the pattern: **Have you ever + worked/tried/regretted**? And the answer: **Yes, I have** or **I've never + past participle**.

### The rule

Use **have/has + ever + past participle** to ask whether someone did something *at any time in their life* up to now.

Use **have/has + never + past participle** to say something has *not happened at any point* in their life.

| Structure | Example |
|---|---|
| Have you ever + past participle? | Have you ever led a reorg? |
| Has she/he/it ever + past participle? | Has your CTO ever shipped on a Friday? |
| I/You/We/They have never + past participle | I've never used Rust in production. |
| She/He has never + past participle | She has never missed a sprint review. |

- **ever** = "at any time" → used in questions
- **never** = "not at any time" → used in statements (stronger than "not")
- Short answers: "Have you ever deployed on a Friday?" → "**Yes, I have.**" / "**No, I haven't.**"

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **Have you ever reviewed a PR with more than 1,000 changed files?** *(tech)*
2. **Our CTO has never led a fully distributed team before this year.** *(tech)*
3. **Have you ever deployed to production on a Friday?** *(tech — don't.)*
4. **I have never seen a sprint planning session go that smoothly.** *(tech)*
5. **Has your skip-level ever asked you to present an engineering roadmap?** *(tech)*
6. **I've never worked with a codebase that had zero tests — until this client.** *(tech)*
7. Have you ever eaten street food in São Paulo?
8. She has never been to a concert outside Brazil.

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **Have you ever went to a hackathon?**
✅ **Have you ever gone to a hackathon?**
*Why: "ever" requires the **past participle** (gone), not the simple past (went).*

❌ **I never have worked with Kubernetes.**
✅ **I have never worked with Kubernetes.**
*Why: "never" comes **after** have/has, not before it.*

❌ **Have you ever been working with GraphQL?**
✅ **Have you ever worked with GraphQL?**
*Why: with ever/never, use the simple past participle alone — no -ing form.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **ever** as a time detective scanning your *entire timeline* for evidence:
> "Have you **ever** done X?" = "Anywhere in your whole past, did X happen?"

**Never** = **not + ever** — it seals the timeline shut:
> "I have **never** done X" = "Search all you like — X is not in my history."

Bonus: if you remember **"ever" lives in questions** and **"never" lives in statements**, you'll almost never mix them up.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words — IPA

| Word/Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ever | /ˈɛvər/ | ⚠️ Final /r/ — retroflex in American English |
| never | /ˈnɛvər/ | ⚠️ Same final /r/ — don't drop it |
| Have you ever | /hæv juː ˈɛvər/ | Fast speech: /həvjə ˈɛvər/ |
| I've | /aɪv/ | ⚠️ Contract it — don't say "I have" each time |
| been | /bɪn/ | ⚠️ Short /ɪ/ — Brazilians sometimes stretch it to /biːn/ |
| seen | /siːn/ | Long /iː/ — this one IS long |
| worked | /wɜːrkt/ | ⚠️ The /ɜːr/ vowel doesn't exist in Portuguese |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Final /r/ in "ever" / "never":** In Portuguese, final r's are often silent or guttural. In American English, curl the tip of your tongue slightly back without touching anything — it's a retroflex /r/. Without it, "ever" sounds like "évê" to native ears. ⚠️
- **The /æ/ in "have":** More open than the Portuguese "a". Drop your jaw a bit more — like you're about to say "á" but your mouth is wider.
- **"I've" vs. "I have":** In natural speech, always contract. "I have never" = fine in writing, but sounds stiff spoken. Say *I've never* /aɪv ˈnɛvər/.
- **"Have you ever" flow:** In real conversation this merges: *"Havyu ever"* — practice it as one flowing unit.

### 🎙️ Pronunciation Practice

Repeat each phrase out loud 2–3 times:

2. `I've never done that.` → /aɪv ˈnɛvər dʌn ðæt/ ⚠️
3. `She has never been to London.` → /ʃiː həz ˈnɛvər bɪn tə ˈlʌndən/

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Para falar sobre experiências de vida em inglês, usamos o **present perfect com *ever* e *never***. A estrutura para perguntas é **have/has + ever + particípio passado**: *Have you ever led a platform migration?* (Você já liderou uma migração de plataforma?). Para afirmar que nunca fez algo, usamos **have/has + never + particípio passado**: *I have never worked with a fully serverless architecture* (Nunca trabalhei com uma arquitetura totalmente serverless). Lembre: *ever* vai nas **perguntas**; *never* vai nas **afirmações negativas**.

O erro mais frequente é usar o **simple past** no lugar do particípio: ❌ *Have you ever went* → ✅ *Have you ever gone*. Outro cuidado: *never* vem **após** o auxiliar — ❌ *I never have done* → ✅ *I have never done*. Na pronúncia, o maior desafio para falantes de português é o /r/ retroflexo no final de "ever" e "never" — esse som não existe em português e exige prática deliberada.
