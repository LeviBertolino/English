## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **1:1 catch-up — Levi and his manager, Thursday afternoon:**
>
> **Manager:** How's the quarter going for you?
>
> **Levi:** Pretty good. I **'m leading** the platform migration this quarter — it's a temporary assignment, not a permanent shift. The team **is growing** fast, so we **'re moving** from a mono-repo to a federated structure.
>
> **Manager:** Is the architecture stable?
>
> **Levi:** **It's getting** better. We **'re also onboarding** three new Staff engineers, so there's a lot of context-sharing happening. AI tooling **is changing** so fast right now — **we're adjusting** our practices every sprint.
>
> **Manager:** What does next week look like?
>
> **Levi:** I **'m presenting** the new architecture to the CTO on Tuesday. And on Friday, **I'm flying** to São Paulo for the engineering off-site.

Notice something: most of these actions are **not happening right now** as they speak. Levi isn't presenting to the CTO *at this exact moment*. But the present continuous is still correct — because it covers **two extra uses** beyond "right now."

### The rule: two more uses of present continuous

**Use 1 — Temporary or changing situations (around now, not necessarily at this moment)**

The present continuous describes situations that are **in progress for a period** but are **not permanent**:

| Permanent → Present Simple | Temporary → Present Continuous |
|---|---|
| *I work remotely.* (always) | *I'm working from São Paulo this week.* (just this week) |
| *She leads the backend team.* (her role) | *She's leading the incident response.* (temporarily, this situation) |
| *We use a mono-repo.* (current practice) | *We're moving to a federated structure.* (in progress, changing) |
| *AI improves every year.* (general fact) | *AI tooling is changing incredibly fast right now.* (ongoing shift) |

The key question: **is this a permanent fact, or a temporary/evolving situation?**
- Permanent / always true → **present simple**: *I live in Curitiba.*
- Temporary / changing / happening for a limited period → **present continuous**: *I'm living in São Paulo this month.*

**Use 2 — Future arrangements (something you've planned or scheduled)**

When you've already arranged something for the future, use present continuous:

> *"I'm presenting to the CTO on Tuesday."* → It's in the calendar; it's arranged.
> *"She's flying to Berlin next week."* → Ticket booked, meeting scheduled.
> *"We're deploying the new service on Friday."* → Deployment window booked.

This is about **personal arrangements** — things you've scheduled or committed to. The future meaning is clear from a time word (Tuesday, next week, Friday) or context.

> ⚡ Quick contrast: *"I'll present to the CTO"* is a decision or prediction. *"I'm presenting to the CTO on Tuesday"* is a fixed arrangement.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I work from home this week."** → ✅ **"I'm working from home this week."**
*Why: "This week" signals a temporary situation, not a permanent fact. Temporary = present continuous. If it were your permanent setup, "I work from home" would be correct.*

❌ **"I will have a call with the CTO on Tuesday."** (when it's already scheduled) → ✅ **"I'm having a call with the CTO on Tuesday."**
*Why: Once an arrangement is fixed (in the calendar, booked, committed to), present continuous is the natural English choice — not "will." "Will" sounds like a new decision, not a pre-arranged plan.*

❌ **"The team is working hard every day."** → ✅ **"The team works hard every day."**
*Why: "Every day" signals a habit or permanent situation, not something temporary or in progress right now. Habits = present simple. Reserve present continuous for things that feel temporary, changing, or specifically arranged.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**Present continuous = not forever.**

Ask yourself: *"Is this permanent or temporary?"*
- **Permanent** → present simple: *I live in Curitiba. I lead the backend team.*
- **Temporary / changing / arranged** → present continuous: *I'm living in São Paulo this month. I'm leading the migration. I'm presenting on Friday.*

A quick mental test: *"Could I say 'but not forever'?"*
- *"I'm working on the RFC — but not forever."* ✅ → continuous
- *"I work in tech — but not forever."* ❌ sounds weird → simple

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I'm leading | /aɪm ˈliːdɪŋ/ | Contraction — one syllable /aɪm/ ⚠️ |
| we're moving | /wɪər ˈmuːvɪŋ/ | "we're" — /wɪər/, not /wiː.ɑːr/ ⚠️ |
| it's getting | /ɪts ˈɡetɪŋ/ | Flap-T: "getting" → /ˈɡedɪŋ/ in American English ⚠️ |
| we're adjusting | /wɪər əˈdʒʌstɪŋ/ | /dʒ/ sound — like "judge" — soft J |
| I'm presenting | /aɪm prɪˈzentɪŋ/ | Stress on 2nd syllable: pre-SENT-ing ⚠️ |
| I'm flying | /aɪm ˈflaɪɪŋ/ | Two vowel sounds together: /laɪ.ɪŋ/ — keep them separate |
| temporary | /ˈtempəreri/ | 4 syllables: TEM-po-rar-y ⚠️ |
| arrangement | /əˈreɪndʒmənt/ | Stress on 2nd syllable: a-RANGE-ment |
| migrating | /maɪˈɡreɪtɪŋ/ | 3 syllables: mi-GRATE-ing |
| onboarding | /ˈɒnbɔːrdɪŋ/ | Stress on 1st syllable: ON-boarding |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **Contractions** ⚠️: *"I am presenting"* is correct but formal and slow. In natural speech: *"I'm presenting."* Practice until contractions feel as natural as the full forms.
- **"it's getting"** ⚠️: American English uses a flap-T — the /t/ in "getting" sounds like a soft /d/: */ˈɡedɪŋ/*. British English keeps a clear /t/. Both are fine — just be aware so you can understand both.
- **"presenting"** ⚠️: The stress is on the *second* syllable — *pre-SENT-ing*. Brazilians often say *PRE-sent-ing* (first syllable). Shift the weight to the middle.
- **"temporary"** ⚠️: 4 syllables — *TEM-po-rar-y*. In fast speech: *TEM-pruh-ree* (3 syllables). Common mistake: *tem-po-RÁ-rio* (Portuguese-style). Keep the stress firmly on the first syllable.
- **"we're"** ⚠️: /wɪər/ — one syllable, a diphthong. Brazilians sometimes say /wiː.ɑːr/ (two syllables, like "wee-ARE"). Keep it short and merged: /wɪər/.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **presente contínuo (2)** tem dois usos que vão além de "está acontecendo agora mesmo": **(1) situações temporárias ou em mudança** — ações que estão em andamento por um período limitado, mas que não são fatos permanentes: *"I'm leading the migration this quarter"* (estou liderando esta trimestre, mas não para sempre); *"AI tooling is changing fast"* (está mudando agora, em andamento); e **(2) compromissos futuros já combinados** — quando algo está na agenda, marcado, reservado: *"I'm presenting to the CTO on Tuesday"* (apresentação já marcada para terça). Nesses dois casos, o presente contínuo é a escolha natural, mesmo que a ação não esteja acontecendo no exato momento em que você fala.

O erro mais comum é usar o presente simples onde o contínuo é o certo: *"I work from home this week"* soa como fato permanente; *"I'm working from home this week"* comunica corretamente que é temporário. Da mesma forma, para compromissos futuros já agendados, evite *"I will have a meeting"* — o inglês natural é *"I'm having a meeting on Thursday."* A regra prática: **temporário / em mudança / compromisso marcado → present continuous; permanente / hábito / fato geral → present simple**.
