## 1. Recap — What this week covered

| Unit | Title | Core rule |
|---|---|---|
| **017** | Have you ever...? | *Have you ever + past participle?* → life experience questions. *I have never* → zero-results-in-life negation. Superlative + *ever* for extremes. |
| **018** | How long have you...? (for and since) | *For* = a duration (📏 ruler). *Since* = a starting point (📍 pin). Both with Present Perfect for situations still ongoing now. |
| **019** | for, since, ago | *Ago* = **when** it happened → past simple **only**. *Since* = **from when** → present perfect **only**. *For* = **how long** → either tense, depending on whether it's finished. |
| **020** | I have done and I did | Golden test: *Did I say when?* Yes → past simple. No, and it still matters now → present perfect. |
| **021** | is done / was done | Passive = **is/are + past participle** (present) and **was/were + past participle** (past). Use when the doer is unknown, obvious, or irrelevant — default language of technical docs and post-mortems. |

## 3. 🖊️ Integrative Writing Task (self-directed)

Write a short paragraph (5–7 sentences) about a **recent project or initiative at work** — real or imagined. Use at least 3 of the 5 structures from this week:

1. *Have you ever / I've never* (Unit 017)
2. *for* or *since* + present perfect (Unit 018)
3. *ago* + past simple (Unit 019)
4. Present Perfect + Past Simple in the same paragraph (Unit 020)
5. Passive voice — *was/were + past participle* (Unit 021)

**📝 Model paragraph for self-comparison:**

> Our team kicked off a full platform migration about eight months ago, and I've been leading the infrastructure side of it since day one. A new event-driven architecture was adopted to replace the legacy monolith — the decision was made after a major incident that highlighted how fragile the old system had become. I've never worked on a migration this complex, but the team has been remarkably focused throughout. Two months ago, we hit a critical milestone: the first production service was fully migrated and deployed without any downtime. We've shipped four services since then, and three more are currently being tested by QA.

Notice: *ago* + past simple (kicked off / hit), *since* + present perfect (I've been leading / we've shipped), passive (was adopted / was made / was deployed / are being tested), and *I've never*.

## 4. 🎤 Pronunciation Drill — 5 Trickiest Sounds This Week

These came up in the ⚠️ sections of Units 017–021. If any still feel shaky, today is the day to drill them.

---

### ⚠️ 1 — Three -ed endings for past participles (Unit 021)

Not all past participles end in /ɪd/. The ending depends on the final sound of the verb:

| Ending | Sound | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| After voiced consonant or vowel | **/d/** | *reviewed* /rɪˈvjuːd/, *merged* /mɜːrdʒd/, *deployed* /dɪˈplɔɪd/ |
| After voiceless consonant | **/t/** | *checked* /tʃɛkt/, *pushed* /pʊʃt/, *blocked* /blɒkt/ |
| After /t/ or /d/ | **/ɪd/** | *tested* /ˈtɛstɪd/, *rejected* /rɪˈdʒɛktɪd/, *updated* /ˈʌpdeɪtɪd/ |

**Drill out loud:** *"The PR was reviewed, the test was rejected, and the build was checked."*

---

### ⚠️ 2 — *Have you ever* → connected speech (Units 017 & 020)

In natural speech, *"Have you ever"* runs together into one fast unit: /həvjuːˈɛvər/ — not three separate words. The *have* reduces to /həv/, and *you* blends into it.

**Drill out loud:** *"Have you ever deployed a hotfix to production?"*
Say it until it feels like one word: *həvjuːˈɛvər dɪˈplɔɪd...*

---

### ⚠️ 3 — Short /ɪ/ in *since* — never "seence" (Units 018 & 019)

*Since* = /sɪns/ — the same short, clipped /ɪ/ as in *bit*, *sit*, *quick*. Not /siːns/.

**Drill out loud:** *"Since — I've been here since 2022 — since then — since Monday."*
Keep every *since* short and clipped. If you can rhyme it with *"hints"*, you're right.

---

### ⚠️ 4 — Unstressed *was* and *were* = /wəz/, /wər/ (Unit 021)

In passive sentences, *was* and *were* are almost always unstressed — they reduce to /wəz/ and /wər/. Stress falls on the past participle, not on *was/were*.

**Drill out loud:** *"The PR was merged. Tests were run. A fix was deployed."*
Hit *was/were* quickly and lightly; land hard on *merged*, *run*, *deployed*.

---

### ⚠️ 5 — *Ago* = weak-STRONG /əˈɡoʊ/ (Unit 019)

The first syllable of *ago* is an unstressed schwa /ə/ — a tiny, swallowed sound. All the stress falls on *-GO*. Brazilians often give both syllables equal weight: *"AH-go"* — aim for *"uh-GO"*.

**Drill out loud:** *"Two years ago — three months ago — six sprints ago."*
Tap your foot on the second syllable each time: *uh-GO, uh-GO, uh-GO.*

## 5. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**O que deve estar mais sólido agora:**
Essa semana cobriu o núcleo do Present Perfect em inglês — a família de estruturas mais importante do seu nível atual. *Have you ever...?* e *I've never...* (Unidade 017) são as ferramentas de experiência de vida: você as usa em entrevistas, perfis no LinkedIn e conversas com colegas internacionais. *For* e *since* (Unidade 018) respondem "há quanto tempo" — e a distinção entre eles (*for* mede, *since* aponta o início) deve estar automática. A tríade *for/since/ago* (Unidade 019) completa o quadro: *ago* sempre exige passado simples, *since* sempre exige Present Perfect — sem exceções. O teste de ouro da Unidade 020 — *"Eu disse quando? Se sim, use o passado simples"* — é a regra mais útil da semana inteira: memorize-a e use antes de escolher o tempo verbal. Por fim, a voz passiva (Unidade 021) é a linguagem padrão de post-mortems, documentação técnica e changelogs — você já a praticou no contexto exato em que vai usá-la no trabalho.

**O que provavelmente ainda precisa de atenção — e o que reler este fim de semana:**
O contraste entre Present Perfect e passado simples (Unidade 020) é o mais sutil — especialmente quando não há palavra-gatilho (*yet, ever, ago*) e você precisa julgar pela intenção: resultado ainda relevante agora vs. evento fechado no passado. Se esse critério ainda parece impreciso, releia a seção 2 da Unidade 020 este fim de semana. A pronúncia dos particípios em *-ed* (Unidade 021) também merece revisão separada — as três formas (/d/, /t/, /ɪd/) dependem do som final do verbo e não têm equivalente em português. Recomendo reler as seções 7 das Unidades 018 e 021 se esses pontos ainda parecerem instáveis. Próxima semana, o ciclo continua — você entra no território do futuro e das formas modais com uma base sólida de Present Perfect.
