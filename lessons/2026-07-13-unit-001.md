### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

*To be* is the most common verb in English, and in the present it has **three
forms**. The form depends on the subject.

| Subject | Full | Short (contraction) | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | am | I'm | *I'm the sponsor.* |
| he / she / it | is | he's / she's / it's | *The plan is ready.* |
| you / we / they | are | you're / we're / they're | *They're aligned.* |

Negative: add **not** → *I'm not, he isn't, they aren't.*
Question: **invert** subject and verb → *Is the budget approved? Are we ready?*

Contractions (*I'm, it's, they're*) are normal and correct in speech and in most
professional writing — they are not slang.

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não

**Why does this verb exist / what does it do?** *To be* links a subject to
**what it is, how it is, or where it is** — an identity, a state, or a location:
*I **am** the sponsor* (identity), *the market **is** volatile* (state), *the
CFO **is** in London* (location). It carries no action; it carries **existence
and description**. Almost every sentence that describes a situation runs through
it, which is why getting the three forms automatic matters so much.

**Decision rule — the form follows the subject, not the meaning:**

| If the subject is… | use | 
|---|---|
| **I** | am |
| **one** thing/person (he, she, it, *the plan, the CEO, our strategy*) | is |
| **you**, or **more than one** (we, they, *the numbers, the directors*) | are |

The one trap: **you** always takes **are**, even for a single person —
*"You are right,"* never *"you is right."*

**Rival structure — *to be* vs the plain verb.** English learners sometimes
glue *to be* onto a normal action verb: *"I am agree,"* *"We are focus on AI."*
That is wrong — action verbs stand alone (*I agree, we focus*). Use *to be* only
before a **noun, an adjective, or a place** (*I am confident, we are a
tech-first company, the report is on your desk*), never before another verb in
its plain form.

**PT→EN mapping.** Portuguese splits this one English verb into **two**: *ser*
(permanent identity) and *estar* (temporary state). English does **not** care
about that difference — *am/is/are* covers both. So *"Eu sou o patrocinador"*
(ser) and *"Eu estou confiante"* (estar) are **both** just *I am*. Good news:
you never have to choose ser vs estar in English. The interference goes the
other way — from **omitting** the verb, because Portuguese often drops it (*"O
mercado volátil"* feels almost complete), while English **never** drops it.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *The market very competitive right now.* → ✅ *The market **is** very
  competitive right now.* — *English requires the verb; you can't leave the
  sentence without it the way Portuguese sometimes can.*
- ❌ *I am agree with the CEO.* / *We are believe in this bet.* → ✅ *I **agree**
  with the CEO. / We **believe** in this bet.* — *don't put* am/are *in front of
  an action verb; the action verb already stands alone.*
- ❌ *The numbers is strong. / You is right.* → ✅ *The numbers **are** strong. /
  You **are** right.* — *plural subjects and* you *take* are, *not* is.

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. **I'm** the executive sponsor of the AI transformation program. — *identity
   (Portuguese "ser"); single subject "I" → am.*
2. Our data strategy **is** the foundation for everything else. — *one subject
   ("our data strategy") describing what it is → is.*
3. The board members **are** not fully aligned yet. — *plural subject → are;
   negative formed with "not".*
4. **Is** the AI budget approved for next quarter? — *question by inverting "is"
   and the subject; the natural way to check status in a meeting.*
5. We'**re** an AI-first company now — that **is** our positioning. — *"we" → are
   (identity); "that" → is; both are the linking verb, not an action.*
6. I'**m** confident, but the market **is** unpredictable. — *state, Portuguese
   "estar" — English still just uses am/is; no ser-vs-estar choice.*
7. The CFO **is** in London until Friday. — *location; "to be" also answers
   where, not only what.*
8. The kids **are** at school and my wife **is** at work. — *everyday life;
   plural "kids" → are, single "my wife" → is.*

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** O verbo *to be* (ser/estar) tem **três formas** no
presente, e a forma segue o **sujeito**: **I → am**; **um** sujeito (he, she,
it, *o plano, o CEO*) **→ is**; **you** e **plurais** (we, they, *os números*)
**→ are**. Use *to be* para dizer **o que algo é, como está, ou onde está** —
antes de substantivo, adjetivo ou lugar (*I'm the sponsor, the market is
volatile, the CFO is in London*). Contrações (*I'm, it's, they're*) são normais
e profissionais.

**As armadilhas.** (1) O inglês **nunca omite o verbo**: *"O mercado volátil"*
vira obrigatoriamente *"The market **is** volatile."* (2) Não cole *am/is/are*
antes de um verbo de ação: é *"I agree"*, não *"I am agree"*. (3) O inglês
**não** distingue *ser* de *estar* — os dois viram *am/is/are*, então você nunca
precisa escolher; a única atenção é lembrar que **you** e plurais pedem **are**,
não *is*.
