# Unit 019 — I have done and I did (1)

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson — **Unit 018: for, since and ago** — explained how these three words anchor time differently. The Slack incident post-mortem showed: *"The alert fired **two hours ago**"* (past simple — looking back at the event) vs *"The service has been recovering **for the last thirty minutes**"* (present perfect — duration still ongoing) vs *"Since then, error rates have been dropping"* (present perfect — from that point until now).

Three rapid recall questions:

1. Fill in the blank (and choose the correct tense): *"We released the hotfix ___ (10 minutes ago / for 10 minutes). Which word requires past simple?"*
2. Which is correct? *"She has worked here ___ 2023."* — **for** or **since**?
3. Translate and pick the right structure: *"Comecei o projeto há três meses."* → does this need **ago** or **for**?

👉 **Answers:**
1. ✅ **10 minutes ago** → past simple: *"We released the hotfix ten minutes ago."* (looking back at the moment)
2. ✅ **since** — 2023 is a starting point, not a quantity of time
3. ✅ **ago** + past simple: *"I started the project three months ago."* (looking back at the event)

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — after a major release:**
>
> **@ana (EM):** Has anyone reviewed the PR for the auth service refactor? 🔍
>
> **@dev1:** I **reviewed** it this morning — left comments about the token expiry logic.
>
> **@ana:** Great. **Have you seen** the security scan results yet?
>
> **@dev1:** Not yet. Did they finish running?
>
> **@ana:** Yes! They **finished** about an hour ago — no critical issues. 🎉
>
> **@dev2:** **Did you include** the new rate-limiting middleware in that PR?
>
> **@dev1:** Yes, I **included** it last week when I wrote the first draft. But I **haven't merged** it yet — waiting for the approval.

Notice the pattern:
- *"I **reviewed** it this morning"* — **this morning** is a specific finished slot of time → past simple
- *"**Have you seen** the scan results yet?"* — no specific time given; asking about *any* moment up to now → present perfect
- *"They **finished** about an hour ago"* — **ago** points back to a specific moment → past simple
- *"I **haven't merged** it yet"* — still relevant right now, the result matters today → present perfect

---

### The rule

The difference between present perfect and past simple often comes down to one question: **is the time finished, or is it still connected to now?**

| Tense | When to use | Signal words |
|---|---|---|
| **Present perfect** (I have done) | The time period is **not finished**, OR the result/relevance is **still now**, OR no specific past time given | today, this week, this year, ever, never, just, already, yet, recently, so far |
| **Past simple** (I did) | The time is **finished** and we're looking back at a specific moment or period | yesterday, last week, last month, in 2022, two days ago, when I was… |

**The same fact — two different frames:**

| Frame | Sentence | Tense |
|---|---|---|
| Result matters **now** | *"I **have lost** my SSH key."* (I don't have it now → problem) | present perfect |
| **When** it happened | *"I **lost** my SSH key last month."* (specific past time) | past simple |

> ⚠️ **One-sentence test:** If you can naturally add *yesterday / last week / in 2022*, use the past simple. If removing the time completely still makes sense and the connection to *now* is what matters, use the present perfect.

> ⚠️ **Common trap for Portuguese speakers:** In English you can't say *"I have seen him yesterday"* ❌ — "yesterday" forces past simple: *"I saw him yesterday."* ✅ The present perfect is never used with a finished-time marker.

### Time expression quick-sort

**→ Past simple:**
yesterday · last night · last week · last month · last year · in 2022 · two days ago · when I was… · at 3 pm · on Tuesday

**→ Present perfect:**
today · this morning (if still morning) · this week · this year · ever · never · just · already · yet · recently · so far · up to now

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The engineering team has shipped five features this quarter — and the quarter isn't over yet."** *(present perfect — this quarter is still ongoing)*
2. **"We shipped the real-time collaboration feature last quarter after six weeks of development."** *(past simple — last quarter is finished)*
3. **"Have you ever designed a system that handles more than a million requests per second?"** *(present perfect — any time in your career, no specific date)*
4. **"She designed the event-sourcing architecture when she was a Staff engineer at her previous company."** *(past simple — specific past context, she's no longer there)*
5. **"I haven't reviewed the ADR for the new caching layer yet — it just landed in the queue."** *(present perfect — still relevant now, yet = not happened up to this moment)*
6. **"He reviewed the ADR last Friday and approved it with two minor suggestions."** *(past simple — last Friday is a finished point in time)*
7. **"Our AI model has already improved recall by 12% this week after the fine-tuning run."** *(present perfect — this week is still in progress, result is now)*
8. **"I called my mom last Sunday — we talked for almost two hours."** *(past simple — last Sunday is a finished day)*

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose **present perfect** or **past simple** and write the correct form of the verb in brackets:

*"Our team ___ (complete) three on-call rotations this month and the month isn't over yet. Last month, we ___ (have) a major incident — a database failover ___ (go) wrong on a Friday night. Since then, the platform team ___ (rewrite) the failover runbook completely. Have you ___ (read) the new version yet?"*

✅ **Answer:**
*"Our team **has completed** three on-call rotations this month and the month isn't over yet. Last month, we **had** a major incident — a database failover **went** wrong on a Friday night. Since then, the platform team **has rewritten** the failover runbook completely. Have you **read** the new version yet?"*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of two modes:

| Mode | Verb | Question it answers |
|---|---|---|
| **📡 Live signal** | present perfect | *"What's the situation right now?"* |
| **📁 Archived record** | past simple | *"What happened, and when?"* |

> *"I **have deployed** the fix."* → 📡 Live signal: the system is patched **now**.
> *"I **deployed** the fix at 2 am on Tuesday."* → 📁 Archived: the event is in the log.

When a timestamp appears (Monday, last sprint, in 2024), it's **filing** the event into the archive → past simple. When there's no timestamp and the result is still live, it's a 📡 live signal → present perfect.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A distinção entre o **present perfect** (I have done) e o **past simple** (I did) é um dos pontos mais importantes do inglês — e um dos que mais gera confusão para falantes de português, porque o português brasileiro raramente usa o equivalente ao present perfect no dia a dia. A regra central: use o **present perfect** quando o período de tempo **ainda não terminou** (*this week, today, this year*) ou quando o resultado da ação ainda importa agora — sem precisar citar quando aconteceu. Use o **past simple** quando você está se referindo a um momento ou período **concluído** no passado — especialmente com marcadores de tempo como *yesterday, last week, in 2022, two days ago*.

O erro mais comum é combinar o present perfect com um marcador de tempo passado específico: *"I have seen him yesterday"* ❌ não existe em inglês. Se você tem o *when*, use o simple past: *"I saw him yesterday"* ✅. Outra armadilha: em português, *"terminamos três features esse trimestre"* pode usar o presente do indicativo normalmente, mas em inglês você precisa escolher — se o trimestre ainda está acontecendo, use present perfect: *"We have shipped three features this quarter"* ✅. Se o trimestre já fechou, use past simple: *"We shipped three features last quarter"* ✅. O presente simples em português frequentemente esconde um present perfect em inglês — fique atento a essa diferença sempre que falar sobre resultados recentes.
