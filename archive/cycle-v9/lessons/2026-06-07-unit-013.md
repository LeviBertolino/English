# Unit 013 — I didn't... / Did you...? (Past Simple negative and questions)

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson was **Unit 012 — worked/got/went etc. (Past Simple — positive forms)**. You practiced sentences like *"The AI team ran a series of A/B tests"*, *"She sent the report to management"*, and *"We built the new onboarding pipeline from scratch"*. You also learned the three -ed pronunciations: /t/ (*worked*), /d/ (*reviewed*), and /ɪd/ (*started*).

Three rapid recall questions:

1. From yesterday's sprint wrap-up dialogue: *"The infra engineer \_\_\_ (go) through the entire runbook and \_\_\_ (update) it."* — Fill in the past simple forms.
2. ❌ *"She sended the report to management."* — What is the correct past form of *send*?
3. How do you pronounce the -ed ending in *deployed*? What about *started*? What's the difference and why?

👉 **Answers:**
1. **went** (irregular) / **updated** (regular: update → updated, add -d only)
2. **sent** — *send* is irregular: send → sent (same pattern as spend → spent, lend → lent)
3. *Deployed* ends in /d/ — the base verb *deploy* ends in a voiced sound. *Started* ends in /ɪd/ — an extra syllable because the base verb *start* ends in /t/. The rule: only add the full /ɪd/ when the base ends in /t/ or /d/.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — Monday morning, post-incident:**
>
> **Levi:** *"Hey — did the on-call engineer see the alert before the outage hit?"*
>
> **Ana:** *"She saw it, but she didn't have time to escalate. The pipeline didn't fail immediately — it degraded slowly."*
>
> **Levi:** *"Did the monitoring dashboard show the error rate spike?"*
>
> **Ana:** *"No, it didn't. The dashboard didn't update in time because the caching layer blocked the metrics stream."*
>
> **Levi:** *"OK. So the fix worked — but why didn't anyone test the caching layer before the release?"*

Notice the pattern: *did… see*, *didn't have*, *didn't fail*, *Did… show*, *didn't update*, *didn't test*. The same auxiliary verb — **did / didn't** — handles both questions and negatives in the past simple. That's today's rule.

### The rule: Past Simple — Negatives and Questions

In Unit 012, you learned the positive forms: *worked, went, built, sent*. To make a **negative** or a **question**, English uses a helper verb: **did** (and its negative **didn't**).

#### Negatives → subject + **didn't** + base verb (infinitive)

| Positive | Negative |
|---|---|
| She **saw** the alert. | She **didn't see** the alert. |
| The pipeline **failed**. | The pipeline **didn't fail**. |
| We **built** the feature. | We **didn't build** the feature. |
| I **went** to the all-hands. | I **didn't go** to the all-hands. |

> ⚠️ **Critical rule**: after *didn't*, always use the **base form** (infinitive) — **not** the past form.
> ❌ *"She didn't saw the alert."* → ✅ *"She didn't see the alert."*
> ❌ *"We didn't built it."* → ✅ *"We didn't build it."*

#### Questions → **Did** + subject + base verb?

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| The dashboard **showed** the spike. | **Did** the dashboard **show** the spike? |
| They **tested** the layer. | **Did** they **test** the layer? |
| He **went** to the demo. | **Did** he **go** to the demo? |

**Short answers:**
- *Did the monitoring work?* → **Yes, it did. / No, it didn't.**
- *Did you find the bug?* → **Yes, I did. / No, I didn't.**

> 🔑 One rule covers everything: **did/didn't + base verb** for all negatives and questions — regular *and* irregular verbs alike.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The team didn't finish the feature in time — the scope grew by 30% in the last sprint."** — negative, tech context
2. **"Did the code reviewer catch the race condition before the PR was merged?"** — question, code review
3. **"I didn't deploy to production on Friday — we never push changes before the weekend."** — negative, engineering practice
4. **"Did the Staff engineer present the RFC at the architecture review last Thursday?"** — question, engineering leadership

6. **"Did the AI model perform better after you fine-tuned it on the domain-specific dataset?"** — question, ML engineering
7. **"She didn't go to the conference last year, but she watched all the recorded talks online."** — negative, everyday/professional
8. **"I didn't sleep well the night before the system migration — I kept thinking about the rollback plan."** — negative, everyday life

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"She didn't saw the error in the logs."** → ✅ **"She didn't see the error in the logs."**
*Why: after* didn't, *always use the base form (infinitive). The past tense is already in* didn't *(=* did + not*). Using the past form twice is redundant and wrong.*

❌ **"Did he went to the post-mortem?"** → ✅ **"Did he go to the post-mortem?"**
*Why: same rule —* Did *already carries the past meaning, so the main verb returns to its base form. This trips up learners who know "went" is the past of "go" and try to use it in questions.*

❌ **"He not deploy the fix."** → ✅ **"He didn't deploy the fix."**
*Why: in English, you cannot form a negative without an auxiliary verb. You can't just add* not *directly to a main verb (unlike Portuguese: "Ele não fez"). You need* didn't *to carry both the past tense and the negation.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**"Did" kidnaps the past tense.**

> When you add *did* or *didn't* to a sentence, it **steals** the past tense marker. The main verb is left with nothing — it goes back to the base form.
>
> Think of it as a slot: only **one** past marker per sentence.
> → *She **went*** — one marker, in the verb.
> → *She **didn't** go* — one marker, in *didn't*. The verb is bare.
> → ***Did** she **go**?* — one marker, in *Did*. The verb is bare.

This mental model works for every past simple sentence with *did/didn't*, whether the verb is regular or irregular.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word/phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| didn't | /ˈdɪdnt/ | 2 syllables; the /t/ is often almost silent in fast speech ⚠️ |
| did you | /ˈdɪdʒu/ | in natural speech, "did you" merges into /dɪdʒu/ ⚠️ |
| did he/she | /dɪd i/ / /dɪd ʃiː/ | no merging here — keep both syllables |
| didn't catch | /ˈdɪdnt kæʧ/ | the /t/ of *didn't* can disappear before /k/ ⚠️ |
| didn't go | /ˈdɪdnt ɡoʊ/ | smooth connection: *didn't-go* |
| deploy | /dɪˈplɔɪ/ | stress on second syllable; base form after *didn't* |
| configure | /kənˈfɪɡjər/ | stress on second syllable ⚠️ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"didn't" — don't add a vowel** ⚠️: Brazilians sometimes say "didji-not" or "deednit" (inserting vowels). The correct form is **/ˈdɪdnt/** — *did-nt*, two syllables, ending in a consonant cluster /dnt/. The /t/ at the end is very soft in natural speech but shouldn't become a full vowel.
- **"did you" → /dɪdʒu/** ⚠️: in fluent speech, "did you" fuses into one unit that sounds like *"didja"* (/dɪdʒu/). This is normal and correct, not sloppy. Practice the fusion: *"Didja see it?"*, *"Didja test it?"*
- **Base verb after "didn't"** — no extra sound ⚠️: Brazilians sometimes say "didn't deployed" (adding the Portuguese past tense instinct). After *didn't*, there's no -ed, no -iu, nothing — just the bare base: *"didn't deploy."*
- **Stress placement**: in *didn't*, stress falls on the first syllable: **DID**-nt. In questions, stress the key information word, not *did*: *"Did they **test** it?"* not *"**Did** they test it?"*

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **past simple negativo e interrogativo** usa o auxiliar **did** para carregar o tempo verbal, liberando o verbo principal para voltar à forma base (infinitivo sem *to*). Para **negar**: sujeito + **didn't** + verbo na forma base — por exemplo: *"She didn't see the alert"* (não *"didn't saw"*). Para **perguntas**: **Did** + sujeito + verbo na forma base — por exemplo: *"Did the dashboard show the spike?"* (não *"Did the dashboard showed"*). As **respostas curtas** são: *Yes, it did / No, it didn't*. Essa regra vale para **todos os verbos**, regulares e irregulares — o *did/didn't* já carrega todo o passado, então o verbo principal fica na forma base.

Na pronúncia, o ponto mais crítico é a contração **/ˈdɪdnt/** ("didn't"): dois sílabas, terminando em cluster consonantal /dnt/. Evite inserir vogais como "deednit" ou "didji-not". Outra fusão importante: *"did you"* em fala natural vira **/dɪdʒu/** (como "didja") — isso é pronúncia fluente e correta, não erro. Lembre também que, depois de *didn't*, o verbo não leva -ed nem nenhum sufixo de passado: *"didn't deploy"*, não *"didn't deployed"*. Por fim, ao fazer perguntas, o acento cai na palavra de **conteúdo informacional**, não em *did*: *"Did they **test** it?"* — com a ênfase em *test*.
