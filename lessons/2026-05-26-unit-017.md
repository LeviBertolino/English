## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — Wednesday afternoon:**
>
> **Pedro:** Hey Levi, I'm stuck on this PR. The integration test keeps failing and I can't figure out why — been at it for two hours.
>
> **Levi:** Send me the link. I**'ll take a look** right now.
>
> **Pedro:** Thanks. Also — the on-call doc is outdated. Someone should update the runbook.
>
> **Levi:** I**'ll handle that** tonight. It won't take long.
>
> **Pedro:** One more thing — should I block the release until the test is fixed?
>
> **Levi:** Don't block it. I**'ll talk to** the release manager and explain the situation. We**'ll find** a workaround.
>
> **Pedro:** You're the best. Oh, and happy birthday by the way!
>
> **Levi:** Ha — thanks! I**'ll buy** the team lunch tomorrow to celebrate.

Notice something about every *will* in that conversation: none of these were decisions Levi made before the conversation started. Pedro raised each problem, and Levi decided **at that exact moment** what to do. That's the core job of *will (1)*.

### The rule

**"Will"** in this unit covers three closely related uses — all involving a decision made **right now**, in the moment of speaking.

---

**Use 1 — Spontaneous decisions (decided this second)**

You just heard something, someone just asked you something, or you just realised you need to act — and you decide immediately. No prior plan.

> *"The CI is broken." — "I'll restart the runner."* ← decided right now
> *"We're out of runway for Q2." — "I'll reprioritise the backlog."* ← immediate decision
> *"The client wants a demo on Friday." — "I'll prepare the environment tonight."* ← no prior plan, decided just now

Compare with *going to*: if the meeting was already on your calendar, you'd say *"I'm going to prepare the environment tonight"* — that was a plan before this moment.

---

**Use 2 — Offers**

You see a need and volunteer to help. You're not obligated — you're choosing to step up.

> *"I'll review your PR before you go to bed."*
> *"Let me know if you're stuck — I'll pair with you on it."*
> *"You look swamped. I'll take the on-call rotation this weekend."*

---

**Use 3 — Promises and commitments**

You're giving your word that something will happen. The listener can count on it.

> *"I'll have the architecture doc ready by Thursday."*
> *"We won't ship this feature without proper rollback testing — I promise."*
> *"I'll let you know as soon as the deployment goes green."*

---

**Forms**

| Subject | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / you / he / she / we / they | I'll help | I won't help | Will you help? |

- **'ll** is the spoken contraction — always use it in natural conversation. *"I will"* (uncontracted) sounds unusually emphatic or formal.
- **won't** = will not — the only irregular contraction here.
- *Will* is the same for **all subjects** — no *wills*, no *willing* after a subject.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The pull request looks good overall — I'll approve it after I check the migration script."** — spontaneous decision made while reading the PR.
2. **"I'll set up the observability dashboard for the new service — send me the endpoint and I'll wire it up."** — offer made after hearing about a gap.
3. **"We won't merge this to main until the integration tests pass. That's non-negotiable."** — firm promise/commitment.
4. **"Will you join the post-mortem on Thursday? We need the incident commander's perspective."** — asking about willingness/offer.
5. **"The on-call alert just fired — I'll acknowledge it and start the runbook."** — spontaneous, reactive decision made this second.
6. **"I'll walk you through the new deployment flow in our next 1:1 — it's confusing until you've done it once."** — offer to help a team member.
7. **"My laptop just died during the presentation. I'll borrow yours — thanks."** — immediate decision triggered by a new situation.
8. **"I'll send you a calendar invite right now."** — spontaneous decision made while speaking (or typing).

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I'm going to take a look at it right now"** (when you just heard the problem and decided) → ✅ **"I'll take a look at it right now"**
*Why: "going to" implies you already planned to look at it. If the decision is instant — triggered by what someone just said — "will" is more natural. Using "going to" for a snap decision sounds like you had a hidden agenda.*

❌ **"I will to review your PR"** → ✅ **"I'll review your PR"**
*Why: "will" is a modal verb — the main verb after it is always in the base form with NO "to." The "to" is not added here (unlike "going to," which does require "to").*

❌ **"I won't to approve this until the tests pass"** → ✅ **"I won't approve this until the tests pass"**
*Why: same rule — "won't" + base verb, no "to." Brazilian learners sometimes add "to" by analogy with "going to" or with Portuguese constructions.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of *will* as your **instant reflex** and *going to* as your **GPS route**.

- *Will*: you didn't plan the route — someone just asked you a question and your hand shoots up. *"I'll do it."* The decision is **born right now**.
- *Going to*: the route was already loaded before you spoke. The plan existed before this moment.

A quick self-check: *"Did I decide this before I started talking, or did I just decide it?"*
- **Just decided** → *will*
- **Already decided** → *going to*

> *"The build just broke. I'll take a look."* ← just decided (will)
> *"I'm going to refactor the build pipeline this sprint."* ← already on the plan (going to)

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| will | /wɪl/ | Short, crisp — not /wiːl/ (that's "wheel") ⚠️ |
| 'll (contraction) | /l/ or /əl/ | Almost silent — *"I'll"* = /aɪl/ |
| won't | /wəʊnt/ | Rhymes with "don't" — NOT /wɒnt/ (that's "want") ⚠️ |
| I'll | /aɪl/ | One syllable — "aisle" |
| you'll | /juːl/ | One syllable — like "yule" |
| he'll / she'll | /hiːl/ / /ʃiːl/ | One syllable each |
| we'll | /wiːl/ | One syllable — rhymes with "feel" |
| offer | /ˈɒfər/ | British: /ˈɒfə/ — stress on first syllable |
| promise | /ˈprɒmɪs/ | *PROM-iss* — stress on first syllable |
| approve | /əˈpruːv/ | Stress on second syllable: *a-PROVE* ⚠️ |
| acknowledge | /əkˈnɒlɪdʒ/ | *ack-NOL-idge* — stress on second syllable |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **won't vs. want** ⚠️: This is a very common mix-up. *"Won't"* /wəʊnt/ rhymes with "don't." *"Want"* /wɒnt/ rhymes with "font." Practice the contrast: *"I won't do it"* vs. *"I want to do it"* — completely different vowel sounds.
- **'ll contraction** ⚠️: Native speakers almost always use *'ll* in natural speech. Saying *"I will take a look"* (uncontracted) sounds formal or emphatic — like you're making a point of it. Practice gliding from the subject directly into 'll: *I'll, you'll, she'll, we'll, they'll*.
- **will vs. "wheel"** ⚠️: Portuguese doesn't distinguish /ɪ/ (as in "will") from /iː/ (as in "wheel"). *Will* has a short, relaxed /ɪ/ — your tongue is lower and more central than for /iː/. Try saying "i" as in "kit" rather than "ee" as in "feet."
- **"approve" stress** ⚠️: As with "deploy" yesterday, the stress is on the second syllable: *a-PROVE*, not *AP-prove*.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O *will* desta unidade tem três usos principais — todos ligados a decisões tomadas **no momento em que você fala**: (1) **decisões espontâneas**, quando você acabou de ouvir algo e decide na hora o que fazer (*"I'll take a look"* = decidi agora, não antes); (2) **ofertas**, quando você percebe que alguém precisa de ajuda e se oferece (*"I'll review your PR"*); e (3) **promessas e compromissos**, quando você dá a sua palavra de que algo vai acontecer (*"I'll have the doc ready by Thursday"*). O ponto-chave que diferencia o *will* do *going to*: se a decisão já existia antes de você começar a falar, use *going to*. Se nasceu agora, neste instante, use *will*.

Na forma, o *will* é simples: igual para todos os sujeitos (*I will, she will, we will* — sem -s), sempre seguido de verbo na forma base sem "to" (*"I'll review"*, nunca *"I'll to review"*), e a contração *'ll* é usada quase sempre na fala natural. A maior armadilha de pronúncia para brasileiros é o par *won't* /wəʊnt/ (rima com "don't") vs. *want* /wɒnt/ (rima com "font") — são vogais completamente diferentes e a confusão pode mudar o sentido radicalmente. Pratique os dois até ficarem automáticos.
