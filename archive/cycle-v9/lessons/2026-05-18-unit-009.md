## 1. 📘 Unit 009 — Have... and I've got...

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Hiring manager:** Do you have experience with distributed tracing?
> **Levi:** Yes — I've got about four years working with OpenTelemetry. I also have a background in observability platforms.
> **Hiring manager:** Has your team got a standard for incident response?
> **Levi:** We do. We have a runbook for every service, and I've got templates I can share.
> **Hiring manager:** Do you have time this week for a follow-up call?
> **Levi:** I don't have anything on Thursday. Does that work?

Notice *do you have*, *I've got*, *I have*, *has your team got*, *I don't have* — these express the same idea in slightly different forms. They're interchangeable in most cases.

### The rule

**"Have"** and **"have got"** both express possession, relationships, and characteristics. They mean the same thing.

| Form | Positive | Question | Negative |
|---|---|---|---|
| **have** | I have a meeting. | Do you have a Mac? | I don't have Slack. |
| **have got** | I've got a meeting. | Have you got a Mac? | I haven't got Slack. |

**Key points:**

- **"Have got"** is more common in **spoken British English** and informal contexts. *"I've got a bug to fix."*
- **"Have"** (with *do/does*) is more common in **American English** and all **formal writing** — emails, documentation, pull request descriptions, technical specs.
- **Never mix the two in the same question:** ❌ *"Do you have got…?"* — pick one system.
- **Contractions are standard** with "have got": *I've got / she's got / we've got / they've got.*
- **"Has got"** is third-person singular: *"The service has got a memory leak."* = *"The service has a memory leak."*
- Both forms work for the **present** only. For past, use *had* (not *had got*): *"We had an outage yesterday."*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **The platform team has got a new on-call rotation** starting next quarter.
2. **Do you have access to the production logs**, or do you need me to share the Grafana link?
3. **I've got a conflict in my schedule** — can we move the architecture review to Thursday?
4. **Our AI model has a latency issue** under high concurrency; we haven't got a fix yet.
5. **Has the repo got a CODEOWNERS file**, or does everyone approve their own PRs?
6. **We have an SLA of 99.9%**, and right now we don't have the monitoring to back it up.
7. **I've got two interviews this week** and a performance review on Friday — it's a full calendar.
8. **She doesn't have a car** — she cycles to the office every day.

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"Do you have got the credentials?"** → ✅ **"Do you have the credentials?"** or **"Have you got the credentials?"**
*Why: Never combine the two systems. "Do you have" and "Have you got" are parallel structures — pick one.*

❌ **"I haved a look at the PR"** → ✅ **"I had a look at the PR"**
*Why: "Have" as an action verb (have a look, have a meeting, have a problem) uses simple past "had" — not "haved". This is irregular.*

❌ **"She has got a problem yesterday"** → ✅ **"She had a problem yesterday"**
*Why: "Have got" exists only in the present. For past, use "had". No exceptions.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **"have got"** as the **casual spoken shortcut** and **"have"** as the **formal written standard**. 

A quick rule of thumb: if you're **speaking** or texting a teammate → either works, "have got" sounds natural. If you're **writing** — documentation, an email, a PR description — use "have" with "do/does/don't/doesn't". That keeps your written English register-appropriate.

Picture it: casual Slack message → *"I've got a question"*; formal RFC document → *"The system has two primary dependencies."*

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and phrases

| Word/Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| I've got | /aɪv ɡɒt/ | "I've" blends smoothly — /aɪv/, not two separate words ⚠️ |
| have got | /həv ɡɒt/ | "have" reduces to /həv/ in connected speech ⚠️ |
| Do you have | /də jə həv/ | Same "do you" reduction as Unit 7 |
| hasn't got | /ˈhæzənt ɡɒt/ | Stress on first syllable: HAS-n't got ⚠️ |
| haven't got | /ˈhævənt ɡɒt/ | Stress: HAV-n't got |
| I don't have | /aɪ doʊnt həv/ | "have" at the end reduces to /həv/ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"I've got"**: The contraction *I've* is /aɪv/ — the "v" blends into the next word. Brazilians often pause between *I've* and *got*, making it sound choppy. Practice running them together: *"aɪvɡɒt"* as nearly one unit. ⚠️
- **The /æ/ vowel in "have" and "hasn't"**: This is the short, open *a* as in *cat* or *bad* — /hæv/, /ˈhæzənt/. In Brazilian Portuguese this sound doesn't exist cleanly; it often becomes /ɛ/ (like "hev") or /a/ (like "hav"). Open the mouth a bit wider and drop the jaw slightly. ⚠️
- **"Got" vowel**: In British English, *got* is /ɡɒt/ — the /ɒ/ is like the *o* in *hot*, rounded and short. In American English it shifts to /ɡɑːt/. Either is fine; just don't say /ɡoʊt/ (that's "goat"). ⚠️
- **Reduction**: In natural speech, *"have you got"* often sounds like *"havya got"* /ˈhævjə ɡɒt/. You don't need to produce this, but you'll hear it.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**"Have"** e **"have got"** expressam posse, características ou relações — e significam a mesma coisa. A diferença é de registro e estilo: *"have got"* é mais comum na fala e no inglês britânico informal (*"I've got a meeting"*), enquanto *"have"* com *do/does/don't/doesn't* é o padrão no inglês americano e na escrita formal (*"Do you have the credentials?"*). O erro mais comum é misturar os dois sistemas na mesma frase — especialmente *"do you have got"*, que não existe. Escolha um sistema e mantenha-o.

Para o passado, os dois viram *"had"* — nunca *"had got"* ou *"haved"*. Na pronúncia, preste atenção em *"I've got"* (/aɪvˈɡɒt/), onde o *"I've"* e o *"got"* se fundem quase como uma palavra só, e no som /æ/ de *"have"* e *"hasn't"*, que é mais aberto do que o *a* do português. Se a diferença de registro ainda parece abstrata: Slack casual → *"have got"*; RFC ou e-mail profissional → *"have"*.
