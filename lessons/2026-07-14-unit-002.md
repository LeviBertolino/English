### 2. 🧠 Mini-context

> **CFO:** **Are** we ready to defend the AI budget on Thursday?
> **You:** **Is** the board expecting exact figures, or a direction?
> **CFO:** A direction. **Am** I right that the pilot is already live?
> **You:** You are. **Why is** it still framed as an experiment, then?

Notice the shape before any rule: to ask, the verb **jumps to the front** —
*we are* becomes ***are* we**, *the board is* becomes ***is** the board*, *I am*
becomes ***am** I*. Nothing else moves; the verb just leads.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

Yesterday you built statements. A **question** is the same sentence with the
subject and the *to be* verb **swapped**: the verb goes **first**.

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| We **are** ready. | **Are** we ready? |
| The board **is** aligned. | **Is** the board aligned? |
| I **am** right. | **Am** I right? |

**With a question word** (*what, where, why, who, how*), the word comes first,
then the verb, then the subject:

| Question word | + verb + subject |
|---|---|
| **Where** | **is** the CFO? |
| **Why** | **are** they cautious? |
| **How** | **is** the pilot going? |

**Short answers** repeat the verb — don't just say *"Yes"*:
*Is the budget approved?* → **Yes, it is. / No, it isn't.**
*Are we ready?* → **Yes, we are. / No, we aren't.**

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não

**Why does this pattern exist?** Because English marks a question by **word
order**, not just by tone. Moving *am/is/are* in front of the subject is the
signal that turns a description into a request for information. Get the
inversion automatic and every *to be* question — status checks, clarifications,
challenges in a meeting — comes out right.

**Decision rule — inversion vs. "do you…?"** This is the trap that matters most.
*To be* questions invert the verb itself. **Action verbs** don't — they borrow
**do/does**. So the rival isn't another *to be* form; it's the *do*-question you
must **not** use here.

| Verb type | Question | ✅ / ❌ |
|---|---|---|
| *to be* (are) | **Are** you confident? | ✅ |
| *to be* — wrong helper | **Do** you be confident? | ❌ |
| action (agree) | **Do** you agree? | ✅ |
| action — wrong inversion | **Agree** you? | ❌ |

Rule of thumb: if the statement uses *am/is/are*, the question just **flips**
it — never add *do*. If the statement uses an action verb (*agree, invest,
lead*), the question needs *do/does*.

**PT→EN mapping.** In Portuguese you can ask a question with **no change of word
order at all** — *"Estamos prontos?"* is *"Estamos prontos"* with a rising tone.
English does **not** allow that in careful speech or writing: *"We are ready?"*
read flat is not how you ask — you must invert to *"Are we ready?"*. The
interference is Levi keeping statement order and leaning on intonation, the way
Portuguese lets you.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *You are available tomorrow?* → ✅ ***Are** you available tomorrow?* —
  *a real question inverts the verb; don't just rely on a rising tone the way
  Portuguese does.*
- ❌ *Do you be ready for the board?* → ✅ ***Are** you ready for the board?* —
  *never add* do *to a* to be *question; flip* are *instead.*
- ❌ *Why the market is so volatile?* → ✅ *Why **is** the market so volatile?* —
  *after a question word, the verb still comes before the subject.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. **Are** we positioned as an AI-first company, or still catching up? — *"we"
   statement uses* are, *so the question just flips to* are we; *no* do.*
2. **Is** the innovation portfolio balanced across horizons? — *single subject
   ("the portfolio") → is, moved to the front to ask.*
3. **Am** I the right sponsor for this transformation? — *"I" → am; even* am *inverts
   the same way to form the question.*
4. **Where are** the biggest risks in the AI roadmap? — *question word first,
   then* are, *then the plural subject "risks".*
5. **Why is** the board still cautious about the investment? — *"why" + is +
   subject; the verb stays before "the board", not after.*
6. **How is** the pilot performing so far? — *asking about state with* how; *the
   natural way to request a status update in a sync.*
7. **Is** the CFO in London this week, or back at HQ? — *location question;* to be
   *also asks "where", inverted to* is the CFO.*
8. **Are** the kids at school today? — *everyday life; plural "kids" → are, flipped
   to the front to ask.*

### 8. 🗣️ Para levar para a aula

- Ask your tutor to run a **rapid-fire status check**: they give you a statement
  (*"The pilot is live," "The directors are cautious," "You are the sponsor"*)
  and you flip each one into a question in under two seconds — *"Is the pilot
  live?"* Goal: make the inversion automatic under time pressure.
- Then a **do-vs-be sorting round**: your tutor mixes *to be* prompts with
  action-verb prompts (*"you / confident"* vs *"you / agree"*) and you ask the
  right question — *"Are you confident?"* vs *"Do you agree?"* — so you stop
  reaching for *do* where the verb should just flip.

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** Para fazer uma pergunta com *to be*, você **inverte**:
o verbo (*am/is/are*) vai **para a frente** do sujeito — *"We are ready"* vira
*"**Are** we ready?"*. Com palavra interrogativa (*what, where, why, how*), a
ordem é **palavra + verbo + sujeito**: *"**Why is** the market volatile?"*.
Respostas curtas repetem o verbo: *"Yes, it **is**. / No, we **aren't**."* — não
responda só *"Yes"*.

**As armadilhas.** (1) O inglês **não** aceita pergunta só pela entonação como o
português (*"Estamos prontos?"*): você **precisa** inverter — *"Are we ready?"*,
não *"We are ready?"* dito com tom de pergunta. (2) **Nunca** use *do* com *to
be*: é *"Are you confident?"*, jamais *"Do you be confident?"* — o *do* é para
verbos de ação (*"Do you agree?"*). (3) Depois da palavra interrogativa, o verbo
continua **antes** do sujeito: *"Why **is** the pilot delayed?"*, não *"Why the
pilot is delayed?"*.
