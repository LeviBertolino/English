# Unit 010 — was/were

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday you studied **Unit 009: I have… and I've got…** — two structures that express possession in English.

From that lesson: *"Our AI team has three H100 GPUs in the on-prem cluster — we've got enough compute for the fine-tuning runs."*

Three rapid recall questions:

1. Correct or incorrect? *"Does she have got a laptop?"* Why?
2. Fill in: *"The new microservice \_\_\_ a circuit-breaker pattern — \_\_\_ (it) retry logic and timeout handling out of the box."*
3. Which sentence is wrong and why? a) *"I've got lunch at noon every day."* b) *"I've got a new terminal setup."* c) *"They haven't got production access."*

👉 **Answers:**
1. ❌ Incorrect — two systems mixed. Choose one: *"Does she have a laptop?"* OR *"Has she got a laptop?"* — never *does + have got* together.
2. **has** / **it's got** (both *have* forms work; *have got* only for possession states)
3. **(a)** is wrong — *have got* doesn't work for habits/routines. Use *"I have lunch at noon every day."*

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Post-mortem Slack thread — Monday morning:**
>
> *Alex: "Hey Levi, the deploy last Friday was a mess. Were you online when the alert fired?"*
>
> *Levi: "I was in a meeting, actually. The on-call engineer was Ana — she was the first to notice. The metrics weren't right from the start, but the deploy pipeline was green, so nobody stopped it."*
>
> *Alex: "Were the feature flags disabled at least?"*
>
> *Levi: "They were. That's why the blast radius was small. But the rollback was slow — the infra team wasn't ready for that kind of failure scenario."*
>
> *Alex: "Was there a runbook?"*
>
> *Levi: "There wasn't. That's the main action item now."*

Notice: **was**, **were**, **wasn't**, **weren't** — these are all past forms of *be*. They're doing the same job as *am/is/are*, just shifted into the past.

### The rule

**was** and **were** are the simple past tense of the verb *be*.

| Subject | Present | Past |
|---|---|---|
| I | am | **was** |
| he / she / it | is | **was** |
| you | are | **were** |
| we | are | **were** |
| they | are | **were** |

**Memory hook:** I/he/she/it → **was** (singular). You/we/they → **were** (plural).

#### Negative forms

| Subject | Full form | Contracted |
|---|---|---|
| I / he / she / it | was not | **wasn't** |
| you / we / they | were not | **weren't** |

#### Question forms

| Subject | Question |
|---|---|
| I / he / she / it | **Was** I / he / she / it…? |
| you / we / they | **Were** you / we / they…? |

**Short answers:**
- *"Was the deploy successful?"* → *"Yes, it **was**."* / *"No, it **wasn't**."*
- *"Were the tests passing?"* → *"Yes, they **were**."* / *"No, they **weren't**."*

> **Key trap:** *you* always takes **were** — even when referring to a single person. *"Were you at the standup?"* ✅ — never *"Was you…?"* ❌

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"The deployment was successful, but the rollback procedure wasn't well-documented."** — post-mortem language, engineering
2. **"Were the integration tests passing before the merge? They weren't — that's why the pipeline broke."** — code review context
3. **"She was the on-call engineer when the incident happened; her response time was under four minutes."** — incident report, tech leadership
4. **"The API latency was acceptable in staging, but it wasn't in production."** — performance testing
5. **"Were you in the architecture review yesterday? It was a really useful session."** — work meeting, question form
6. **"The team wasn't aware of the schema change — it wasn't in the migration notes."** — engineering, double negative
7. **"I was at the gym when my phone buzzed — it was my manager asking about the outage."** — everyday life with work crossover
8. **"The coffee was terrible at the conference, but the talks were excellent."** — everyday life, *was* vs *were* contrast

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Fill in **was**, **were**, **wasn't**, or **weren't**:

> The Friday deploy \_\_\_ (1) supposed to be low-risk, but the new service \_\_\_ (2) ready for the traffic spike — it \_\_\_ (3) only tested with synthetic load. The engineers \_\_\_ (4) notified about the change, but the warnings \_\_\_ (5) clear enough. \_\_\_ (6) there a rollback plan? Yes, there \_\_\_ (7), but the steps \_\_\_ (8) too slow.

✅ **Answer:**
1. **was** (*deploy* — singular)
2. **wasn't** (negative, singular)
3. **was** (*it* — singular, positive)
4. **were** (*engineers* — plural)
5. **weren't** (*warnings* — plural, negative)
6. **Was** (question, singular *there*)
7. **was** (positive short answer)
8. **were** (*steps* — plural)

### Production task

*Write 2 sentences about a past project, system, or meeting — one positive and one negative or question.*

**Model answer:**
1. *"The first version of our recommendation system was slow and difficult to maintain — the architecture wasn't designed for scale."*
2. *"Were the stakeholders aligned on the timeline before the sprint started? They weren't — that's why the scope kept changing."*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**I/HE/SHE/IT → WAS** (one entity, one *s* — wa**s**). **YOU/WE/THEY → WERE** (more entities, more letters). The one exception to drill: *you* is always **were**, even for a single person — because English treats *you* as grammatically plural, always.

## 6. 🚀 Daily Production Challenge (self-directed)

Today, think back to a past project, incident, sprint, or meeting. Describe it briefly in a Slack message, PR comment, retrospective note, or journal entry. Use at least one **was/were** and one **wasn't/weren't**.

Starter prompt: *"The [project/meeting/incident] was… It wasn't… Were you/we…?"*

This kind of past-state language appears constantly in post-mortems, retrospectives, status updates, and architecture discussions — it's high-frequency writing for any engineering leader.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**Was** e **were** são as formas do passado simples do verbo *be* (ser/estar). A regra é direta: **I/he/she/it → was**; **you/we/they → were**. Nas formas negativas, usamos as contrações *wasn't* e *weren't*. Nas perguntas, invertemos sujeito e verbo: *"Was the deploy successful?"*, *"Were you online?"*. Nas respostas curtas, repetimos apenas o auxiliar: *"Yes, it was."* / *"No, they weren't."* — nunca repita o verbo principal.

**A armadilha mais comum para brasileiros:** usar *was* com *you*. Em português, "você era" parece singular, então *was* parece natural — mas em inglês, *you* **sempre** usa *were*, mesmo quando se refere a uma única pessoa: *"Were you at the meeting?"* ✅ / *"Was you at the meeting?"* ❌. Outro erro frequente é usar *"it was not"* em contextos informais — em Slack, code reviews e retrospectivas, prefira a contração *"it wasn't"*: soa mais natural e profissional ao mesmo tempo.
