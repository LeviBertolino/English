# Unit 021 — How long have you (been) ...?

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson — **Unit 020: I have done and I did (2)** — deepened the PP→PS handoff pattern and introduced "been to" vs "gone to." From those examples: *"Have you ever worked with GraphQL at scale? — Yes, I worked with it at my previous company"* (PP opens the topic, PS fills in the detail). And the "first/second/third time" rule: *"It's the third time the CI pipeline has timed out this sprint."*

Three rapid recall questions:

1. Why is *"I have seen him yesterday"* ❌ wrong — and what's the correct sentence?
2. Complete: *"___ you ever ___ (lead) a full zero-downtime migration? — Yes, I ___ one in 2023."*
3. True or false: *"She's gone to the design sprint"* means she's already back in the office.

👉 **Answers:**
1. ✅ *"yesterday"* is a closed timestamp → forces past simple: *"I **saw** him yesterday."*
2. ✅ **Have** you ever **led** a full zero-downtime migration? — Yes, I **led** one in 2023.
3. ✅ **False** — *"gone to"* = she left and hasn't returned. *"Been to"* would mean she's back.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **A conversation in a tech-company Slack thread:**
>
> **@Maria (Engineering Manager):** Levi, how long have you **been using** Rust in production?
>
> **@Levi:** About two years — I've **been working** with it since Q3 2024. We started with one small service and now it's powering our data pipeline.
>
> **@Maria:** Wow. And how long has the data pipeline **been running** on Rust?
>
> **@Levi:** It's **been running** for eight months with zero incidents. Honestly, I've **known** Rust would be a good fit for this workload since I first benchmarked it.
>
> **@Maria:** Nice. How long have you **had** that benchmark data?
>
> **@Levi:** I've **had** it since the initial spike, so almost two years now.

Notice:
- *"How long have you **been using** Rust?"* → present perfect continuous — an **action** still happening.
- *"since Q3 2024"* → starting point in the past (the action is still going on now).
- *"for eight months"* → duration (eight months is the length of time).
- *"I've **known** Rust would be a good fit"* → present perfect simple — **state** verbs (*know, have, like, believe*) don't use the continuous.
- *"How long have you **had** that data?"* → *have* is a state verb → always simple, never continuous.

---

### The rules for Unit 021

#### Rule 1 — "for" vs "since"

| Word | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **for** | length of time (duration) | *for eight months / for two years / for a long time / for ages* |
| **since** | starting point | *since Q3 2024 / since Monday / since I joined the company / since the last deploy* |

> *"I've been on this team **for** three years."* ✅
> *"I've been on this team **since** 2023."* ✅
> *"I've been on this team **since** three years."* ❌ (since = point in time, not a duration)
> *"I've been on this team **for** 2023."* ❌ (for = duration, not a year)

#### Rule 2 — Present perfect (simple or continuous) for ongoing situations

To talk about a situation that **started in the past and is still true now**, use present perfect — NOT present simple or past simple:

| Wrong ❌ | Correct ✅ |
|---|---|
| *I am on this team since two years.* | *I've been on this team for two years.* |
| *I know her since we were interns.* | *I've known her since we were interns.* |
| *How long are you using Kubernetes?* | *How long have you been using Kubernetes?* |

> ⚠️ **Brazilian Portuguese trap:** In PT-BR you say *"Estou aqui há dois anos"* (present tense + *há*). In English, you **must** use present perfect: *"I've been here for two years."* Using present simple (*"I am here for two years"*) is a very common Brazilian error.

#### Rule 3 — Continuous vs Simple with "how long"

Use **present perfect continuous** for **activities** (ongoing actions):
> *"I've been **reviewing** pull requests all morning."*
> *"How long have you been **working** on this feature?"*

Use **present perfect simple** for **states** (verbs like *know, have, like, believe, own, understand*):
> *"I've **known** about this bug for weeks."* (NOT *"I've been knowing"* ❌)
> *"How long have you **had** that laptop?"* (NOT *"How long have you been having"* ❌)

**Tip:** If you can't use the verb in present continuous (*"I am knowing"* sounds wrong), you can't use it in present perfect continuous either.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"I've been leading this engineering team for eighteen months — it's been the steepest learning curve of my career."** *(ongoing activity: for + duration)*
2. **"How long has the AI model been running in production? — It's been live since the v2.3 release in April."** *(how long + since + starting point)*
3. **"We've known about the performance regression for two weeks, but we haven't prioritized it yet."** *(state verb: know → simple, not continuous)*
4. **"The new Staff engineer has been onboarding since her first day last Monday — she's picking things up fast."** *(ongoing activity: since + point in time)*
5. **"How long have you had that architecture diagram? I need to check if it's still accurate."** *(state verb: have → simple)*
6. **"I've been learning Rust for about a year and I finally feel comfortable writing async code."** *(ongoing activity: for + duration)*
7. **"She's been the engineering director since the reorg in Q1 — the team culture has improved a lot."** *(ongoing state/role: since + point in time)*
8. **"How long have you been waiting for a response from the security review team?"** *(ongoing activity: how long + continuous)*

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Fill in the blanks with *for*, *since*, or the correct form of the verb in brackets:

*"How long ___ you ___ (work) in the tech industry? — I ___ (be) in tech ___ about twelve years, ___ I graduated and joined my first startup. I ___ (know) from day one that I wanted to build distributed systems — I ___ (have) that same passion ___ over a decade now."*

✅ **Answer:**
*"How long **have** you **been working** in the tech industry? — I **have been** in tech **for** about twelve years, **since** I graduated and joined my first startup. I **have known** from day one that I wanted to build distributed systems — I **have had** that same passion **for** over a decade now."*

*(Note: "been working" = ongoing activity; "known" and "had" = state verbs → simple PP)*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **for** and **since** as two ways to describe a bridge between the past and now:

> **FOR** = the **length** of the bridge 📏 → *"for three years / for ages / for a long time"*
> **SINCE** = where the bridge **starts** 📍 → *"since 2022 / since the last sprint / since I joined"*

And the rule for continuous vs simple:
> If the verb sounds odd in *"I am ___-ing right now"* → use **present perfect simple**.
> *"I am knowing"* ❌ → *"I've known"* ✅
> *"I am working"* ✅ → *"I've been working"* ✅

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A **Unit 021** resolve uma das interferências mais persistentes do português no inglês: em PT-BR, usamos o **presente simples** com *há* para falar de situações que começaram no passado e continuam agora — *"Trabalho aqui há três anos", "Sei disso desde ontem"*. Em inglês, o equivalente obrigatório é o **present perfect**: *"I've been working here for three years"* / *"I've known about this since yesterday."* Nunca use o presente simples nesse contexto: *"I work here since three years"* ❌ soa errado para qualquer falante nativo. A regra é simples: situação que começou no passado e ainda é verdade **agora** → present perfect + *for* ou *since*.

O segundo ponto é distinguir quando usar o **continuous** e quando usar o **simple** com *for/since*. Para **ações** que ainda estão em andamento, use o continuous: *"I've been learning Rust for a year."* Para **verbos de estado** (*know, have, like, believe, understand, own*), use o simple: *"I've known her for ten years"* (não *"I've been knowing"* ❌). O truque: se o verbo soa estranho no presente contínuo (*"I am knowing"*), também vai soar errado no present perfect continuous. A lista dos verbos de estado é curta e vale memorizar — eles funcionam sempre no simple, nunca no continuous.
