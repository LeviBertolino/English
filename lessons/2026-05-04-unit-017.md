## 1. 📘 Unit 017 — Have you ever...?

**Grammar focus:** Present Perfect with *ever* (questions) and *never* (negatives) to talk about life experiences.

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **Hiring manager:** Have you ever led a fully remote engineering team?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, I have. I've managed distributed teams for about three years.
>
> **Manager:** Impressive. Have you ever worked with a team across more than four time zones?
>
> **Levi:** Actually, I've never done that — five time zones would be new for me. But I've
> handled async workflows across three continents.
>
> **Manager:** Honestly, this is the most impressive leadership background I've ever reviewed.

Notice: *Have you ever...?* / *I've never...* / *the most X I've ever...*
All three use **have + past participle**. The time isn't specified — we're asking about *any point in life*.

---

### The Rule

| Structure | Use |
|---|---|
| **Have you ever + past participle?** | Ask about any experience in someone's life |
| **Yes, I have. / No, I haven't.** | Short answers — always use the auxiliary |
| **I have never + past participle** | Say something has not happened at any point in life |
| **This is the [best/worst/most X] I have ever + past participle** | Superlative + life experience |

**Key points:**
- *Ever* = "at any time in your life" → goes **between the subject and the past participle**: *Have **you** ever **seen**...?*
- *Never* = "at no time" → goes **between *have* and the past participle**: *I have **never** seen...*
- Tense is **Present Perfect** because the experience connects the past to the present — it's part of who you are now.
- Short answers never repeat the main verb: ✅ *Yes, I have.* — never ❌ *Yes, I have seen.*

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Using past simple instead of present perfect for life experiences:**
> ❌ Did you ever work on a distributed system?
> ✅ **Have you ever worked** on a distributed system?
>
> *Why:* For experiences without a specific finished time, English requires present perfect, not past simple. "Did you ever..." only sounds natural in informal American speech — stick to "Have you ever..." in professional and written contexts.

**Mistake 2 — Putting *ever* in the wrong position:**
> ❌ Have ever you shipped a feature on a Friday?
> ✅ Have **you ever** shipped a feature on a Friday?
>
> *Why:* Word order is fixed: **Have + [subject] + ever + [past participle]**. *Ever* comes after the subject, never before it.

**Mistake 3 — Dropping *have* in the short answer:**
> ❌ Have you ever seen a 500-line function? — Yes, I seen.
> ✅ Have you ever seen a 500-line function? — Yes, **I have.**
>
> *Why:* Short answers echo the auxiliary verb (*have*), not the main verb. *"I seen"* is never correct in standard English.

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The "search engine" trick:**

Think of *Have you ever...?* as running a **full-text search across someone's entire life**:

> *"Have you ever [verb]?"* = *"Search all memories: did this happen at any point?"*

The search covers everything from birth to now — that's why the time is never specified.
And *never* = **zero results found**.

**Word order trick:** *ever* is the **filling in a sandwich**:
🍞 `Have` — 🥗 `[subject] + ever` — 🍞 `[past participle]`
→ *Have **you ever worked** remotely?*

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| have (weak, in questions) | /həv/ | Reduced vowel — like a mumbled "hev" |
| have (strong, in answers) | /hæv/ | Full vowel — "Yes, I **have**." |
| ever | /ˈɛvər/ | Stress on first syllable |
| never | /ˈnɛvər/ | Stress on first syllable |
| been | /bɪn/ | Short /ɪ/ — NOT /biːn/ in connected speech |
| seen | /siːn/ | Long /iː/ |
| worked | /wɜːrkt/ | Final cluster -rkt |
| done | /dʌn/ | Short /ʌ/ — rhymes with "sun" |
| led | /lɛd/ | Short /ɛ/ — rhymes with "bed" |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — /æ/ in *have* (stressed form)** ⚠️
Portuguese has no /æ/ vowel. It sits between the /a/ in *"cama"* and the /e/ in *"cedo"*.
Mouth tip: open wide for /a/, then push your tongue slightly forward and flatten it.
→ *"have"* ≠ "héve" (too /e/) ≠ "have" (too /a/) — aim for the middle.

**Sound 2 — /ə/ schwa in connected speech** ⚠️
In natural speech, *"Have you ever"* contracts to */həv juː ˈɛvər/* or even */həvjuˈɛvər/*.
Brazilians tend to pronounce each word fully and separately — try to blend them.

**Sound 3 — Final /r/ in *ever* and *never*** ⚠️
In American English (recommended for clarity in international tech contexts), you **do** pronounce the final /r/: *ˈɛv**ər***, *ˈnɛv**ər***.
This /r/ doesn't exist in most Portuguese dialects — practice it separately: *"er... er... ever"*.

**Sound 4 — Past participle endings** ⚠️
- *worked* /wɜːrkt/ — a consonant cluster (-rkt) that's hard for Brazilians. Don't add a vowel: ❌ "worked-ee" → ✅ *wɜːrkt*
- *deployed* /dɪˈplɔɪd/ — the final /d/ is often swallowed, don't add an /i/ sound after it

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
Usamos o presente perfeito com *ever* e *never* para falar sobre experiências de vida — coisas que aconteceram (ou nunca aconteceram) em algum momento da sua vida, sem especificar quando. A estrutura de pergunta é **Have you ever + particípio passado?** ("Você já...?"). Para negar, usamos **I have never + particípio passado** ("Eu nunca..."). Também usamos essa estrutura com superlativos: *"This is the best project I have ever worked on"* ("Este é o melhor projeto em que já trabalhei").

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais frequente para falantes de português é usar o passado simples no lugar do presente perfeito: ❌ *"Did you ever work remotely?"* → ✅ *"Have you ever worked remotely?"* Quando não há um tempo específico no passado, o inglês exige o presente perfeito. Outro ponto: *ever* fica sempre **depois** do sujeito — *"Have **you** ever..."*, nunca *"Have ever you..."*. E nas respostas curtas, sempre repita o auxiliar: *"Yes, I **have**."*, nunca *"Yes, I seen."*
