### 1. 📘 Unit 010 — was / were

---

### 2. 🧠 Mini-context

> **CEO:** Last quarter's AI pilot — **were** you happy with the results?
> **You:** I **was**, mostly. Adoption **was** strong, but the two vendors
> **weren't** aligned on the roadmap.
> **CEO:** And the budget? **Was** it enough?
> **You:** It **wasn't**, honestly. We **were** short by about fifteen percent,
> so the data team **was** stretched all quarter.

Notice the shape before any rule: every one of these is just the **past of
am / is / are**. *I am happy* → *I **was** happy*. *They are aligned* → *they
**weren't** aligned*. It describes states and situations **in the past** —
finished, done, over.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

*was / were* is simply the **past tense of the verb *to be*** (Units 001–002).

| Subject | present | **past** |
|---|---|---|
| I | am | **was** |
| He / She / It / The board | is | **was** |
| We / You / They / The vendors | are | **were** |

**Key facts:**
- **Negative:** add *not* → **was not / were not**, contracted to **wasn't /
  weren't**. *"The budget **wasn't** enough."* / *"The vendors **weren't**
  aligned."*
- **Question:** invert, exactly like the present — the verb jumps to the front.
  *"**Was** it enough?"* / *"**Were** you happy?"* No *do/did* here — *be*
  never borrows an auxiliary.
- **there was / there were:** the past of *there is / there are*. *"**There
  was** one blocker."* (singular) / *"**There were** three risks."* (plural).

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** Because you constantly need to describe **how
things were** — a state, a situation, a feeling, a fact — at a **finished point
in the past**. *Was / were* is the past "state" verb: last quarter, in the last
meeting, back then. It carries no action; it just fixes a condition in past
time.

**Decision rule #1 — *was* vs *were* (choose by the subject).** This is the
whole grammar of the unit, and it mirrors the present exactly:

| Use **was** | Use **were** |
|---|---|
| I, he, she, it, and any **singular** subject | you, we, they, and any **plural** subject |
| *The CEO **was** cautious.* | *The directors **were** cautious.* |
| *There **was** one open risk.* | *There **were** three open risks.* |

Minimal pair — the subject alone flips the verb:
- *The vendor **was** late with the report.* → one vendor.
- *The vendors **were** late with the report.* → more than one.

**Decision rule #2 — *was/were* (a state) vs the past of action verbs (next
unit).** *Be* describes **how something was**; action verbs describe **what
someone did**. Keep them apart — and never combine them:
- *The pilot **was** successful.* → a state (how it was). ✅
- *We **launched** the pilot in March.* → an action (what we did). ✅
- ❌ *We **was launched** the pilot* — mixing a state verb with an action verb.
  It's either *was successful* (state) **or** *launched* (action), never both.

**PT→EN mapping — the big one.** Portuguese splits the past of *ser/estar* into
**two tenses**; English fuses them into **one**. *Was/were* covers **all** of:
- *era* (imperfeito): *"A meta **era** ambiciosa"* → *"The target **was**
  ambitious."*
- *estava* (imperfeito): *"O time **estava** exausto"* → *"The team **was**
  exhausted."*
- *foi / esteve* (pretérito): *"A reunião **foi** tensa"* → *"The meeting
  **was** tense."*

Good news: you **don't** choose between them in English — one form, *was/were*,
does all three. The interference runs the **other way**: don't over-think it or
hunt for a second past form that doesn't exist.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *There **was** three risks on the roadmap.* → ✅ *There **were** three
  risks on the roadmap.* — *plural subject → **were**; "there was" only for a
  singular thing.*
- ❌ *I **had** 30 when I joined the board.* → ✅ *I **was** 30 when I joined
  the board.* — *age uses **be**, in the past too: "I was 30", never "I had 30
  years" (direct from "eu tinha 30 anos").*
- ❌ *The results **was** strong last quarter.* → ✅ *The results **were**
  strong last quarter.* — *"results" is plural → **were**; a common slip when
  the subject is a plural noun, not a pronoun.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. Adoption **was** strong last quarter, but the two vendors **weren't**
   aligned. — *both are past states of "be"; singular "adoption" → was, plural
   "vendors" → weren't.*
2. **Were** you happy with the AI pilot's results? — *question = invert "be";
   "you" always takes **were**, no did needed.*
3. **There were** three open risks on the budget before we cut scope. — *past
   existence, plural → "there were" (never "there was three").*
4. The 2026 target **was** too ambitious for a single fiscal year. — *"era
   ambiciosa" (imperfeito) → English still just "was"; one form covers it.*
5. The board **wasn't** convinced by the first version of the roadmap. — *past
   negative state: singular "board" → wasn't; "be" never uses didn't.*
6. We **were** short on the AI budget by about fifteen percent. — *plural "we"
   → were; a finished past situation, now resolved.*
7. I **was** 30 when I first presented a strategy to the C-level. — *age in the
   past uses "be" → "was 30", not "had 30 years".*
8. The kids **were** at their grandparents' all weekend. — *everyday life;
   plural subject in a finished past state → were.*

### 9. ✅ Gabarito

**Warm-up (Unit 009)**
1. *"**Have** you got the numbers?"* inverts *have* (the *have got* system);
   *"**Do** you have the numbers?"* uses **do** (plain *have* behaves like an
   action verb). — *Two separate systems; never mix into "do you have got".*
2. **There are** three risks. — *this is existence ("tem" = there is/are), not
   possession; "have" would need an owner as its subject.*
3. ❌ *"has 55 years"* → ✅ **"Our CEO is 55 and has thirty years in the
   industry."** — *age uses "be" (is 55); "have" returns only for the real
   possession, thirty years of experience.*

**Spaced review**
- **(Unit 003)** **are hiring** — *"this quarter" marks a temporary, in-progress
  action → present continuous; the simple "hire" would describe it as a
  permanent policy, which the sentence explicitly contrasts with "normally".*

**Recognition drill**
1. **were** — *"results" is plural → were.*
2. ❌ *"There was four vendors"* → ✅ **"There were four vendors on the
   shortlist last year."** — *plural subject → there were.*
3. ❌ *"had 48"* → ✅ **"Our CFO was 48 when she joined."** — *age uses "be" in
   the past too: was 48, never "had 48 years".*
4. **"The transformation was slower than planned, so we revised the timeline in
   April."** — *"was" fills the state slot (how the transformation was); the
   action verb "revised" fills the action slot (what we did). One word can't do
   both — "be" describes a condition, it doesn't perform the action.*

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** *was/were* é simplesmente o **passado do verbo *to
be*** (am/is/are). Use para descrever **como algo era/estava** num ponto
**terminado do passado** — um estado, uma situação, um sentimento, um fato: *"The
pilot **was** successful"*, *"The vendors **weren't** aligned"*. A única escolha
é pelo **sujeito**: **was** para I/he/she/it e qualquer singular; **were** para
you/we/they e qualquer plural — inclusive em *there was* (singular) vs *there
were* (plural). Nas perguntas, o verbo **inverte** (*"Were you happy?"*, *"Was it
enough?"*) e nunca usa *did* — o *be* não pega auxiliar.

**As armadilhas.** (1) O grande ponto de interferência: o português divide o
passado de *ser/estar* em **dois tempos** (*era/estava* no imperfeito, *foi/esteve*
no pretérito), mas o inglês funde tudo num só — **was/were** cobre *era*,
*estava* e *foi*. Não procure uma segunda forma; ela não existe. (2) **Idade** no
passado usa **be**, não *have*: *"Eu tinha 30 anos"* → *"I **was** 30"*, jamais
*"I had 30 years"*. (3) Concordância com sujeito plural: *"The results **were**
strong"*, *"There **were** three risks"* — cuidado ao ver um substantivo plural e
escapar um *was*. Se o contraste estado-vs-ação embolar, ele fica mais claro na
Unit 011 (o passado dos verbos de ação); por ora, guarde: *was/were* diz **como
estava**, não **o que se fez**.
