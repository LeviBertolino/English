# Unit 006 — I don't... (present simple negative)

## 0. 🔁 Warm-up: Quick Recall (≈60 seconds)

Yesterday's lesson: **Unit 005 — I do/work/like etc. (present simple) (1)** — habits, routines, and general facts about systems and teams.

Three rapid recall questions:

1. From yesterday's examples: *"The pipeline \_\_\_ the full test suite on every pull request."* — Which verb form fits? *run* or *runs*?
2. *"Our team uses feature flags for everything."* — Why is this present simple, not present continuous?
3. ❌ *"She doesn't works on Fridays."* — What's the fix? Why is the original wrong?

👉 **Answers:**
1. **runs** — pipeline is he/she/it subject, so add -s: *"The pipeline runs the full test suite."*
2. It's a **general fact / permanent practice** about the team — not something happening right now or temporarily. General truths → present simple.
3. ✅ **"She doesn't work on Fridays."** — With *doesn't*, the main verb returns to base form. The -s is already "inside" doesn't (does + n't). Two -s markers would be double-marking, and English doesn't do that.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **A one-on-one between Levi and a junior engineer:**
>
> **Junior:** I'm not sure about the review process. Does everyone review every PR?
>
> **Levi:** Not exactly. I **don't** review every PR myself — I focus on architectural decisions and system-level changes. Mia **doesn't** do backend reviews; she owns the frontend codebase. The automated checks **don't** replace human review, though — they just catch the obvious stuff. We **don't** merge without at least one approval.
>
> **Junior:** Got it. So the pipeline handles the small things, and the team focuses on what matters.
>
> **Levi:** Exactly. The system **doesn't** flag style issues anymore — we moved that to a linter. And I **don't** expect juniors to catch architecture problems yet — that's what pairing is for.

Notice the negative pattern: **don't** (I/you/we/they) and **doesn't** (he/she/it) + the **base form** of the verb (no -s). Every negative sentence above follows that exact shape.

### The rule: present simple negative

To make any present simple sentence negative, insert **don't** or **doesn't** before the base verb:

| Subject | Negative form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I | **I don't** + verb | *I don't review every PR.* |
| you | **you don't** + verb | *You don't need to handle this alone.* |
| he / she / it | **he/she/it doesn't** + verb | *She doesn't do backend reviews.* |
| we | **we don't** + verb | *We don't merge without approval.* |
| they | **they don't** + verb | *They don't use Jira — they prefer Linear.* |

### The key rule about the main verb

When you use *don't* or *doesn't*, the **main verb loses its -s** and returns to the base form:

| ✅ Positive | ✅ Negative |
|---|---|
| She work**s** remotely. | She **doesn't work** remotely. |
| The system deploy**s** at midnight. | The system **doesn't deploy** at midnight. |
| He review**s** architecture docs. | He **doesn't review** architecture docs. |
| It run**s** every hour. | It **doesn't run** every hour. |

The -s belonged to the main verb to signal "third person." Once you add *doesn't* (which already carries that signal), the main verb drops the -s. One signal is enough.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"I don't approve PRs without reading the full diff — speed isn't worth a production bug."** — tech, habit (I)
2. **"Our deployment pipeline doesn't run on weekends — we freeze releases on Fridays."** — tech, general fact (it)
3. **"She doesn't own the backend stack; her domain is data infrastructure and pipelines."** — tech, role boundary (she)
4. **"We don't make architectural changes without an RFC — the team needs to align first."** — tech, process rule (we)
5. **"The observability platform doesn't alert on every error — only on anomalies above the defined threshold."** — tech, system behavior (it)
6. **"They don't use a monorepo; each service lives in its own repository."** — tech, team decision (they)
7. **"I don't drink coffee after 3 pm — otherwise I can't sleep."** — everyday, personal habit (I)
8. **"He doesn't take meetings before 10 am — he blocks that time for deep work."** — everyday, personal rule (he)

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"She doesn't works on the platform team."** → ✅ **"She doesn't work on the platform team."**
*Why: doesn't already signals third person. The main verb must return to base form — no -s.*

❌ **"I don't working on Fridays."** → ✅ **"I don't work on Fridays."**
*Why: after don't/doesn't, always use the base form (infinitive), never -ing. Don't + base verb, full stop.*

❌ **"The system don't deploy automatically."** → ✅ **"The system doesn't deploy automatically."**
*Why: "the system" = it → third person singular → needs doesn't, not don't. This is the same -s rule from the positive, applied to the auxiliary.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**"Doesn't" steals the -s.**

Think of it this way: the -s has to live somewhere in the sentence to mark third person. In the positive, it sits on the main verb (*she work**s***). When you bring in *doesn't*, the -s moves there (*doe**s**n't*) — and the main verb is left bare.

> *She work**s**.* → *She **doesn't** work.* (The -s jumped from *works* to *does*.)

If you ever hesitate on *doesn't work* vs. *doesn't works*, ask: *"Where is the -s right now?"* It's in *doesn't* — so the main verb has none.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| don't | /doʊnt/ | diphthong /oʊ/ — starts open, glides to /ʊ/ ⚠️ |
| doesn't | /ˈdʌzənt/ | 2 syllables: **DUZ**-nt — short /ʌ/ vowel ⚠️ |
| don't merge | /doʊnt mɜːrdʒ/ | link the /t/ lightly into *merge* |
| doesn't run | /ˈdʌzənt rʌn/ | /ʌ/ appears twice in a row ⚠️ |
| doesn't deploy | /ˈdʌzənt dɪˈplɔɪ/ | 5 syllables: DUZ-nt-di-PLOY |
| approve | /əˈpruːv/ | stress on 2nd syllable: uh-PROOV |
| merge | /mɜːrdʒ/ | /ɜːr/ — doesn't exist in Portuguese ⚠️ |
| threshold | /ˈθrɛʃhoʊld/ | the /θ/ ("th") — very different from /t/ or /d/ ⚠️ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"doesn't" vowel** ⚠️: The vowel in *doesn't* is /ʌ/ — like the "u" in "cup" or "fun." Brazilians often say "doz-ent" (using /o/ from Portuguese *"dose"*). Practice: **DUZ-nt** — mouth almost open, tongue low and back.
- **"don't" diphthong** ⚠️: The /oʊ/ in *don't* is a gliding vowel — it moves from /o/ toward /ʊ/. Portuguese has a pure /o/ with no glide. Push through to the end of the sound: *DOH-nt* with a slight /ʊ/ at the close.
- **"merge"** ⚠️: The /ɜːr/ vowel (as in *her, bird, word*) doesn't exist in Brazilian Portuguese. Brazilians typically substitute /ɛ/ + /r/. Try: lips neutral, tongue mid, add the /r/ in the American way (retroflex, tongue curled slightly back). Practice slowly: *mmm-ERRR-j*.
- **"th" in threshold"** ⚠️: /θ/ is made with the tongue tip lightly between the teeth, releasing air — not /t/ or /d/. *"Threshold"* is a common tech word — worth drilling. Say it slowly: *THRESH-hold*.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A **forma negativa do presente simples** usa **don't** (I / you / we / they) ou **doesn't** (he / she / it) seguido do verbo principal na **forma base** — sem o -s. Esse é o ponto mais importante da aula: quando você adiciona *doesn't*, o verbo principal perde o -s porque o auxiliar já carrega essa marcação de terceira pessoa. Exemplos: *"She work**s**"* → *"She **doesn't** work"* (não *"doesn't works"*); *"It run**s**"* → *"It **doesn't** run"*. O padrão vale para qualquer verbo no presente simples: basta identificar o sujeito, escolher *don't* ou *doesn't*, e usar o verbo na forma infinitiva.

Na pronúncia, os dois pontos mais importantes: **"doesn't"** tem a vogal **/ʌ/** (como em *cup*), não /o/ — diga **DUZ-nt**, não "doz-ent"; e **"don't"** tem o ditongo **/oʊ/**, que em português tendemos a "achatar" para um /o/ simples — abra o som e deixe ele deslizar levemente para o /ʊ/ no final. Essas negativas são muito frequentes em inglês técnico para definir escopo, delimitar responsabilidades e documentar comportamento de sistemas — *"This service doesn't handle retries," "I don't review every PR,"* *"We don't merge without approval"* — frases que você vai usar todo dia como tech leader.
