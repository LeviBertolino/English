## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Onboarding conversation — first day at a new company:**
>
> *Manager: "Do you have a MacBook Pro yet, or are you still on the loaner?"*
>
> *Levi: "I've got the new M3 Pro. It has 36 GB of RAM, which is great for running models locally."*
>
> *Manager: "Nice. Does your team have access to the staging cluster?"*
>
> *Levi: "Yes — we have the credentials, but we haven't got the VPN config sorted yet. Do you have a ticket number for that?"*
>
> *Manager: "I don't have it handy, but IT has a setup guide on Confluence."*

Notice the pattern: **have**, **'ve got**, **has**, **haven't got**, **don't have**, **has** — all expressing possession or availability. Two structures, one meaning.

### The rule

**"Have"** and **"have got"** both express possession (owning something, holding something, a feature or attribute). They are largely interchangeable, but note:

| | have | have got |
|---|---|---|
| More common in | American English (and formal writing) | British English (and informal speech) |
| Works for all meanings of "have" | ✅ yes (*"I have breakfast at 8"*) | ❌ only possession (*"I've got a MacBook"* ✅ / *"I've got breakfast at 8"* ❌) |

#### Positive forms

| Subject | have | have got (contracted) |
|---|---|---|
| I | I have | I've got |
| he / she / it | he has | he's / she's / it's got |
| you / we / they | you have | you've / we've / they've got |

#### Negative forms

| | have | have got |
|---|---|---|
| I | I **don't have** | I **haven't got** |
| he / she / it | he **doesn't have** | he **hasn't got** |
| you / we / they | you **don't have** | you **haven't got** |

#### Question forms

| | have | have got |
|---|---|---|
| I | **Do I have**…? | **Have I got**…? |
| he / she / it | **Does she have**…? | **Has she got**…? |
| you / we / they | **Do you have**…? | **Have you got**…? |

> **Key point:** "Have got" uses **have/has** as the auxiliary in questions and negatives — not *do/does*. Pick one system per sentence; don't mix them: ✅ *"Does he have…?"* or ✅ *"Has he got…?"* — ❌ *"Does he have got…?"*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"Our AI team has three H100 GPUs in the on-prem cluster — we've got enough compute for the fine-tuning runs."** — possession of a resource, tech context
2. **"Do you have access to the production logs, or do I need to request permissions for you?"** — checking access/availability, engineering context
3. **"She hasn't got a direct report yet — her team is still being formed."** — negative, Staff engineering / team structure
4. **"The new microservice has a circuit-breaker pattern built in; it's got retry logic and timeout handling out of the box."** — describing a system's attributes, software architecture
5. **"Does your company have an internal developer platform, or does each team manage its own infra?"** — question about org capabilities, tech leadership
6. **"We don't have a clear on-call rotation yet — that's on the roadmap for next quarter."** — negative, team-operations context
7. **"I've got a dentist appointment at 3pm, so I'll be offline for about an hour."** — everyday life
8. **"They have a dog and two cats — the apartment is full of chaos."** — everyday possession

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

**"Have got" = snapshot possession.** The *got* is like a past particle frozen in the present — *"I got it, and I still have it."* That's why it only works for possession states, not actions or routines: *"I've got a laptop"* ✅ (I possess it now), but *"I've got lunch every day at noon"* ❌ (that's a habit → use *"I have lunch"*).

To keep question/negative forms straight: **if you say "have got," use "have/has" as your helper — no *do/does* needed.** Think of it as a toggle: *have* group uses *do/does*, *have got* group uses *have/has*.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**Regra principal:** tanto **have** quanto **have got** expressam posse — algo que você tem, possui ou dispõe. As duas formas são largamente intercambiáveis, com duas ressalvas importantes. Primeiro, *have got* é mais comum no inglês britânico e no registro informal; *have* predomina no inglês americano e em textos formais. Segundo, *have got* só funciona para posse (*"I've got a laptop"*) — não serve para hábitos ou ações rotineiras (*"I have lunch at noon"* ✅ / *"I've got lunch at noon"* ❌). Nas formas positivas: *I have / I've got*, *she has / she's got*, *they have / they've got*. Nas negativas: *I don't have / I haven't got*, *she doesn't have / she hasn't got*. Nas perguntas: *Do you have…? / Have you got…?* — *Does she have…? / Has she got…?*

**O erro mais comum para brasileiros:** misturar os dois sistemas dentro da mesma frase. Em inglês, você escolhe um: se usa *have*, o auxiliar das perguntas e negativas é *do/does*. Se usa *have got*, o auxiliar é *have/has*. A frase *"Does he have got a computer?"* é errada porque mistura os dois. Escolha *"Does he have a computer?"* ou *"Has he got a computer?"* — nunca os dois juntos. Para contextos de trabalho em inglês (documentações, e-mails, mensagens), a forma com *have* simples tende a ser mais segura e neutra: *"Do you have access to the repo?"*, *"We don't have a staging environment yet."*
