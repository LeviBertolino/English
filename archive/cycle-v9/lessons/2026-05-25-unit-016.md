## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — Monday morning:**
>
> **Ana:** Hey Levi — quick heads-up. The latency on the payments service **is going to spike** again today. I'm looking at the queue depth right now and it **is going to hit** the threshold within the hour.
>
> **Levi:** Copy that. I **am going to pull** the on-call team into a channel. Are you **going to push** the config change you mentioned Friday?
>
> **Ana:** Yes, I **am going to apply** it after the stand-up. Oh, and the new Staff engineer starts today — she **is going to shadow** the incident response process.
>
> **Levi:** Perfect timing for that. The system **is going to give** her a real welcome.

Notice *going to* doing two different jobs in that conversation:
- *"The queue depth is going to hit the threshold"* — Ana can see something **right now** that tells her what's about to happen. Evidence → near-certain prediction.
- *"I am going to pull the team in"* — Levi has already **decided** to do this. Plan → intention.

### The rule

**"Going to"** covers two tightly related ideas:

---

**Use 1 — Intentions and plans (already decided)**

You've made up your mind. You have a plan. The decision was made **before** the moment of speaking.

> *I'm going to refactor the auth module next sprint.* ← already on my list
> *She's going to lead the architecture review on Thursday.* ← planned
> *We're going to migrate to Postgres before Q3.* ← team decision already made

---

**Use 2 — Predictions based on present evidence**

You can see or know something **right now** that makes the outcome obvious or near-certain.

> *Look at the error rate — this deployment is going to fail.* ← the data is right there
> *The team hasn't slept in 36 hours. They're going to burn out.* ← visible trend
> *Your test suite is going to pass — I can see all the checks are green.* ← evidence in front of you

The key distinction from *will*: with *going to* for predictions, there is **present evidence**. With *will*, it's more like an opinion or guess without visible proof.

---

**Forms**

| Subject | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | I'm going to deploy | I'm not going to deploy | Am I going to deploy? |
| you / we / they | You're going to review | You aren't going to review | Are you going to review? |
| he / she / it | She's going to lead | She isn't going to lead | Is she going to lead? |

The base verb after *going to* **never changes** — no -s, no -ing, no -ed.

> *She is going to leads* ❌ → *She is going to lead* ✅
> *He is going to deploying* ❌ → *He is going to deploy* ✅

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **I'm going to rewrite the onboarding docs before the new batch of engineers starts next month.** — intention, already decided.
2. **Look at this memory graph — the service is going to crash before midnight if we don't intervene.** — prediction from evidence visible right now.
3. **Our team is going to adopt a new incident management framework in Q3.** — team plan, already agreed.
4. **She's going to present the RFC to the architecture council on Friday.** — scheduled, already decided.
5. **The CI pipeline is going to time out — the test suite has been running for 45 minutes and shows no sign of finishing.** — prediction from present evidence.
6. **I'm not going to approve this PR until we have at least 80% test coverage on the new module.** — clear intention/decision already made.
7. **Are you going to migrate the legacy service before or after the Q2 release?** — asking about someone's plan.
8. **My flight is in two hours and I haven't packed yet — I'm going to miss it.** — prediction from an obvious current situation (everyday life).

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I'm going deploy the hotfix tonight"** → ✅ **"I'm going to deploy the hotfix tonight"**
*Why: "going to" is a fixed phrase — the "to" is not optional. This is the most common mistake Brazilians make because in Portuguese, "vou" (I'm going) directly precedes the verb with no equivalent of "to."*

❌ **"The build is going to failing"** → ✅ **"The build is going to fail"**
*Why: the verb after "going to" must be in its base (infinitive) form. No -ing, no -ed, no -s. "Going to" already marks the tense; the main verb just gives the action.*

❌ **"It's going to rain, I think it will be cold"** (mixing without awareness) → ✅ understand the difference: **"It's going to rain"** (I can see the clouds — evidence) vs. **"I think it will rain"** (opinion, no specific evidence in front of me)
*Why: "going to" for predictions requires present evidence. If you're making a casual guess or giving an opinion, "will" is more natural. Both are grammatically possible, but native speakers choose based on whether evidence is visible.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of *going to* as a **GPS route already calculated**.

- **Intention**: you've already entered the destination. The route is set. You just haven't arrived yet.
- **Prediction**: you can see the road ahead — a red light, a traffic jam — and the GPS tells you what's about to happen.

Either way, the outcome is **not a surprise**: it's either planned or visible. Compare with *will*, which is more like saying "I think there might be traffic" without actually looking at the map.

> *I'm going to merge this tonight.* = GPS set, destination locked.
> *The server load is going to peak at 2 AM.* = the GPS sees it coming.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| going to | /ˈɡəʊɪŋ tə/ | "to" is weak — /tə/, not /tuː/ ⚠️ |
| gonna | /ˈɡɒnə/ | Informal spoken contraction — avoid in writing |
| I'm going to | /aɪm ˈɡəʊɪŋ tə/ | Three syllables run together in natural speech |
| she's going to | /ʃɪz ˈɡəʊɪŋ tə/ | The contraction /ʃɪz/ is very fast ⚠️ |
| deploy | /dɪˈplɔɪ/ | Stress on second syllable: *de-PLOY* |
| migrate | /ˈmaɪɡreɪt/ | Stress on first syllable: *MY-grate* |
| prediction | /prɪˈdɪkʃən/ | *pre-DIK-shun* — stress on second syllable |
| intention | /ɪnˈtɛnʃən/ | *in-TEN-shun* — stress on second syllable |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"going to" reduction** ⚠️: In natural spoken English, *"going to"* almost always sounds like *"gonna"* /ˈɡɒnə/ in informal speech. But the written form — and careful speech — is *going to* with a weak /tə/ (not /tuː/). Practice the middle ground: *"I'm going to"* → /aɪm ˈɡəʊɪŋ tə/ — the "to" is barely there, almost like a quick *"tuh"*.
- **"gonna" in writing** ⚠️: Brazilians sometimes write "gonna" after learning it's natural spoken English. Use *gonna* only in very informal written chat (not in work messages, PRs, or documentation). Stick to *going to* in writing.
- **Not adding a vowel after -ing** ⚠️: *"going"* ends on /ŋ/ — a nasal stop. Don't say *"going-gee"* or *"going-i."* Close your mouth after the /ŋ/.
- **"deploy" stress** ⚠️: In Portuguese, *deploy* is often said *"DEH-ploy"* (stress on first syllable). In English it's *de-PLOY* — stress shifts to the second syllable.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A estrutura *"going to"* é o equivalente direto do *"vou"* em português — e essa é a boa notícia: você já usa essa ideia o tempo todo, só precisa adaptar a forma em inglês. Há dois usos principais. O primeiro é para **intenções e planos já decididos**: algo que você já decidiu fazer antes de falar (*"I'm going to refactor the module"* = já está no plano). O segundo é para **previsões baseadas em evidências presentes**: você está olhando para algo agora e consegue ver o que vai acontecer (*"The build is going to fail"* = estou olhando os logs e é óbvio). O que distingue o *going to* do *will* para previsões é justamente isso: com *going to*, há uma evidência visível; com *will*, é mais uma opinião ou intuição.

Em termos de forma, a armadilha mais comum para falantes de português é esquecer o "to" — porque em português dizemos *"vou deployar"* sem nenhum equivalente ao "to." Em inglês é obrigatório: *"I'm going **to** deploy."* Além disso, o verbo que segue o *going to* fica sempre na forma base (infinitivo sem "to"): nunca *"going to deploying"* ou *"going to deployed."* Na fala natural, *"going to"* soa quase como *"gonna"* — mas reserve o *gonna* para conversas informais e mantenha *going to* em textos escritos de trabalho.
