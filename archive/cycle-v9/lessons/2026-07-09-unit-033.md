## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Levi, syncing with a new hire before her first on-call rotation:**
>
> **Levi:** You **have to** acknowledge every PagerDuty alert within 5 minutes — that's the team's SLA, it's not up to us.
> **New hire:** Got it. Anything I should personally watch out for?
> **Levi:** Yeah — I **must** remember to hand you the runbook link before Friday, or you'll be stuck without it.
> **New hire:** Do I need to loop in the manager for every alert?
> **Levi:** No, you **don't have to** — only for Sev1s. But you **mustn't** silence an alert without writing a note in the channel; that one's a hard rule.

Notice:
- **have to** = an obligation that comes from **outside** you (a policy, a rule, someone else's authority): the SLA, the on-call policy.
- **must** = an obligation the speaker feels **personally**, often a decision or something they consider urgent right now: *I must remember...*
- **don't have to** = **no obligation** — it's optional, free to skip.
- **mustn't** = **prohibition** — it's not optional, it's forbidden.

### The rule

| Form | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **must** + base verb | obligation the speaker imposes on themselves/feels personally | I **must** finish this RFC before the offsite. |
| **have to** + base verb | obligation from an external rule/authority | Everyone **has to** complete security training this quarter. |
| **don't have to / don't need to** | **no obligation** — it's optional | You **don't have to** attend the retro if you're on PTO. |
| **mustn't** | **prohibition** — not allowed | You **mustn't** push directly to `main`. |

> ⚠️ The biggest trap for Portuguese speakers: **mustn't** ≠ **don't have to**. *"Não precisa"* is **don't have to/don't need to**; *"não pode"* is **mustn't**. Mixing them reverses the meaning completely.
> ⚠️ In everyday speech, **have to** is far more common than **must** for external rules — *"must"* can sound quite formal or urgent/personal.
> ⚠️ For the past, English only has one option: **had to** (there's no "musted"). *"I had to redeploy twice yesterday."*

## 3. ✏️ 8 example sentences

1. Every engineer **has to** pass the security review before merging to `main`.
2. I **must** send that postmortem today — I promised the VP.
3. You **don't have to** rewrite the whole module, just patch the bug.
4. The on-call engineer **mustn't** ignore a Sev1 page, even at 3am.
5. She **had to** reschedule the demo because the staging environment was down.
6. We **have to** renew our passports before the trip in October.
7. You **don't have to** bring anything to the barbecue, we've got it covered.
8. Kids **mustn't** cross the street without an adult.

## 5. 💡 Memory tip

**HAVE TO = the rule comes from outside you (📋 policy). MUST = the push comes from inside you (🔥 urgency).** And remember the trap as a pair: **MUSTN'T = 🚫 forbidden. DON'T HAVE TO = 🆓 optional.** They look similar but sit on opposite ends of the meaning.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

Hoje vimos **must** e **have to** para expressar **obrigação** — um sentido bem diferente do *must* de certeza visto ontem (Unit 032). **Have to** indica uma obrigação que vem de **fora**, imposta por uma regra, política ou outra pessoa; **must** indica uma obrigação que o próprio falante sente ou impõe a si mesmo, geralmente algo mais urgente ou pessoal.

O erro mais perigoso é confundir os negativos: **mustn't** significa **proibido** ("não pode"), enquanto **don't have to** (ou **don't need to**) significa **não é necessário** ("não precisa"). São opostos em força, não sinônimos — misturar os dois inverte completamente o sentido da frase. Para o passado, use sempre **had to**, já que **must** não tem forma de passado.
