Yesterday's lesson — **Unit 019: I have done and I did (1)** — introduced the core question: *is this time period still open, or already closed?* The Slack thread showed: *"Have you seen the scan results yet?"* (present perfect — no specific time given, result matters now) vs *"They finished about an hour ago"* (past simple — "an hour ago" stamps a specific finished moment). The memory tip framed it as 📡 live signal (present perfect) vs 📁 archived record (past simple).

Three rapid recall questions:

1. From yesterday's example — why did @dev1 say *"I **reviewed** it this morning"* using past simple, even though it was still morning?

👉 **Answers:**
1. ✅ Because @dev1 treated "this morning" as a **finished slot** (the review was done, time sealed). If it were still open (*"this morning isn't over yet, I'm mid-review"*), present perfect would work too — but the context signals completion.
2. ✅ **I have never deployed** — *never* belongs to the present perfect family (no specific timestamp, attitude about any moment up to now).
3. ✅ **(B)** — *"yesterday"* is a closed-time stamp; it forces past simple. *"I have seen him yesterday"* ❌ is a classic Brazilian English error.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **A 1:1 conversation between a Staff engineer (Levi) and a new junior hire (Priya):**
>
> **Priya:** Have you ever worked with GraphQL at scale?
>
> **Levi:** Yes, I have. I worked with it pretty heavily at my previous company — we migrated three internal APIs to GraphQL over about eight months.
>
> **Priya:** Interesting! Have you **been to** any tech conferences to talk about it?
>
> **Levi:** Yeah — I **went to** QCon last year and gave a lightning talk on federation. It was the first time I'd presented in front of 200 people, honestly.
>
> **Priya:** Did you find it hard to explain GraphQL tradeoffs to a general audience?
>
> **Levi:** I did. But I've gotten better at it since then.

Notice the flow:
- *"Have you ever worked with GraphQL?"* → present perfect — **any time in your career**, no date needed.
- *"Yes, I have. I worked with it at my previous company."* → The PP opens the topic; the PS gives the **specific detail**.
- *"Have you been to any conferences?"* vs *"I went to QCon last year"* — **been to** (experience; you came back) vs **went to** (specific trip in the past).
- *"Did you find it hard?"* → past simple question because we're asking about **a specific finished event** (that conference, that talk).
- *"I've gotten better since then"* → back to PP — no timestamp, result is live **now**.

---

### The rules for Unit 020

#### Rule 1 — PP opens the topic; PS fills in the details

In natural conversation, when someone introduces a topic with present perfect, both speakers then **switch to past simple** to give specific details:

> A: *"Have you met the new CTO?"*
> B: *"Yes! I met her at the all-hands last Tuesday. She seemed sharp."*

Not: *"Yes! I have met her at the all-hands"* ❌ — once you say *last Tuesday*, you must use past simple.

#### Rule 2 — "been to" vs "gone to"

| Form | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **have been to** | visited and came back | *"I've been to AWS re:Invent twice."* (you're back home) |
| **have gone to** | left and hasn't returned | *"She's gone to the offsite."* (she's still there) |

#### Rule 3 — "Have you ever...?" vs "Did you ever...?"

| Question | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Have you ever...?** | Any time in your life up to now (open window) |
| **Did you ever...?** | During a **finished** period — *"Did you ever use Perl when you worked at [company]?"* |

#### Rule 4 — "It's the first/second/only time..." → always present perfect

> *"This is the first time I've presented to the board."*
> *"That's only the second time the pipeline has failed this year."*

> ⚠️ Portuguese trap: *"É a primeira vez que **apresento** para o board"* uses present in PT-BR, but English always uses present perfect here: *"It's the first time I'**ve** presented..."* ✅

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"Have you ever led a database migration in production? — Yes, I've done two. The first one went smoothly; the second one caused a two-hour outage in 2023."** *(PP opens topic → PS fills in details)*
2. **"It's the third time the CI pipeline has timed out this sprint — we really need to investigate the test parallelization."** *(Rule 4 — "first/second/third time" + PP)*
3. **"Has the new model been deployed to staging yet? — Yes, it went up about twenty minutes ago."** *(PP question → PS answer with timestamp)*
4. **"I've been to three Staff-level calibration sessions, but the one in April 2024 was the most useful one by far."** *(PP "been to" + PS for specific event)*
5. **"The engineering director has gone to the strategy offsite — she'll be back Thursday."** *("gone to" — still there, hasn't returned)*
6. **"Did you ever use Jenkins when you were at your previous company, or had you moved to GitHub Actions by then?"** *(PS "did you ever" — asking about a finished job)*
7. **"This is the first time our team has shipped a feature completely AI-assisted, from spec to code review."** *(Rule 4 — "first time" + PP)*
8. **"I signed up for the AI safety reading group last semester, but I missed half the sessions because of the roadmap crunch."** *(PS with specific past period — last semester is closed)*

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Complete with the correct form of the verb. Some blanks need present perfect; some need past simple.

*"___ you ever ___ (present) at a company all-hands? — Yes! I ___ (present) at the Q3 all-hands last October. It ___ (be) the first time I ___ (speak) in front of the whole company. I ___ (be, negative) so nervous since my university thesis defense. After that I ___ (start) volunteering for more speaking slots."*

✅ **Answer:**
*"**Have** you ever **presented** at a company all-hands? — Yes! I **presented** at the Q3 all-hands last October. It **was** the first time I **had spoken** / **spoke** in front of the whole company. I **hadn't been** / **haven't been** so nervous since my university thesis defense. After that I **started** volunteering for more speaking slots."*

*(Note: "It was the first time I spoke" — in informal speech, simple past is also acceptable here; the textbook form is past perfect "had spoken" because the speaking happened within the finished scenario being described.)*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of present perfect and past simple as **two roles in a conversation:**

> **PP = the headline** — opens the topic, signals relevance now, no timestamp needed.
> **PS = the article** — fills in the when, where, what happened, the specifics.

Just like a news article: the **headline** grabs you (*"Team ships AI-powered code review"*), then the **body** gives details (*"The feature launched Monday after a six-month beta."*).

And for "been to" vs "gone to":
> **been to** = round trip ✈️ (you're back, you experienced it)
> **gone to** = one way 🚀 (they haven't come back yet)

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A **Unit 020** aprofunda dois pontos que o português frequentemente obscurece. Primeiro: em inglês, é natural e correto **abrir um assunto com o present perfect e imediatamente dar os detalhes no past simple**. *"Have you ever presented at a conference? — Yes, I presented at QCon last year."* Em português você provavelmente diria *"Já apresentei em conferência? — Sim, apresentei no QCon no ano passado"* — o mesmo tempo verbal nos dois momentos. Em inglês, o present perfect serve como uma "chamada de atenção" (*"isso é relevante agora"*), e o past simple entra assim que você especifica *quando*. Essa transição PP → PS é o padrão natural do inglês falado.

Segundo: a diferença entre **"have been to"** (estive lá, voltei) e **"have gone to"** (foi lá, ainda não voltou) não existe explicitamente em português — os dois seriam *"foi para"* ou *"esteve em"*. Fique atento: *"She's gone to the offsite"* significa que ela ainda está lá; *"She's been to the offsite"* significa que ela já voltou. Por fim, lembre-se da construção *"It's the first/second time + present perfect"* — em PT-BR usamos o presente (*"É a primeira vez que faço isso"*), mas em inglês o present perfect é obrigatório: *"It's the first time I've done this."* Esse é um dos erros mais comuns e mais fáceis de corrigir uma vez que você aprende a regra.
