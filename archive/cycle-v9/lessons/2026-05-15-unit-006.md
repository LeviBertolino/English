## 1. 📘 Unit 006 — I don't... (present simple negative)

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Tech Lead:** Does your team own the deployment pipeline?
> **Levi:** We own the CI side, but we **don't control** production releases directly. The release team handles that.
> **Tech Lead:** Does the release team use your tooling?
> **Levi:** They use some of it. They **don't follow** the same cadence, though — they release on a fixed schedule.
> **Tech Lead:** And your team **doesn't get** involved in the approval process?
> **Levi:** No, we **don't**. We build the gates; we **don't approve** the deploys.

Notice the pattern: *we don't control*, *they don't follow*, *doesn't get*, *we don't approve* — all negatives of present simple. There's one rule powering all of them.

### The rule

To make a **present simple negative**, use **don't** or **doesn't** + base verb:

| Subject | Negative form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | **don't** + base verb | *We don't control releases.* |
| he / she / it | **doesn't** + base verb | *She doesn't attend standups.* |

**Key points:**
- The base verb **never changes**: *she doesn't* **write** (not ~~writes~~)
- *Don't/doesn't* carries the negative; the main verb stays bare
- Contracted forms (*don't*, *doesn't*) are standard in speech and informal writing. Full forms (*do not*, *does not*) are used for emphasis: *"I do not recommend this approach."*

---

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. We **don't merge** PRs on Fridays — it's a team agreement to reduce weekend incidents.
2. The model **doesn't perform** well on out-of-distribution inputs; it needs additional fine-tuning.
3. I **don't attend** every sprint ceremony — my ICs run standups autonomously.
4. Our platform **doesn't support** custom runtimes yet; that's on the roadmap for Q3.
5. She **doesn't write** production code anymore, but she reviews every major design proposal.
6. They **don't use** a monorepo — each service lives in its own repository.
7. I **don't drink** coffee after 2 PM — it affects my sleep and my focus the next morning.
8. He **doesn't live** in São Paulo; he works fully remote from the south.

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"She don't understand the requirements."** → ✅ **"She doesn't understand the requirements."**
*Why: He/she/it uses "doesn't", not "don't". This is the most common slip.*

❌ **"He doesn't writes code anymore."** → ✅ **"He doesn't write code anymore."**
*Why: After "doesn't", the verb must be the base form — never add -s. Only one marker per verb.*

❌ **"I not work on Saturdays."** → ✅ **"I don't work on Saturdays."**
*Why: English needs a helper verb (do/does) to form present simple negatives. "Not" alone after the subject isn't enough.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **don't/doesn't as a traffic light** 🔴: it stops the action before the verb reaches the subject. The main verb sits quietly behind it in base form.

If the light is "doesn't" (he/she/it), the verb behind it cannot have -s — the light already did the signaling job. *"She doesn't writes"* has two markers for one verb — English doesn't allow that.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and phrases

| Word/Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| don't | /doʊnt/ | Full /t/ at the end — don't drop it ⚠️ |
| doesn't | /ˈdʌzənt/ | DUZ-nt — vowel is /ʌ/ like "bus" ⚠️ |
| don't merge | /doʊnt mɜːrdʒ/ | /t/ links smoothly into /m/ |
| doesn't support | /ˈdʌzənt səˈpɔːrt/ | Stress on "support", not "doesn't" |
| do not | /duː nɒt/ | Emphatic form — each word gets full stress |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"doesn't"**: the vowel in "does" is /ʌ/ (like the "a" in *cama* spoken quickly), **not** /oʊ/. Brazilians often say *"DOHZ-nt"* — aim for *"DUZ-nt"*. ⚠️
- **Final /t/ in "don't"**: in casual Portuguese, final consonants often weaken or disappear. In English, the /t/ in *don't* is essential — dropping it makes it sound like *"doan"*, which is non-standard. ⚠️
- **"doesn't" rhythm**: two syllables, stress on the first: *DUZ-nt*, not *duz-NT*.
- **Linking in natural speech**: *"We don't merge"* sounds like /wiː doʊnt mɜːrdʒ/ — the /t/ and /m/ blend together smoothly. Don't over-separate the words.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **negativo do presente simples** é formado com *don't* (para I/you/we/they) ou *doesn't* (para he/she/it), seguido do verbo na **forma base** — nunca com -s. A confusão mais comum é usar *"don't"* com he/she/it (*"she don't"*) ou adicionar -s depois do *"doesn't"* (*"she doesn't writes"*). Os dois erros vêm da mesma causa: o português não faz essa distinção de forma explícita, então o cérebro busca consistência onde o inglês não tem.

Outra armadilha típica de brasileiro é tentar negar sem auxiliar: *"I not work"* em vez de *"I don't work"*. Em inglês, o presente simples **precisa** do auxiliar *do/does* para negar — não dá para colocar *not* direto depois do sujeito. O auxiliar também aparece nas respostas curtas: *"Does she review? — No, she doesn't."*
