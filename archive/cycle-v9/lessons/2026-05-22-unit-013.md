## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — incident post-mortem:**
>
> **Levi:** What exactly happened during the outage window?
> **Sara:** So — the deployment pipeline was running when the config service dropped its connection. While we were rolling back the feature flag, someone pushed a hotfix to the wrong branch by mistake.
> **Levi:** Did anyone catch it immediately?
> **Sara:** Not right away. I was reading the dashboard alerts when Mateus called me on the phone and told me about the push. After that, we acted fast — we reverted the commit, ran a manual health check, and the monitors went green within 4 minutes.
> **Levi:** Good recovery. While all this was happening, was the traffic being rerouted?
> **Sara:** Yes — the load balancer rerouted automatically. It happened while we were still triaging.

Notice two things happening side by side in this account:
- **Long, ongoing actions** described with *was/were + -ing*: *the pipeline was running*, *we were rolling back*, *I was reading*, *we were triaging*.
- **Shorter, completed events** in plain past simple: *the config service dropped*, *someone pushed*, *Mateus called*, *we reverted*, *monitors went green*.

And when two actions were happening *at the same time at equal length*: *while we were triaging / while all this was happening* — both ongoing, both past continuous.

### The rule

**Two situations:**

**1. Past continuous + past simple: one action interrupted another**
Use **past continuous** for the action already in progress (longer, background), and **past simple** for the event that interrupted it (shorter, sudden).

> *I was reviewing the RFC **when** the Slack notification came in.*
> *(was reviewing = in progress → came in = interruption)*

> *We were running the migration **when** the connection timed out.*

> *She was presenting the roadmap **when** someone asked a question that changed everything.*

Signal words: **when**, **while** (when before the short event; while before the ongoing one).

**2. Both actions ongoing at the same time → both past continuous**
Use *while* to connect two simultaneous ongoing actions.

> ***While** I was writing the RFC, my manager was negotiating the timeline with the client.*
> *(both in progress at the same moment — neither interrupted the other)*

> ***While** the team was migrating the schema, the SRE was monitoring the dashboards.*

**3. Both actions completed in sequence → both past simple**
When one thing finished and then the next thing happened:

> *The tests **failed**, so the pipeline **stopped**.* (not "was stopping")
> *I **read** the error, **opened** the logs, and **found** the root cause.*

The key question is always: **was the action in progress, or did it happen and complete?**

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"The deploy was failing when I was noticing the issue"** → ✅ **"The deploy was failing when I noticed the issue"**
*Why: The noticing was a sudden, short event — an interruption. It should be past simple, not past continuous.*

❌ **"While I read the logs, the system was restarting"** → ✅ **"While I was reading the logs, the system was restarting"**
*Why: "While" signals two simultaneous ongoing actions — both should be past continuous. "I read" makes it sound like reading finished before the restart.*

❌ **"We were finishing the migration, then we were deploying the hotfix"** → ✅ **"We finished the migration, then we deployed the hotfix"**
*Why: Sequential, completed actions don't need past continuous. "We were finishing... we were deploying" implies overlap, but they happened one after the other.*

Choose the correct form for each blank:

> *"I ___ (write/was writing) the incident report when my manager ___ (called/was calling) me. We ___ (talked/were talking) for 20 minutes while the on-call engineer ___ (investigated/was investigating) the root cause. After the call, I ___ (opened/was opening) the ticket and ___ (tagged/was tagging) the right team."*

✅ **Answer:** *was writing / called / talked / was investigating / opened / tagged*
- *was writing* → in progress, interrupted by the call
- *called* → short, interrupting event
- *talked / was investigating* → two simultaneous ongoing actions → but here "talked" is the conversation as a whole (a bounded event) while "was investigating" is ongoing background — both work; "were talking" also correct
- *opened / tagged* → sequential completed actions → past simple

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word / Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| while | /waɪl/ | One syllable — *"wile"*, not *"while-i"* ⚠️ |
| when | /wɛn/ | Clear /ɛ/ — not "wen" (same vowel as in "pen") |
| interrupted | /ˌɪntəˈrʌptɪd/ | Four syllables: *in-tuh-RUP-tid* |
| was writing | /wəz ˈraɪtɪŋ/ | *"wuz RY-ting"* — "was" is unstressed /wəz/ ⚠️ |
| while I was | /waɪl aɪ wəz/ | *"wile-eye-wuz"* — flows quickly in natural speech |
| called | /kɔːld/ | One syllable — not *"call-ed"* ⚠️ |
| tagged | /tæɡd/ | One syllable — ends in /ɡd/, not *"tag-ged"* ⚠️ |
| investigated | /ɪnˈvɛstɪɡeɪtɪd/ | Five syllables: *in-VES-ti-gay-tid* |
| sequential | /sɪˈkwɛnʃəl/ | *si-KWEN-shul* |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"while" /waɪl/**: Brazilians often add a vowel at the end — *"while-i"* or *"wai-li"*. The word ends on the /l/ sound, which in Brazilian Portuguese is often "dark" (velarised). Practice stopping cleanly after the /l/. ⚠️
- **Regular past simple endings** (-ed): These collapse into a single syllable in most cases. *"called"* = /kɔːld/, not /kɔː-led/. *"tagged"* = /tæɡd/, not /tæ-ged/. The -ed ending adds **no extra syllable** after voiced consonants (b, d, g, l, m, n, r, v, w, z) — it just adds /d/. ⚠️
- **Weak "was" in context**: In the phrase *"I was writing"*, the "was" is almost swallowed — *"I-wuz-RY-ting"*. Practice saying it at natural speed so "was" doesn't get stressed.
- **"while I was"** in fast speech sounds like: *"wile-eye-wuz"* — three quick syllables, almost like one word. ⚠️

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A distinção central desta aula é: **past continuous** (was/were + -ing) descreve uma ação que *estava em andamento* num momento passado; **past simple** descreve uma ação *concluída*. O padrão mais importante é o da *interrupção*: uma ação mais longa (past continuous) estava acontecendo quando um evento mais curto e pontual ocorreu (past simple) — *"I was reviewing the PR when the alert fired."* Com *while*, as duas ações são simultâneas e em andamento — ambas ficam no past continuous: *"While I was writing the doc, she was coordinating the deploy."* Para ações sequenciais (uma terminou, depois veio a próxima), ambas ficam no past simple sem -ing: *"I opened the log, found the error, and fixed it."*

Na pronúncia, dois pontos críticos: (1) *"while"* é uma sílaba só — /waɪl/ — sem vogal final, não *"wai-li"*; (2) verbos regulares no passado (-ed) raramente adicionam sílaba — *"called"* = /kɔːld/, *"tagged"* = /tæɡd*. Fique atento a não pronunciar o -ed como sílaba extra nesses casos.
