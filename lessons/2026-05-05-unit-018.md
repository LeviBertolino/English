## 1. 📘 Unit 018 — How long have you...? (for and since)

**Grammar focus:** Using the Present Perfect with *for* and *since* to say how long a situation has been going on — from a point in the past up to right now.

---

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **Hiring manager:** How long have you been in a Staff Engineer role?
>
> **Levi:** I've been a Staff engineer since 2022 — so for about three years now.
>
> **Manager:** And how long has your team been using Kubernetes?
>
> **Levi:** For almost two years. We migrated from plain Docker Compose after the incident in Q1 last year.
>
> **Manager:** Interesting. We've had microservices in production since the company's Series A, and we still have legacy monolith debt. It's been sitting there for a long time.

Notice:
- *since 2022* → a **starting point** in time
- *for about three years* → a **duration** (a length of time)
- *How long have you been...?* → questions about duration

---

### The Rule

| Structure | Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **for** + duration | A period of time | *for two years, for six months, for a long time* |
| **since** + starting point | A point when it began | *since 2020, since Monday, since I joined* |
| **How long have you + past participle?** | Ask about duration up to now | *How long have you worked here?* |
| **Subject + have/has + been + [adj/noun] + for/since** | Ongoing state | *She has been the tech lead since January.* |

**Key points:**
- Both *for* and *since* are used with the **Present Perfect** (not past simple, not present simple).
- ✅ *I have worked here **for** three years.* — NOT ❌ *I work here for three years.*
- *For* measures a span: *three years, six months, a long time, ages.*
- *Since* pins a start: *2019, last March, the last deployment, I graduated.*
- Quick test: Can you replace it with *"during a period of X length"?* → use **for**. Can you replace it with *"starting at that moment"?* → use **since**.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Using *for* with a starting point (a fixed date or event):**
> ❌ I've been on this team for last March.
> ✅ I've been on this team **since** last March.
>
> *Why:* *For* needs a duration — a measurable span like "three months". *Last March* is a starting point in time, not a duration → use *since*.

**Mistake 2 — Using present simple with *since* (direct Portuguese transfer):**
> ❌ I work here since 2021.
> ✅ I **have worked** here since 2021.
>
> *Why:* In Portuguese, *"Trabalho aqui desde 2021"* works perfectly — present tense + *desde*. In English, when the action started in the past and **still continues now**, you must use the Present Perfect: *have/has + past participle*. This is one of the most common errors for Brazilian speakers.

**Mistake 3 — Confusing *for* with *during*:**
> ❌ I've been learning Rust during three months.
> ✅ I've been learning Rust **for** three months.
>
> *Why:* *During* = at some point within a period (*"I learned Rust during the sprint"* — it happened at some time in that sprint). *For* = the whole duration (*"for three months"* = spanning the full three months). They are not interchangeable.

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The ruler vs. the pin:**

- **for** = a 📏 **ruler** — it *measures* a length of time. *"For three years"* = the ruler spans three years.
- **since** = a 📍 **pin on a timeline** — it *marks* where the clock started. *"Since 2021"* = there's a pin at 2021.

Ask yourself before choosing:
> *Am I measuring a length?* → **for** (three years, six months, ages)
> *Am I pointing to a start?* → **since** (2021, last Monday, the incident, I joined)

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| for (stressed) | /fɔːr/ | Long /ɔː/ + clear final /r/ |
| for (unstressed) | /fər/ | Reduced — sounds like *"fer"* in natural speech |
| since | /sɪns/ | Short /ɪ/ — **not** /siːns/ |
| been | /bɪn/ | Short /ɪ/ — **not** /biːn/ in connected speech |
| how long | /haʊ lɒŋ/ | Two stressed syllables — don't merge them |
| I've | /aɪv/ | Voiced /v/ at the end — don't unvoice it |
| long | /lɒŋ/ (UK) / /lɔːŋ/ (US) | Both accepted |
| three | /θriː/ | /θ/ — tongue between teeth |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — Short /ɪ/ in *since*** ⚠️
*Since* has a short, clipped vowel: /sɪns/. Brazilians often lengthen it to "seence" /siːns/. Aim for the same short /ɪ/ as in *bit*, *sit*, *it*. Tip: say "sin" (pecado) and add the final /s/.

**Sound 2 — *been* in connected speech** ⚠️
*"I've been working"* — *been* reduces to /bɪn/, not /biːn/. Brazilians often say the full "been" /biːn/ because it looks like a long vowel word. In fast speech it's very short: *"I've-bin-working."*

**Sound 3 — *I've* = /aɪv/* ⚠️
The final /v/ must be **voiced** (like the *v* in *vivo*). Brazilians sometimes devoice it to /f/, producing *"I'f worked"* — incorrect. Keep it buzzing: /aɪv/.

**Sound 4 — Reduced *for* = /fər/* ⚠️
In sentences like *"for three years"*, *for* almost always reduces to /fər/ (like the English word *"fur"*). Saying the full /fɔːr/ sounds over-formal in natural speech. Practice blending: *"fer three years"*.

**Sound 5 — /θ/ in *three*** ⚠️
Brazilians frequently say /t/ (*"tree"*) or /f/ (*"free"*) instead of /θ/. The tip is the same as always: press the tip of your tongue lightly against the back of your upper front teeth and blow air through.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
Usamos o Presente Perfeito (*have/has + particípio passado*) com **for** e **since** para dizer há quanto tempo uma situação vem acontecendo — desde um ponto no passado até agora. *For* indica a **duração**: *for three years* (por três anos), *for a long time* (há muito tempo). *Since* indica o **ponto de partida**: *since 2021* (desde 2021), *since I joined* (desde quando entrei). A pergunta padrão é *"How long have you been...?"* — equivalente ao nosso "Há quanto tempo você...?".

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais comum para falantes de português é usar o presente simples com *since*: ❌ *"I work here since 2021"* → ✅ *"I have worked here since 2021"*. Em português, *"Trabalho aqui desde 2021"* funciona no presente simples; em inglês, a mesma ideia exige o Presente Perfeito porque a ação começou no passado e **continua agora**. Outro ponto: não confunda *for* com *during* — *for* mede uma duração completa (*for three months* = durante um período de três meses inteiros), enquanto *during* indica que algo aconteceu em algum momento dentro de um período (*during the sprint* = em algum momento do sprint).
