Notice the pattern before any rule: *have got* and plain *have* say the **same

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

To talk about **having** something — possession, relationships, features,
illnesses — English gives you two forms with the **same meaning**: **have** and
**have got**.

| | **have** (plain) | **have got** |
|---|---|---|
| Statement | I / we / they **have**; he/she/it **has** | I've **got**, he's **got** |
| Negative | I **don't have**; she **doesn't have** | I **haven't got**; she **hasn't got** |
| Question | **Do** you **have**…?; **Does** she **have**…? | **Have** you **got**…?; **Has** she **got**…? |

**Key facts:**
- **Same meaning.** *I have a plan* = *I've got a plan*. *Have got* is a bit more
  spoken/informal; plain *have* works everywhere and is the default in writing and
  in American English.
- **Two different question/negative systems.** *Have* uses **do/does** (it behaves
  like a normal action verb — exactly Units 006–007). *Have got* inverts like *to
  be*: the *have/has* jumps to the front, and *got* stays put.
- **Contractions:** *I've got, you've got, we've got, they've got; he's got,
  she's got, it's got.* Here *'s* = **has**, not *is*.

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não (THE core section)

**Why does this structure exist?** Because English needs a plain way to say
**"this exists in my possession"** — a timeline, a team, a mandate, a problem —
without any sense of action. *Have* here is a **state**, not something you *do*.
That is why *have got* feels natural for it: it is a fixed, informal way of
stating what you currently hold.

**Decision rule — plain *have* vs *have got*.** Meaning is identical, so choose by
**register and region**, not by sense:

| | Plain **have** | **Have got** |
|---|---|---|
| Feels | neutral, works everywhere | spoken, informal, more British |
| Questions/negatives | **do / does** (*Do you have…?*) | invert *have* (*Have you got…?*) |
| Board memo, email, report | ✅ preferred | ⚠️ too casual for formal writing |
| Quick spoken exchange | ✅ fine | ✅ very natural |

Minimal pair — same meaning, different register:
- ***Do** we **have** board approval?* → clean, neutral, fine in an email.
- ***Have** we **got** board approval?* → same question, more conversational.

**The one real trap: *have got* ≠ "got" alone, and ≠ the past.** *Have got* is a
**present** possession form. *I got a new laptop* (without *have*) means *I
received/acquired one* — a past action. Keep them apart:
- *I've **got** a strong team.* → I **have** one now (present state).
- *I **got** a new analyst last month.* → I **acquired** one (past event).

**PT→EN mapping.** Portuguese uses **ter** for both possession and existence, and
this is where interference bites:
- *Ter* = possession → **have / have got**: *"Eu tenho um plano" → "I have / I've
  got a plan."* ✅ The direct translation works.
- BUT *ter* for **existence** ("there is/are") does **not** map to *have*:
  *"Tem três riscos no projeto"* is **"There are three risks,"** ❌ not *"Have
  three risks."* English splits what Portuguese fuses — *ter de posse* → *have*,
  *ter de existência* → *there is/are*.
- And **age**: Portuguese *"Eu tenho 40 anos"* uses *ter*, but English uses ***be***
  — *"I **am** 40,"* ❌ never *"I have 40 years."*

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *Have three risks in the roadmap.* → ✅ *There **are** three risks in the
  roadmap.* — *"tem" as existence is "there is/are", not "have". "Have" always
  needs an owner (subject).*
- ❌ *Do you have got the numbers?* → ✅ *Do you **have** the numbers?* or ***Have**
  you **got** the numbers?* — *pick ONE system; never mix do-support with got.*
- ❌ *I have 42 years.* → ✅ *I **am** 42.* — *English states age with "be", not
  "have"; a classic direct-from-Portuguese slip.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. We **have** a clear owner for every AI workstream. — *possession as a neutral
   state → plain "have"; fits a written status update to the board.*
2. **Have** we **got** board approval for the pilot yet? — *same question, spoken
   register → "have got" inverts like to be; "got" stays after the subject.*
3. The CFO **has** the final numbers, but the board **hasn't got** the risk memo. —
   *"has" (plain) and "hasn't got" (got-form) coexist; both just state what each
   party holds right now.*
4. **Does** our lead vendor **have** the security certification? — *plain "have" →
   uses do-support (does + have), exactly like a normal action verb.*
5. We've **got** a two-year window before the market consolidates. — *"'ve got" =
   we currently possess this window (present state), not "we received" it.*
6. **There are** three open risks on the AI budget, not *"we have three risks
   there."* — *existence → "there are"; the Portuguese "tem" trap, where "have"
   would need an owner.*
7. I **got** a new strategy analyst last quarter, and now I've **got** real
   bandwidth. — *first "got" = past acquisition (no "have"); second "'ve got" =
   present possession — same word, two meanings.*
8. **Have** you **got** a minute before the C-level sync? — *everyday, spoken →
   "have got" is natural; "Do you have a minute?" would be the neutral twin.*

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** Para dizer que você **tem/possui** algo — um plano, um
time, um mandato, um problema — o inglês oferece duas formas com o **mesmo
sentido**: **have** e **have got**. *"I have a plan" = "I've got a plan."* A
diferença é só de **registro**: *have got* é mais falado e informal (e mais
britânico); o *have* puro serve para tudo e é o padrão na escrita e no inglês
americano — prefira-o em e-mails e memos para o board. Atenção às perguntas e
negativas, porque são **dois sistemas diferentes**: o *have* puro usa **do/does**
(*"Do you have the numbers?"*, *"She doesn't have it"*), enquanto o *have got*
**inverte** como o *to be* (*"Have you got the numbers?"*, *"She hasn't got it"*).
Nunca misture os dois: *"Do you have got…?"* está errado.

**As armadilhas (interferência do "ter").** O português usa *ter* para tudo, e é aí
que o inglês se separa. (1) *Ter de posse* → **have/have got** e a tradução direta
funciona; mas *ter de existência* → **there is/are**, não *have*: *"Tem três
riscos"* é *"**There are** three risks"*, jamais *"Have three risks"* (o *have*
sempre precisa de um dono/sujeito). (2) **Idade** usa **be**, não *have*: *"Tenho
40 anos"* → *"I **am** 40,"* nunca *"I have 40 years."* (3) Cuidado com *got*
sozinho: *"I've got a team"* é posse no presente, mas *"I got a team"* (sem *have*)
vira ação no passado — *adquiri/recebi*. Se um contraste do presente ainda
embolar, releia `lessons/2026-07-19-unit-007.md` (o do/does) para lembrar por que
o *have* puro pega o auxiliar.
