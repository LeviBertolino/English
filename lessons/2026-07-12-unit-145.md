### 2. 🧠 Mini-context

> **CEO:** Where are we on the AI investment plan?
> **You:** We've **carried out** the first analysis. But I'd like to **put off**
> the final decision until we've **worked out** the real cost.
> **CEO:** Fine. Can you **set up** a session with the CFO this week?
> **You:** Sure — I'll **bring it up** on Thursday and we'll **go over** the
> numbers together.

Notice how natural this sounds. A non-native speaker often says *"conduct",
"postpone", "calculate", "arrange", "raise", "review"* — all correct, but the
room feels more like a native meeting when you use the phrasal verbs above.

---

### 3. 📐 A regra (form)

A **phrasal verb** = verb + particle (a small word like *up, off, out, over,
on*). The particle often changes the meaning: *put* ≠ *put off* ≠ *put up
with*.

Many phrasal verbs take an object. Two word-order rules matter:

| Type | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Separable** (most transitive ones) | object can go **after** the particle OR **between** verb and particle | *set up a meeting* / *set a meeting up* |
| **Separable + pronoun** | a pronoun (it/them/him) **must** go in the middle | ✅ *set it up* — ❌ *set up it* |
| **Inseparable** | object always goes **after** the particle | *go over the numbers* (not *go the numbers over*) |

Rule of thumb: **if it's a pronoun, it goes in the middle.** *Put it off.
Call them off. Bring it up.*

---

### 4. 🎯 Quando usar — e quando não

**Why do phrasal verbs exist?** English has two vocabularies: short
Anglo-Saxon words (*put off, set up, carry out*) and longer Latin-based words
(*postpone, establish, conduct*). The Latin ones sound **formal and written**;
the phrasal ones sound **natural and spoken**. They carry the same meaning but
a different *register* (level of formality).

**Decision rule vs the rival (phrasal verb vs formal single verb):**

| Situation | Choose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Live meeting, casual talk, sounding native | **phrasal verb** | "Let's **put off** the review." |
| Board memo, formal email, written report | **formal single verb** | "The committee will **postpone** the review." |

Look back at the warm-up: sentence 1 (*put off*) is the meeting; sentence 2
(*postpone*) is the memo. **Neither is wrong — the skill is choosing.**

Common pairs for your world:

| Phrasal (spoken) | Formal (written) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| put off | postpone | delay |
| set up | establish / arrange | create, organize |
| carry out | conduct / perform | do (a plan, study) |
| call off | cancel | stop something planned |
| bring up | raise / mention | introduce a topic |
| go over | review / examine | check in detail |
| work out | calculate / resolve | figure out |

**PT→EN mapping:** Portuguese uses **one verb** where English gives you a
choice: *adiar* → put off / postpone; *cancelar* → call off / cancel;
*estabelecer* → set up / establish. Because the formal English word looks like
the Portuguese one (*postpone ≈ adiar, establish ≈ estabelecer, conduct ≈
conduzir*), Brazilians instinctively reach for the **Latin word** — so their
English is correct but sounds **stiff and bookish** in a live meeting. Learning
the phrasal versions is what makes you sound fluent, not just accurate.

**Top Brazilian-speaker errors:**
- ❌ *We will postpone it. We will conduct the analysis. Please, cancel the
  meeting.* (in a casual meeting) → ✅ *Let's put it off. We'll carry out the
  analysis. Can you call the meeting off?* — *correct English, wrong register:
  too formal for the room.*
- ❌ *I'll set up it tomorrow.* → ✅ *I'll set it up tomorrow.* — *a pronoun
  must sit between the verb and the particle.*
- ❌ *Let's go the numbers over.* → ✅ *Let's go over the numbers.* — *"go over"
  is inseparable; the object stays after the particle.*

### 5. ✏️ 8 annotated examples

1. **We've carried out** the first market analysis. — *"carry out" = conduct a
   plan/study; natural in a live update to the CEO.*
2. Let's **put** the AI budget decision **off** until we have real numbers. —
   *separable phrasal verb split around the object; "put off" = delay without
   sounding bureaucratic.*
3. Can you **set up** a working session with the CFO? — *"set up" = arrange;
   the everyday verb executives actually use for organizing a meeting.*
4. The board **called off** the acquisition talks last week. — *"call off" =
   cancel something already planned; more idiomatic than "cancel" in speech.*
5. I'll **bring it up** at Thursday's steering committee. — *pronoun "it" sits
   in the middle — "bring up it" would be wrong.*
6. Before we present, let's **go over** the investment slides one more time. —
   *"go over" = review carefully; inseparable, so the object follows.*
7. Can you **work out** the ROI for the two AI vendors? — *"work out" =
   calculate/figure out; warmer and more collaborative than "calculate".*
8. The kids **grew up** watching us work from home. — *everyday life; "grow
   up" is inseparable and intransitive here — no object at all.*

### 8. 🗣️ Para levar para a aula

- Ask your tutor to **role-play a rapid CEO check-in**. Answer every update
  using a phrasal verb instead of its formal twin: *carry out, put off, set up,
  work out, go over*. The goal is to sound like the meeting, not the memo.
- Then do the **register flip**: take three of your sentences and rewrite them
  as a **formal board memo** (*postpone, establish, conduct, cancel*). Feeling
  both versions of the same idea is how you learn to switch on demand.

### 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra e quando usar.** Phrasal verbs são verbo + partícula (*put off, set
up, carry out*). Eles significam o mesmo que os verbos formais de origem latina
(*postpone, establish, conduct*), mas soam **naturais e falados**. Regra
prática: em **reunião ao vivo**, use o phrasal verb; em **memo/email formal
para o board**, use o verbo latino. Os dois estão certos — a habilidade é
**escolher o registro**. Em ordem de palavras: com objeto normal você pode
separar (*set the meeting up*), mas com **pronome o objeto vai no meio**
obrigatoriamente (*set it up*, nunca *set up it*).

**As armadilhas.** Como o português usa **um só verbo** (*adiar, cancelar,
estabelecer*) e o equivalente formal em inglês parece o português
(*postpone ≈ adiar*), o brasileiro puxa sempre pelo verbo latino — fica
correto, mas **duro e livresco** na conversa. Aprender os phrasal verbs é o que
faz você soar fluente. Cuidado também com a posição do pronome (*set it up*,
não *set up it*) e com os phrasal verbs **inseparáveis** (*go over the
numbers*, nunca *go the numbers over*).
