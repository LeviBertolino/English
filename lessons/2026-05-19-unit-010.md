## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Interviewer:** Tell me about your last role.
> **Levi:** I was the engineering lead at a fintech startup — the team was small, only six engineers, but the systems were complex. The main challenge was that we weren't on a unified observability stack yet.
> **Interviewer:** Was the migration your initiative?
> **Levi:** It was. The previous setup wasn't scalable. My first three months were mostly discovery — where were the real bottlenecks?
> **Interviewer:** Were you the only person driving it?
> **Levi:** No, I wasn't alone. There were two senior engineers who owned the implementation.

Notice *I was*, *the team was*, *the systems were*, *we weren't*, *was the migration*, *wasn't scalable*, *were you*, *I wasn't*, *there were* — this is the **past simple of "be"**, the workhorse for describing the past.

### The rule

**"Was"** and **"were"** are the past forms of *am/is/are*. Use them to describe states, conditions, or identities in the past.

| Subject | Present | Past |
|---|---|---|
| I | am | **was** |
| he / she / it | is | **was** |
| we / you / they | are | **were** |

**Questions:** Invert subject and verb — *Was it* a bug? *Were they* on call?

**Negatives:** Add *not* — *wasn't* (= was not) / *weren't* (= were not).

**Short answers:** *Yes, I was. / No, I wasn't. / Yes, they were. / No, it wasn't.*

**"There was / there were"**: use *there was* for singular, *there were* for plural.
- *There was a production incident last night.*
- *There were three open PRs blocking the release.*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **The initial architecture was a monolith** — the team wasn't ready for microservices yet.
2. **Were the deployment pipelines set up before you joined**, or did you build them from scratch?
3. **The retrospective was productive**: there were clear action items for every team member.
4. **I wasn't the on-call engineer last weekend** — it was the platform team's rotation.
5. **The AI model wasn't accurate enough** in the first benchmark; we were still tuning the prompts.
6. **There were two critical bugs** in the release candidate — the QA cycle was longer than expected.
7. **She was the principal engineer on the migration** project; the results were impressive.
8. **I was so tired after the sprint demo** — it was a long three weeks, but the outcome was worth it.

---

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"The deploy were successful"** → ✅ **"The deploy was successful"**
*Why: singular subjects (the deploy, the model, the team, the bug) use "was", not "were".*

❌ **"Was you on call last night?"** → ✅ **"Were you on call last night?"**
*Why: "you" always takes "were" — even if you're talking to one person. "Was you" is non-standard in all contexts.*

❌ **"There was a lot of issues in the backlog"** → ✅ **"There were a lot of issues in the backlog"**
*Why: "there was/were" agrees with the noun that follows — "issues" is plural, so it's "there were". This is the most common mistake, even among fluent speakers.*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of **was/were** as a vowel pattern you already know:
- **I / he / she / it → WAS** — these are all *singular* subjects. One thing. One word: *was*.
- **We / you / they → WERE** — these are *plural-feeling* subjects (even "you" for one person follows the plural pattern).

The shortcut: *"I was, you were, we were, they were — everything else is was."*

And for *there was/were*: listen to what comes after. *"There was a bug"* (one bug). *"There were bugs"* (multiple). The noun decides.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and phrases

| Word/Phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| was | /wɒz/ (strong) / /wəz/ (weak) | Weak form /wəz/ is the default in connected speech ⚠️ |
| were | /wɜːr/ (strong) / /wər/ (weak) | Reduces to /wər/ mid-sentence ⚠️ |
| wasn't | /ˈwɒzənt/ | Stress on first syllable: WOZ-nt ⚠️ |
| weren't | /ˈwɜːrənt/ | One syllable blend: WER-nt ⚠️ |
| there was | /ðər wəz/ | Both words reduce — "ther-wəz" ⚠️ |
| there were | /ðər wər/ | Very fast in natural speech: "ther-wər" ⚠️ |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **The /ɒ/ in "was" (strong form)**: This is a short, rounded *o* — not the Brazilian *a* in "falar" and not the long *o* in "ônibus." Your mouth should be more open and rounded than in Portuguese. Think of the British English word *hot*: /hɒt/. ⚠️
- **The /ɜː/ in "were" (strong form)**: This sound doesn't exist in Portuguese. It's the same vowel as in *her*, *bird*, *work* — a mid-central vowel with no lip rounding. Brazilians often replace it with /ɛ/ ("weh") or /ɔ/ ("woh"). Try rounding the lips slightly and keeping the tongue in the middle of the mouth. ⚠️
- **Weak forms are natural**: In connected speech, both "was" and "were" reduce heavily. *"He was late"* → /hi wəz leɪt/ (almost "hiwəz-late"). You don't need to produce the strong forms unless you're adding emphasis (*"Yes, it WAS a good decision."*). ⚠️
- **"Wasn't"**: Two syllables, stress on first — *WOZ-nt*. Don't add a vowel at the end: not *"WOZ-en-tch"*. The *nt* cluster is final; close the *n* and stop. ⚠️

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**"Was"** e **"were"** são o passado simples de "be" (*am/is/are*). A regra de concordância é simples: *I/he/she/it* → **was**; *we/you/they* → **were**. O erro mais comum é usar "were" com sujeito singular (*"the deploy were"* ❌) ou "was" com "you" (*"was you"* ❌ — sempre *were you*). Atenção especial ao **there was/were**: o verbo concorda com o substantivo que vem depois — *there was a bug* (singular) vs. *there were bugs* (plural). Essa distinção é frequentemente ignorada até por falantes fluentes.

Na pronúncia, tanto *was* quanto *were* têm **formas fracas** na fala conectada: *was* vira /wəz/ e *were* vira /wər/ — soam quase sem vogal real. Só ficam fortes quando há ênfase (*"It WAS a great decision"*). O som /ɜː/ de *"were"* não existe em português — preste atenção nas frases do Google Translate para calibrar esse som. *Wasn't* e *weren't* são as formas negativas contraídas; lembre-se de não adicionar vogal extra no final: *WOZ-nt*, *WER-nt*.
