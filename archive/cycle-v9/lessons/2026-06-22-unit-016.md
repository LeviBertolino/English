## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — #platform-eng, Monday morning:**
>
> **Sarah:** Has anyone looked at that memory alert from Sunday night?
>
> **Levi:** Yes — I've already investigated it. I've identified the root cause: it's in the connection pool manager. I haven't pushed a fix yet, but I've opened a ticket and tagged the on-call.
>
> **Sarah:** Great. Has Marcus seen the ticket?
>
> **Levi:** I don't know — I've just tagged him. Give him five minutes.

Look at what's happening: *"I've investigated"*, *"I've identified"*, *"I haven't pushed"*, *"I've opened"*, *"I've just tagged"* — all actions happened in the past, but nobody asked *when* exactly. What matters is the **current state**: the investigation is done, the root cause is known, the fix isn't out yet, the ticket exists, Marcus has been notified.

That's the present perfect: **past action + present relevance**.

### The rule

The **present perfect** (have/has + past participle) connects a past action to **right now**. Use it when:

1. **The result matters now** — the exact time doesn't.
   - *"I've reviewed your PR."* → It's reviewed. You can merge.
   - *"The team has deployed the fix."* → It's live. The incident is over.

2. **Something just happened** and is immediately relevant.
   - *"I've just finished the architecture diagram."*
   - *"She has already sent the email."*

3. **Something hasn't happened** and the absence matters now.
   - *"We haven't merged the feature yet."*
   - *"The model hasn't been retrained this sprint."*

**Structure:**

| | Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| I / you / we / they | I **have done** / I**'ve done** | I **haven't done** | **Have** I **done**? |
| he / she / it | She **has done** / She**'s done** | She **hasn't done** | **Has** she **done**? |

**Past participle forms:**
- Regular: deploy → **deployed** · review → **reviewed** · finish → **finished**
- Irregular: write → **written** · send → **sent** · make → **made** · find → **found** · go → **gone**

> ⚠️ **Key insight for Portuguese speakers:** The English present perfect is **not** the same as *pretérito perfeito composto*. In English, *"I have done"* always signals **present relevance** — the result matters NOW. In Portuguese, *"tenho feito"* often signals repetition over time. Don't translate one-to-one.

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"I've reviewed all the open pull requests — three are ready to merge."** — result relevant now: action can follow (engineering)
2. **"The team has deployed the new recommendation engine to production. Early metrics look promising."** — completed action, current state matters (AI / engineering)
3. **"Have you seen the RFC I posted? The architecture committee has already commented on two sections."** — question about recent event + confirmation of current state (tech leadership)
4. **"We haven't migrated the legacy auth service yet — that's still on the Q3 roadmap."** — absence of completion is the current situation (engineering planning)
5. **"She has promoted two engineers to Staff level this quarter — a strong signal for the team's growth trajectory."** — past action with present impact (engineering leadership)
6. **"I've just pushed a hotfix to the staging branch. Can someone review it urgently?"** — very recent action triggering an immediate request (tech)
7. **"I've visited three countries this year — Brazil, Portugal, and Canada."** — experience up to now, time not the focus (everyday life)
8. **"Have you eaten? The team has already ordered from the Thai place."** — current situation depends on the answer (everyday life)

## 4. 🛠️ Practice — recognition + production

### Recognition — gap-fill

Fill in each blank with the correct present perfect form of the verb in parentheses:

*"The engineering manager \_\_\_ (schedule) a post-mortem for Thursday. I \_\_\_ (already / send) the incident report to all stakeholders, but I \_\_\_ (not / include) the root cause analysis yet. \_\_\_ (you / review) the timeline I drafted?"*

✅ **Answer:**

*"The engineering manager **has scheduled** a post-mortem for Thursday. I **have already sent** the incident report to all stakeholders, but I **haven't included** the root cause analysis yet. **Have you reviewed** the timeline I drafted?"*

**Why:** Every action is past, but every result is relevant to the present situation — the post-mortem is Thursday, the report is already out there, the RCA is still missing, and the timeline needs feedback now. Past action, present headline.

### Production task

*Write 2 sentences about something in your current engineering work or team: one using "I've / we've already…" and one using "I / we haven't … yet."*

**Model answer:**
1. *"I've already defined the acceptance criteria for the next sprint — the team can start estimating stories."*
2. *"We haven't aligned on the observability tooling yet — that conversation is still open for the architecture review."*

## 5. 💡 Memory Tip

Think of the present perfect as a **news headline**:

> *"Team has deployed new AI model."*
> *"CTO has announced quarterly roadmap."*

Headlines never say *exactly when* — they say *what has happened and why it matters now*. That's the present perfect: **past action, present headline**.

And never drop the helper: *"I done it"* ❌ → *"I **have** done it"* ✅. The *have/has* is the signal that the past connects to now.

## 6. 🚀 Daily Production Challenge (self-directed)

The next time you write a **Slack status update, a sprint review note, a PR description, or a team email**, use the present perfect at least twice:

> *"I've finished reviewing the auth service proposal. I've left comments on three sections. The team hasn't aligned on the caching strategy yet — that needs a quick sync."*

The present perfect is the natural tense for **status updates** in professional English. Using it correctly — especially *"we haven't… yet"* and *"I've already…"* — immediately makes your written English sound more fluent and precise in leadership contexts.

## 8. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

O **present perfect** é formado com **have/has + particípio passado**: *I have done, she has sent, we haven't deployed*. A ideia central é conectar uma ação passada ao **momento presente** — o que importa é o **resultado ou a relevância agora**, não o momento exato em que a ação aconteceu. Use-o quando quiser dizer que algo já foi feito (e isso importa agora), que algo acabou de acontecer, ou que algo ainda não aconteceu: *"I've reviewed the PR"* (está revisado, pode agir), *"We haven't aligned yet"* (ainda está em aberto), *"Has she sent the report?"* (preciso saber agora).

**Atenção para falantes de português:** o present perfect em inglês **não equivale** ao *pretérito perfeito composto*. Em inglês, *"I have done"* sempre implica relevância presente — não é usado para expressar repetição habitual ao longo do tempo como *"tenho feito"* em português. O erro mais comum é usar o simple past quando o resultado é o foco: em vez de *"I sent the proposal"* (correto quando o tempo importa — *"I sent it yesterday"*), use *"I've sent the proposal"* quando o que importa é o fato de ele ter sido enviado, e o tempo é secundário. Se você está descrevendo o estado atual das coisas em inglês — o present perfect é quase sempre a escolha certa.
