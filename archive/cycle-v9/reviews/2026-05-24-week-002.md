## 1. Recap — What You Covered This Week

| Unit | Title | Core rule |
|---|---|---|
| 007 | Do you...? | Form questions with *do/does* + base verb. *Does* signals 3rd-person singular — the main verb never adds -s in questions. |
| 009 | Have / I've got | Both express possession. *Have got* = informal/spoken/British; *have* with do/does = formal/written/American. Never mix: ❌ *Do you have got...?* |
| 010 | Was/were | Past of "be": *was* for I/he/she/it; *were* for you/we/they. *There was/were* agrees with the noun that follows. |
| 011 | Didn't / Did you...? | Past simple negatives and questions for all verbs except "be". *Did* absorbs the past — the main verb returns to base form. |
| 012 | I was doing | Past continuous = *was/were + -ing*. Action in progress at a specific past moment. |
| 013 | Was doing and Did | Past continuous = ongoing background; past simple = completed or interrupting event. |
| 014 | When vs. While | *While* = two simultaneous ongoing actions (both past continuous). *When* = interruption (past continuous + past simple) OR sequence (past simple + past simple). |

## 2. 8 Mixed Exercises

### Exercise 1 — Gap-fill: Present Tenses (Units 007 + 009)

Fill in the blanks. Choose from: *do*, *does*, *don't*, *doesn't*, *have*, *has*, *have got*, *has got*, *haven't got*.

> *"___ your team ___ (use) a shared observability stack, or ___ each service ___ its own dashboards? Our platform team ___ a Grafana instance everyone can access, but honestly I ___ (not have) time to configure all the panels for my services yet. ___ you ___ a template I could start from?"*

✅ **Answers:**
*Does / use / does / has / have / don't have / Do / have* (or: *Does / use / does / has got / haven't got / Have / got*)

### Exercise 2 — Error Correction (Units 007, 009, 010)

Find and fix **one error** in each sentence. Write the corrected version and name the rule.

1. *Does the CI pipeline runs integration tests before every merge?*
2. *Do you have got a CODEOWNERS file in that repo?*
3. *There was a lot of open PRs blocking the release last Friday.*

✅ **Answers:**
1. *Does the CI pipeline **run** integration tests...?* — After *does*, the main verb must be the base form; never add -s.
2. *Do you **have** a CODEOWNERS file...?* (or: *Have you **got** a CODEOWNERS file...?*) — Never combine the two systems in one question.
3. *There **were** a lot of open PRs...* — *there was/were* agrees with the noun that follows; *PRs* is plural → *were*.

### Exercise 5 — Choose the Correct Form (Units 013 + 014)

Choose past simple or past continuous for each blank. Then choose *when* or *while*.

> *"I ___ (write/was writing) the incident report ___ (when/while) my manager ___ (called/was calling) me. We ___ (talked/were talking) for 20 minutes ___ (when/while) the on-call engineer ___ (investigated/was investigating) the root cause. ___ (When/While) the call ended, I ___ (opened/was opening) the ticket and ___ (tagged/was tagging) the right team."*

✅ **Answers:** *was writing / when / called / talked (or: were talking) / while / was investigating / When / opened / tagged*

- *was writing* → in progress, interrupted by the call.
- *called* → short, complete interrupting event.
- *while ... was investigating* → two simultaneous ongoing actions.
- *When the call ended* → the call finished; then the next step (sequence).
- *opened / tagged* → sequential completed actions.

### Exercise 6 — When/While from Lesson Context (Unit 014)

*This passage comes directly from Unit 014's practice exercise.*

> *"___ (While / When) the data pipeline ___ (processed / was processing) the batch, someone ___ (noticed / was noticing) a spike in memory usage and ___ (stopped / was stopping) the job. ___ (While / When) we investigated the root cause, it ___ (turned out / was turning out) to be a misconfigured buffer size."*

✅ **Answer:**
*While the data pipeline **was processing** the batch, someone **noticed** a spike and **stopped** the job. When we investigated the root cause, it **turned out** to be a misconfigured buffer size.*

- *While ... was processing* → ongoing background.
- *noticed / stopped* → sudden completed events inside the background.
- *When we investigated ... it turned out* → sequence: investigation, then result.

### Exercise 7 — Sentence Transformation (All Units)

Rewrite each sentence as indicated without changing the meaning.

1. *The service doesn't have autoscaling.* → Rewrite using *have got*.
2. *"Was the migration your initiative?"* → Rewrite as a past simple question using *did*.
3. *I was in a retro when the P0 alert fired.* → Keep the meaning but swap the clauses (start with *When...*).

✅ **Answers:**
1. *The service **hasn't got** autoscaling.*
2. *Did you initiate the migration?* (Note: "was the migration your initiative" uses "be" — technically you can't use "did" there; accept variations like *"Did you drive/start the migration?"*)
3. *When the P0 alert fired, I was in a retro.*

### Exercise 8 — Mixed Bag: Find the Error (All Units)

Four sentences, four errors. Identify and correct each one.

1. *Did the incident report was filed before the post-mortem?*
2. *While the load balancer detected the spike, it rerouted traffic.*
3. *She didn't attended the sprint demo — she was in a skip-level.*
4. *I were working on the RFC when the Slack notification came in.*

✅ **Answers:**
1. *Was the incident report filed before the post-mortem?* — "Be" has its own past question form; never use *did* + *was*.
2. *When the load balancer detected the spike, it rerouted traffic.* — Detecting and rerouting are sequential completed actions (sequence), not simultaneous; use *when*, not *while*.
3. *She didn't **attend** the sprint demo.* — After *didn't*, the verb must be the base form.
4. *I **was** working on the RFC...* — *I* always takes *was*, never *were*.

## 3. Integrative Writing Task (self-directed)

Write a short paragraph of **5–7 sentences** about a recent project, incident, or team situation at work — in English. Use **at least 3 of the 7 structures** from this week.

Aim to use naturally:
- A present simple question form (Unit 007) or possession form (Unit 009)
- At least one *was/were* or *was/were + -ing*
- At least one *didn't* or *did...?*
- Ideally one *when/while* contrast

**Model paragraph for self-comparison:**

> *Our team has been running an observability initiative for the past quarter. Do we have perfect coverage? Not yet. We didn't instrument every service when we started — we prioritised the ones with the highest traffic first. While we were mapping the service dependencies, an incident happened: the metrics pipeline was processing a large batch when a misconfigured scrape interval caused a memory spike. I was writing the runbook at the time when my on-call engineer pinged me. We investigated fast, found the root cause, and deployed a fix the same afternoon. The team was already exhausted, but the result was worth it.*

Structures used: *has been running* (possession feel), *Do we have* (Unit 007), *didn't instrument... when we started* (Unit 011), *while we were mapping* (Unit 014), *was processing ... when ... caused* (Units 012/013), *was writing ... when ... pinged* (Unit 014), *was ... was* (Unit 010).

## 4. Pronunciation Drill — The 5 Trickiest Sounds from This Week

These sounds appeared across all 6 lesson files. They are the most common stumbling blocks for Brazilian Portuguese speakers at this stage. Revisit each one.

---

### 🔊 Sound 1 — "do you" /də jə/ reduction (Unit 007)

In casual and natural English, *"do you"* collapses from /duː juː/ to /də jə/ — almost *"d'ya"*.

Brazilians tend to over-articulate: *"DOO YOO work with Kubernetes?"* sounds formal and stilted. Native speech: *"d'ya work with Kubernetes?"*

Practice: say each phrase twice — once full, once reduced.
> *Do you use Terraform?* → *D'ya use Terraform?*
> *Do you have access to prod?* → *D'ya have access to prod?*

---

### 🔊 Sound 2 — "didn't" final cluster /dɪdənt/ (Unit 011)

The word ends on /nt/ — no trailing vowel. Common Brazilian error: *"DID-en-tchi"* (with a final /i/ or affricate).

Practice: close your mouth after the /t/. The sequence is: *D-I-D-nt* → stop.
> *I didn't deploy on Friday.*
> *She didn't attend the retro.*
> *We didn't catch the regression.*

---

### 🔊 Sound 3 — "I've got" contraction /aɪv ɡɒt/ (Unit 009)

*"I've"* and *"got"* should run together — nearly one unit. Brazilians often pause between them: *"I've ... got"* sounds choppy.

Practice: run them together as *"aɪvgɒt"* with no gap.
> *I've got a question about the architecture.*
> *I've got two incidents open this week.*
> *She's got the runbooks updated already.*

---

### 🔊 Sound 4 — Weak "was" /wəz/ and weak "were" /wə/ (Units 010, 012)

In connected speech, both auxiliaries almost disappear:
- *"was"* → /wəz/ (like a quick *"wuz"*)
- *"were"* → /wə/ (like a quick *"wuh"*)

Strong forms (/wɒz/, /wɜːr/) are only used for emphasis.

Practice: say these at natural speed — let the auxiliaries be background noise:
> *The team was great under pressure.* → /ðə tiːm wəz ɡreɪt/
> *We were already debugging.* → /wɪ wə ɔːlˈrɛdi/
> *I was reviewing the PR when it happened.* → /aɪ wəz rɪˈvjuːɪŋ/

---

### 🔊 Sound 5 — "-ing" suffix /ɪŋ/ — no trailing vowel (Unit 012)

The -ing ending in English is a nasal stop at the back of the throat: /ɪŋ/. Brazilians frequently append a vowel: *"working-i"*, *"deploy-ing-gee"*.

Practice: close your mouth at the /ŋ/ — the sound stops there.
> *I was working on the incident.* → /ˈwɜːkɪŋ/ — stops at /ŋ/
> *We were monitoring the dashboards.* → /ˈmɒnɪtərɪŋ/
> *The pipeline was processing the batch.* → /ˈprəʊsɛsɪŋ/

---

## 5. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

### O que deve estar sólido agora

Depois de 6 aulas (Units 007 e 009–014), estes padrões devem começar a sair naturalmente:

- **Perguntas no presente simples** com *do/does* + forma base — especialmente com "does" para terceira pessoa (*Does the CI run...? Does she lead...?*).
- **"Have" vs. "have got"** — você sabe que são equivalentes, e que "do you have got" não existe.
- **Was/were** no passado, incluindo o *there was/were* concordando com o substantivo seguinte.
- **Didn't + forma base** para negar no passado — o "did" já carrega o tempo, então o verbo relaxa.
- **Past continuous** (was/were + -ing) para ações que estavam em andamento.

### O que provavelmente ainda precisa de trabalho

- **A distinção was/were + -ing vs. passado simples** sob pressão — quando você fala rápido, é fácil usar o passado simples onde o contínuo caberia melhor (especialmente no padrão "estava fazendo X quando Y aconteceu").
- **"When" vs. "while"** — o *when* com dois empregos diferentes (interrupção vs. sequência) é o conceito mais sutil desta semana. Se ainda parece instável, releia a seção 2 da Unit 014.
- **Pronúncia** — os cinco sons do drill acima. Em especial: não adicionar vogal depois de *-ing* e não adicionar *"tchi"* depois de *didn't*.

### Arquivos para revisitar neste final de semana (se sobrar tempo)

- **Unit 013** (*I was doing and I did*) — contém o post-mortem de incidente mais completo e o contraste central da semana.
- **Unit 014** (*When vs. While*) — o exercício de gap-fill da seção 5 (reproduzido aqui como Exercício 6) vale refazer de cabeça sem olhar as respostas.
