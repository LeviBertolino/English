## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

> **Slack thread — incident war room, 2:47 am:**
>
> **Levi:** *"What exactly were you doing when the database cluster went down?"*
>
> **Ana:** *"I was reviewing the migration script when the first alert fired. The pipeline was still processing the last batch when the primary node crashed."*
>
> **Levi:** *"Did the on-call engineer catch it right away?"*
>
> **Ana:** *"He was sleeping, but his phone woke him up. While he was rolling back the migration, two more services went offline."*
>
> **Levi:** *"Got it. So the crash interrupted the batch run — it didn't finish cleanly."*

Notice the contrast: *"were you doing"*, *"was reviewing"*, *"was still processing"*, *"was sleeping"*, *"was rolling back"* — these describe **actions in progress** at a moment in the past. *"went down"*, *"fired"*, *"crashed"*, *"went offline"*, *"woke him up"* — these describe **events that happened** (and interrupted, or occurred alongside, those ongoing actions).

That contrast is today's entire rule.

### The rule: Past Continuous vs. Past Simple

**Past Continuous** = was/were + -ing
→ describes an action **in progress at a moment in the past** (background, ongoing activity).

**Past Simple** = verb in past form (worked, went, crashed)
→ describes a **completed event or action** (foreground, what happened).

### The classic contrast: when/while

| Structure | Meaning |
|---|---|
| I **was writing** the RFC **when** the call **came** in. | Background activity interrupted by an event. |
| **While** I **was debugging**, the deploy **finished**. | Two things: one ongoing, one completed. |
| The team **was reviewing** the PR **when** the server **went** down. | Same pattern. |

> 🔑 **"was/were + -ing" = the scene. Past simple = what happened in that scene.**

### Two actions in progress at the same time

When **both** actions are ongoing simultaneously, use *while* + continuous for both:

> *"While the CI was running, the QA team was writing test cases."*
> *"While I was presenting the roadmap, the engineers were already asking questions in the chat."*

### Just telling a story (no contrast needed)

When you're narrating a **sequence of completed events** — not emphasising background — use past simple throughout:

> *"The alert fired. The on-call engineer woke up. She rolled back the deployment. The cluster recovered."*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

1. **"I was writing the RFC when the CTO asked me to join an emergency call."** — past continuous interrupted by past simple, tech leadership
2. **"While the team was running the load tests, one of the microservices started throwing 503 errors."** — while + continuous, engineering
3. **"The Staff engineer was reviewing the PR when she noticed a critical SQL injection vulnerability."** — past continuous interrupted, code review
4. **"While the ML pipeline was training the model, the GPU cluster ran out of memory and crashed."** — two actions, one interruption, AI/engineering
5. **"We were discussing the system design when the product manager changed the requirements entirely."** — interrupted meeting, tech leadership
6. **"While I was committing the hotfix, the monitoring dashboard showed a second spike."** — while + continuous, DevOps
7. **"She was talking on the phone when the flight attendant asked her to switch it to airplane mode."** — everyday life
8. **"I was cooking dinner when my laptop sent me a notification about a failed deployment."** — everyday life blending with tech

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

❌ **"I was reviewing the PR when I was finding the bug."** → ✅ **"I was reviewing the PR when I found the bug."**
*Why: the interrupting event — the thing that happened — uses the past simple. Only the background/ongoing action uses the continuous. Finding the bug is a single completed moment, not an ongoing process.*

❌ **"While the pipeline ran, the server crashed."** → ✅ **"While the pipeline was running, the server crashed."**
*Why: the action that was in progress in the background needs to be in the past continuous. Past simple "ran" makes both actions sound like they started and finished at the same time — which changes the meaning. "While" almost always signals that one action is ongoing (continuous).*

❌ **"At 2 am, the engineer was sleeping and the alert fired and he was waking up."** → ✅ **"At 2 am, the engineer was sleeping when the alert fired and woke him up."**
*Why: don't chain everything in the continuous when describing a sequence of events. The continuous sets the scene (sleeping). The events that happened in sequence (fired, woke him up) are in the past simple.*

## 5. 🛠️ Practice

### Recognition — gap-fill

Choose the correct form (past continuous or past simple) for each blank:

> *"The team \_\_\_ (do) a live production deploy when the database \_\_\_ (start) rejecting connections. While the on-call engineer \_\_\_ (investigate) the logs, someone \_\_\_ (accidentally / drop) the wrong table in staging. Fortunately, the migration script \_\_\_ (not / touch) production."*

✅ **Answer:**
1. **was doing** — ongoing background action (the deploy was in progress)
2. **started** — interrupting event (the rejection happened at a moment)
3. **was investigating** — action in progress while another event occurred
4. **dropped** — completed event that happened at a specific moment
5. **didn't touch** — past simple negative; a single fact about what didn't happen

### Production — write + speak

Write 2 sentences about an engineering incident, sprint, or code review scenario using today's contrast:

- One sentence using **while…was…ing…past simple**.
- One sentence using **was…ing when…past simple**.

Say them out loud, focusing on the /wəz/ and /wɜːr/ sounds (see Section 7).

**Model answer:**
- *"While the deployment pipeline was running, a config error caused the staging environment to go offline."*
- *"I was writing a design doc when my teammate flagged a critical edge case I had completely missed."*

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The Movie Scene trick:**

> Imagine the past continuous as the **camera panning across a scene**: background music playing, characters moving slowly — the situation is set.
> Then the past simple is a **sharp cut**: something happens, the scene changes.
>
> *"The team **was debugging** (slow pan, tense atmosphere)… **when** the server **went** down (sharp cut, alarm sounds)."*
>
> Continuous = the camera. Simple = the cut.

Whenever you write about the past and one action was the "background atmosphere" and another was a "sudden event," use continuous for the atmosphere and simple for the event.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA

| Word/phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| was | /wəz/ (unstressed) | In natural speech, "was" is almost always /wəz/ — a schwa, not /wɒz/ ⚠️ |
| were | /wər/ (unstressed) | Same — unstressed /wər/, not /wɜːr/ ⚠️ |
| was running | /wəz ˈrʌnɪŋ/ | The -ing ending: /ɪŋ/, not "inge" or "ing-gi" ⚠️ |
| were debugging | /wər dɪˈbʌɡɪŋ/ | Stress on *bug*, not on *de* |
| when | /wɛn/ | Short vowel; common in fast speech to reduce to /wən/ |
| while | /waɪl/ | Diphthong /aɪ/ — longer than "will" |
| crashed | /kræʃt/ | Final cluster /ʃt/ — don't add a vowel ⚠️ |
| reviewing | /rɪˈvjuːɪŋ/ | 3 syllables: re-VIEW-ing |

### Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

- **"was" is /wəz/, not /wɑz/** ⚠️: Brazilian learners often over-pronounce *was* as /wɑz/ (full vowel, like in "wash"). In unstressed position — which is most of the time — *was* is a schwa: **/wəz/**. Say *"I wəz writing"*, not *"I WAZ writing"*.
- **"were" is /wər/** ⚠️: Similarly, *were* is often unstressed: **/wər/**, rhyming with "her". Don't say /wɛr/ (like "where") in mid-sentence. *"They wər debugging"*.
- **The -ing ending** ⚠️: In Portuguese, there's no /ɪŋ/ ending. Brazilians sometimes say "running-gi" or insert a vowel after the /ŋ/. Keep it tight: **/rʌnɪŋ/**, no extra vowel at the end.
- **Consonant clusters at the end** ⚠️: Words like *crashed* /kræʃt/, *finished* /ˈfɪnɪʃt/ end in two consonants. Don't add a vowel: not "crasht-i", just **/kræʃt/**.

### Pronunciation Practice — say each phrase out loud

1. *I was reviewing the RFC when the alert fired.* ⚠️ — /aɪ wəz rɪˈvjuːɪŋ… wɛn ðə əˈlɜːrt ˈfaɪərd/
2. *While the pipeline was running, the server crashed.* ⚠️ — /waɪl ðə ˈpaɪplaɪn wəz ˈrʌnɪŋ… kræʃt/
3. *The team was debugging when the deploy finished.* ⚠️ — /ðə tiːm wəz dɪˈbʌɡɪŋ wɛn ðə dɪˈplɔɪ ˈfɪnɪʃt/
4. *Were you writing tests while the CI was running?* ⚠️ — /wər juː ˈraɪtɪŋ… waɪl ðə siː-aɪ wəz ˈrʌnɪŋ/
5. *She was sleeping when her phone woke her up.* ⚠️ — /ʃiː wəz ˈsliːpɪŋ wɛn hər foʊn woʊk hər ʌp/

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

A diferença entre **past continuous** (was/were + -ing) e **past simple** é uma das mais importantes do inglês: ela define quem é o *pano de fundo* e quem é o *evento*. O **past continuous** descreve uma ação que **estava em progresso** num momento do passado — como a câmera abrindo a cena: *"I was reviewing the RFC"* (estava revisando, ação em andamento). O **past simple** descreve um **evento que aconteceu** — o corte: *"when the alert fired"* (quando o alerta disparou, evento pontual). A estrutura clássica é: *was/were + -ing + when + past simple* (para ação interrompida) ou *while + was/were + -ing + past simple* (para ação em paralelo que acontece ao mesmo tempo).

Dois erros comuns de brasileiros: usar o continuous para o evento interrompido (*"I was finding the bug"* ❌ → *"I found the bug"* ✅) e pronunciar *was* como /wɑz/ com vogal aberta e forte. Na fala natural, *was* é quase sempre /wəz/ (schwa), e *were* é /wər/ — sons reduzidos, sem ênfase. Pratique dizer *"wəz running"* e *"wər debugging"* com a vogal bem fraca e a atenção na sílaba tônica do verbo principal.
