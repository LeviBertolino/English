## 1. 📘 Unit 023 — be/been/being

**Grammar focus:** The three forms of passive "be" — when to use *be*, *been*, and *being* after different auxiliary verbs, and how to choose the right one every time.

## 2. 🧠 Adquirir — Mini-context first, rule second

### Read this first — notice the pattern:

> **#engineering Slack thread — weekly status update:**
>
> Hey team, here's where we stand:
>
> — The new caching layer **is being implemented** by the platform team. ETA Thursday.
> — Three critical bugs **have been fixed** since Monday's deployment.
> — The API rate limiter **should be deployed** by end of week.
> — After **being reviewed** by the security team, the OAuth changes **will be merged** this afternoon.
> — Legacy endpoints **need to be deprecated** before the sprint closes.
>
> Any blockers? Drop a reply here.

Notice: *is being implemented*, *have been fixed*, *should be deployed*, *being reviewed*, *will be merged*, *need to be deprecated* — all passive, all using "be" in a different form. The form of "be" changes depending on what comes **before** it.

---

### The Rule

Unit 021 taught you the base passive: **is done** (present) and **was done** (past).
This unit answers: *when do I use* **be**, **been**, or **being**?

The rule is simple — look at **what comes immediately before**:

| What comes before | Form of "be" | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Modal verb (*will, should, can, must, might, would*) | **be** | *"This must **be** documented."* |
| *have / has / had* | **been** | *"The PR has **been** reviewed."* |
| *is / are / was / were* | **being** | *"The feature is **being** tested."* |
| Preposition (*after, before, without, by*) | **being** | *"After **being** deployed, the service restarted."* |
| *need / going / seem / want + to* | **to be** | *"This needs **to be** fixed."* |

**One clean test:** Ask yourself — *"What is the word right before my passive 'be'?"*

- Modal → **be**: *"It should **be** refactored."*
- have/has/had → **been**: *"It has **been** refactored."*
- is/are/was/were → **being**: *"It is **being** refactored."*
- to/need/going → **to be**: *"It needs **to be** refactored."*

## 3. ✏️ 8 Example Sentences

**Tech & leadership (5):**

1. *The new monitoring dashboard is being built by the observability team.* — present passive continuous; work in progress right now.
2. *Three high-priority tickets have been resolved since the last standup.* — present perfect passive; result of recent work, still relevant.
3. *The entire architecture should be reviewed before we scale to new regions.* — modal passive; recommendation or obligation.
4. *After being approved by the principal engineer, the design doc was shared with the wider team.* — passive -ing after preposition; sequence of events.
5. *The deprecated API endpoints need to be removed before Q3 to avoid security risks.* — to-infinitive passive; a task that must be done.

**Everyday life (3):**

6. *The concert tickets will be sold online starting Friday.* — modal passive (will); future plan.
7. *The package has been delivered — I got a notification an hour ago.* — present perfect passive; recent completed action.
8. *The bridge is being repaired, so there's a detour this week.* — present passive continuous; ongoing process.

## 4. ⚠️ 3 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 — Using *been* after a modal instead of *be*:**
> ❌ The deployment should been reviewed by the architect.
> ✅ The deployment should **be** reviewed by the architect.
>
> *Why:* Modals (*should, must, will, can, might*) are followed by the base infinitive. The base form of "be" is just *be* — not *been*. *Been* only follows *have/has/had*.

**Mistake 2 — Using *be* after *have* instead of *been*:**
> ❌ The ticket has be assigned to the wrong team for two days.
> ✅ The ticket has **been** assigned to the wrong team for two days.
>
> *Why:* *Have/has/had* always require the **past participle** of the following verb. The past participle of *be* is *been*, not *be*.

**Mistake 3 — Skipping *being* and using the past participle directly after *is/are/was/were* for ongoing actions:**
> ❌ The new feature is tested right now by QA.
> ✅ The new feature **is being tested** right now by QA.
>
> *Why:* If the action is **ongoing at this moment** (continuous aspect), use *is/are + being + past participle*. *"Is tested"* is the present simple passive — it describes a general habit or fact, not something happening right now. Compare: *"All features are tested before release"* (general truth) vs. *"The feature is being tested right now"* (happening now).

## 6. 💡 Memory Tip

**The "what's before me?" trick:**

Think of passive "be" as a chameleon — it changes form to match what's sitting to its left:

> **Modal** (should/must/will/can) → bare base → **be**
> *"should **be** merged"*
>
> **Have/Has/Had** → past participle → **been**
> *"has **been** merged"*
>
> **Is/Are/Was/Were** → -ing → **being**
> *"is **being** merged"*
>
> **To/Need/Going** → to-infinitive → **to be**
> *"needs **to be** merged"*

All four mean passive — only the timing and the auxiliary change. Once you know what comes before, the form is automatic.

## 7. 🎤 Pronunciation Guide

### Key words and IPA transcriptions

| Word / phrase | IPA | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| being | /ˈbiːɪŋ/ | Two syllables: BEE-ing. The /ɪŋ/ ending. |
| been | /bɪn/ (UK) / /biːn/ (US) | Short /ɪ/ in British English; longer /iː/ in American. |
| should be | /ʃʊd biː/ | Both fully pronounced; *be* is often reduced in fast speech to /bi/ |
| has been | /hæz bɪn/ → /həz bɪn/ | *Has* reduces to /həz/ in fast speech — the /h/ may nearly disappear |
| is being | /ɪz ˈbiːɪŋ/ | *Is* may reduce to /ɪz/ — stress falls on *BE*-ing |
| need to be | /niːd tə biː/ | *To* reduces to /tə/; natural rhythm: NEED-tə-bee |
| have been | /hæv bɪn/ → /həv bɪn/ | *Have* reduces to /həv/ in fast speech |
| deprecated | /ˈdɛprɪkeɪtɪd/ | Four syllables: DEP-ri-KAY-tid |
| implemented | /ˈɪmplɪmentɪd/ | Four syllables: IM-pli-MEN-tid |
| completed | /kəmˈpliːtɪd/ | Three syllables: kəm-PLEE-tid — -ed = /ɪd/ after /t/ |

### ⚠️ Tips for Brazilian Portuguese speakers

**Sound 1 — "been" = /bɪn/ (not /biːn/ in British English)** ⚠️
The short /ɪ/ in *been* (British) is the same "relaxed i" from Unit 018 context. Brazilians tend to produce /biːn/ (long vowel). In fast speech, *"has been fixed"* becomes /həz bɪn fɪkst/ — both *has* and *been* reduce. Practice the whole chunk reduced: *"it's been done"* → /ɪts bɪn dʌn/.

**Sound 2 — Reductions in fast speech: *has been*, *have been*, *is being*** ⚠️
These chains compress dramatically in natural English:
- *"has been"* → /həz bɪn/ (the /h/ in *has* almost disappears, *been* stays short)
- *"is being"* → /ɪz ˈbiːɪŋ/ (both words reduced, stress on BEE)
- *"should be"* → /ʃʊd bi/ (*be* often unstressed and short)
Brazilians tend to give equal weight to every syllable. In these chains, the first word is weak; stress lives on *been/being*.

**Sound 3 — "being" ends in /ɪŋ/, not /iŋ/ or /ɪŋɡ/** ⚠️
The -ing ending is /ɪŋ/ — there is **no /ɡ/ sound** at the end. And the vowel is the short, relaxed /ɪ/, not the tight Portuguese /i/. ❌ *"BEE-eeng"* → ✅ *"BEE-ing"* /ˈbiːɪŋ/.

**Sound 4 — *deprecated* has four syllables** ⚠️
Tech vocabulary often trips up non-native speakers. *Deprecated* = /ˈdɛprɪkeɪtɪd/ — DEP-ri-KAY-tid. Brazilians sometimes stress the wrong syllable: ❌ *"dep-ruh-KAH-teh-did"* → ✅ *"DEP-ri-KAY-tid"*. The stress is on the first syllable.

**Sound 5 — Modal + be: no extra vowel after *be*** ⚠️
*"Should be reviewed"* = /ʃʊd biː rɪˈvjuːd/. Don't add a vowel after *be*: ❌ *"should be-ee reviewed"* → ✅ *"should be reviewed"*. *Be* ends cleanly — no trailing vowel sound.

## 10. 🇧🇷 Resumo em português

**A regra:**
Esta aula completa o que você aprendeu na Unidade 021 (*is done / was done*): agora você aprende **qual forma de "be" usar** dependendo do que vem antes dela na frase passiva. A regra é simples: depois de verbo modal (*should, must, will, can, might*), use **be**: *"This must be reviewed."* Depois de *have/has/had*, use **been**: *"The PR has been approved."* Depois de *is/are/was/were* (para ações em andamento), use **being**: *"The feature is being tested."* Depois de verbos como *need* ou *going to*, use **to be**: *"This needs to be fixed."* O que você precisa fazer é olhar para a palavra **imediatamente anterior** ao "be" e deixar ela guiar a forma.

**O que evitar:**
O erro mais comum é confundir *been* e *be* depois de verbos modais: ❌ *"should been done"* → ✅ *"should be done"*. Verbos modais sempre pedem o infinitivo base, e o infinitivo base de *be* é simplesmente *be*. O segundo erro frequente é usar *"is tested"* quando a ação está acontecendo agora: ❌ *"The feature is tested right now"* → ✅ *"The feature is being tested right now"* — para ações em andamento, você precisa do *being*. Em fala rápida, *"has been"* e *"is being"* ficam muito reduzidos — *has* vira /həz/, *been* fica curto /bɪn/, *is* vira /ɪz/ — e o acento recai sobre o *being*. Vale praticar essas cadeias em voz alta para soar natural.
